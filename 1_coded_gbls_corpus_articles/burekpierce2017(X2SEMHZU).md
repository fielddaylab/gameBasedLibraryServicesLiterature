## Summary
Burek Pierce's foundational text synthesizes research on adolescent development from psychology, neuroscience, education, health, and communication studies to inform library practice with teens. The second edition (2017) updates the first (2008) to reflect evolving research on adolescent brain development, contemporary media consumption, identity formation, and the role of libraries in supporting healthy teen development.

The book establishes that understanding adolescence requires moving beyond myth to evidence-based knowledge. Pierce directly addresses five myths about teens: (1) teenagers are essentially adults; (2) teens hate their parents; (3) adolescents lead unhealthy lives; (4) teens know technology best; and (5) we can understand teens through media marketed to them. Each section draws on empirical research to complicate stereotypes. For example, Pierce describes how neuropsychological research demonstrates that brain development continues into the mid-twenties, particularly in the prefrontal cortex associated with reasoned decision-making. This finding contradicts assumptions that older teens require no adult guidance. Similarly, research on parent-teen relationships reveals that most teens maintain supportive relationships with parents and view parents as preferred sources of information on sensitive topics—a finding that challenges library policies that assume all parent-child contact jeopardizes teen privacy.

Pierce organizes subsequent chapters thematically around identity and community, sexuality and gender, cognitive development, media literacy, and library work with teens. The author emphasizes that teen identity is both individual and social, shaped by race, ethnicity, gender, socioeconomic status, immigration, and disability. Libraries, she argues, must design spaces and services that recognize the complexity and multiplicity of teen identities rather than treating teens as a monolithic population. The book consistently privileges interdisciplinary knowledge, encouraging librarians to consult scholarship from psychology, sociology, communication, and health fields rather than relying on intuition or field experience alone.

The book's treatment of media consumption is particularly relevant to games-based library services. Pierce documents research on segmented programming and "bifurcated media worlds," showing that teens do not share a common culture or media preferences. This finding directly challenges marketing assumptions and media-driven collection development. Pierce presents data on teen social-media use showing variation by age, gender, and race, and notes that platform adoption does not necessarily indicate value or engagement. For GBLS, this suggests that game collections and programming should be informed by community assessment and individual patron needs rather than by industry trends or assumptions about what "teens want."

Throughout, Pierce emphasizes the importance of professionals maintaining awareness of research while remaining critical and contextual in its application. Her chapter on reading research provides guidance for evaluating different types of scholarly work—basic research, applied research, quantitative studies, qualitative studies, critical scholarship, and practitioner reflection—equipping librarians to assess claims with appropriate skepticism. This meta-cognitive approach models the kind of evidence evaluation that the baseline GBLS review also emphasizes.

The book's 2017 revision adds contemporary attention to student debt, social-justice movements, LGBTQ+ rights, mental-health awareness, and the proliferation of digital platforms. Pierce notes that while the fundamental understanding of adolescent development from the first edition remains sound, the contexts in which teens develop have shifted, requiring librarians to continually update their knowledge rather than relying on fixed conceptions of youth.

## Productive Incongruences and Challenges

This book is not specifically about games-based library services; it is a foundational text on adolescent development and youth library practice. As such, it does not directly address game formats, gaming programs, or game collections. However, its emphasis on evidence-based practice, interdisciplinary knowledge, community assessment, and the complexity of teen identity and media consumption provides essential conceptual grounding for games-based services. 

A key incongruence is that Burek Pierce's treatment of "video games" is limited and embedded within broader discussion of media consumption and teen culture rather than examined as a distinct service modality. The book acknowledges digital games as part of contemporary media landscapes but does not analyze their specific affordances, design qualities, or potential for learning or community-building. For a games-based library services review, this represents a gap: the book provides crucial context for understanding why teen assessment and skilled facilitation matter, but does not address how games specifically function within that ecological understanding.

Additionally, while the book repeatedly emphasizes that research findings are conditional rather than universal, and that librarians must consider local context and individual variation, it does not explicitly frame games as a service modality worthy of systematic research evaluation. The framing is more general: understand teen development, assess community needs, design services with teen input, and maintain awareness of evidence while remaining appropriately skeptical. This generalist stance is valuable but leaves open how games specifically should be studied, implemented, and evaluated in libraries.

## Suggested Review Contributions

**Target Section: Instruction, Orientation, and Information Literacy**

Burek Pierce's discussion of teen research skills and information-seeking behavior, combined with her treatment of segmented media worlds and critical media literacy, contributes to understanding how games might be designed for information-literacy instruction. Specifically, her finding that teens have documented weaknesses in evaluating online information despite technical facility suggests that games incorporating critical analysis, information verification, and decision-making under incomplete knowledge could address identified gaps. The book provides research grounding for why such games should focus on habits of thought rather than merely engagement.

**Target Section: Community, Belonging, Wellness, and the Public Commons**

Burek Pierce's emphasis on identity multiplicity and the research on parent-teen relationships, coupled with her discussion of the importance of community assessment and professional relationships with other youth-serving sectors (health, education, social work), contributes to the ecology of practices that support teen belonging. Her argument that librarians must understand teen development from interdisciplinary sources, and her documentation that most teens maintain supportive adult relationships, suggests that games designed for community-building, identity exploration, or peer collaboration should be embedded within broader youth-development frameworks rather than treated as isolated entertainment.

**Target Section: Professional Practice, Education, Training Gaps**

The book explicitly addresses librarians' knowledge gaps regarding contemporary adolescent development and recommends specific strategies for staying informed about research across disciplines. Burek Pierce models how librarians can read and critically evaluate scholarship outside library science, suggesting that professional education should include research literacy and intellectual humility. For games-based library services, this translates to a recommendation that librarian training should include not only game design and facilitation skills, but also foundational knowledge about how games function as media, how they are experienced by players of different demographics, and how their effects depend on facilitation and social context.
