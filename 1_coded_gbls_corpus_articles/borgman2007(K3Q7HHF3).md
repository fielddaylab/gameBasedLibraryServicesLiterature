## Summary
Christine Borgman's monograph examines how digital technologies are transforming scholarly communication and research infrastructure across academic disciplines. The book synthesizes scholarship from information science, computer science, communication studies, and disciplinary research practices to understand how scholars seek, use, create, and share information in digital environments.

Borgman argues that understanding scholarship in the digital age requires analyzing it as a sociotechnical system—attending to both technical infrastructure (networks, digital libraries, data repositories) and social practices (collaboration, publication norms, trust in sources). The book traces the continuity and discontinuity between print and digital scholarly communication, examining how digital technologies have disrupted traditional publishing models while preserving core scholarly functions (legitimization, dissemination, archiving).

Key themes include:
- **Information infrastructure:** The roles of digital libraries, networks, databases, and repositories in supporting scholarly work
- **Data as scholarship:** How scientific, social scientific, and humanistic disciplines handle data differently and why
- **Scholarly communication:** Forms and functions of academic publishing, including preprints, conferences, peer review, and open access
- **Disciplinary differences:** How information practices, artifacts, and needs vary across sciences, social sciences, and humanities
- **Governance and economics:** Business models, intellectual property, and institutional structures supporting scholarly publishing

The book is comprehensive and research-based, drawing on empirical studies of scholar behavior, case studies of infrastructure projects, and theoretical frameworks from information science. Borgman emphasizes that effective digital scholarship infrastructure must accommodate disciplinary variation rather than imposing universal systems.

## Productive Incongruences and Challenges

This book does not address games, play, libraries as service venues, or recreational uses of information. It is focused on scholarly research infrastructure and academic publishing rather than library public services or user engagement. Its inclusion in the GBLS corpus suggests it may provide foundational context about information infrastructure and institutional change, but it does not directly examine games-based services.

The book treats libraries primarily as custodians of scholarly records and information rather than as spaces for community engagement, learning, or play. This represents a different institutional model from the one that emphasizes GBLS as public service.
