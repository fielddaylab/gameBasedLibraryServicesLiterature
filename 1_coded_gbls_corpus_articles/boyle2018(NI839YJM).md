### Summary
# **Boyle, C. (2018). Play time: Why video games are essential to urban academic libraries. *Urban Library Journal, 24*(1), 1-11.**

Boyle argues that video games are not merely advantageous for academic libraries but essential for urban academic libraries. The article is a practitioner-facing argumentative review rather than an empirical study, but it makes a focused GBLS contribution by connecting video game collections and programs to the specific service conditions of urban campuses: commuter students, diverse users, digital access gaps, limited campus attachment, and the need to make the library visible as an intellectual commons.

The article begins by challenging stereotypes about gamers. Drawing on Entertainment Software Association and Pew data, Boyle notes that the average player age is around 35, that the gender distribution of players is much less male-dominated than common assumptions suggest, and that many U.S. households include regular game players. This demographic framing matters because Boyle uses it to argue that games are already culturally relevant to a large portion of the student population. Urban academic libraries, in this view, should not treat games as a fringe recreational interest detached from their users' lives.

Boyle grounds the argument in ACRL's standards for academic libraries, especially the ideas that libraries are intellectual commons where users interact with ideas across physical and virtual environments, and that libraries support discovery of information in all formats. Video games are therefore framed as both community media and information objects. They can support recreation, collaboration, research, information seeking, and contact with library staff and services.

The first major claim is that video games act as community builders. Boyle synthesizes prior work by Nicholson, Brown and Kasper, Adams, and Cross, Mould, and Smith to argue that game collections, game labs, game clubs, and game events draw patrons into shared library space and can strengthen social connections. This is especially important for urban academic libraries because commuter students may have fewer informal reasons to linger on campus or identify the library as a gathering place. Group play gives students a specific, low-stakes reason to enter the library, meet others, collaborate, and interact with library staff.

The second claim is that video games can increase awareness and use of library sources and services. Boyle argues that game events can work as gateways to other library activity when librarians display related materials, connect games to research topics, advertise gaming-related reference services, or use game conversations to introduce broader information literacy. Examples include linking a game such as *Skyrim* to resources on feudalism, Nordic history, or Norse mythology; offering strategy guides or gaming research sources; and using game programs as occasions to discuss searching, source use, or library services.

The article also treats video games as information sources in their own right. Boyle notes that games are increasingly used as primary sources in game studies and multidisciplinary research, and that games can prompt players to seek books, historical information, strategy materials, forums, mods, and other supplemental resources. This supports the GBLS claim that games in libraries are not only entertainment items. They can connect to academic inquiry, curricular support, media literacy, and cross-format discovery.

For the larger GBLS project, Boyle is useful because it adds the urban academic-library layer to the literature. The article asks why an academic library serving commuter students and diverse urban users might need video games, not only why libraries in general might experiment with games. The answer is service-oriented: games can make the library a more inviting campus space, reduce distance between students and staff, support collaboration, provide equitable access to game media and hardware, and introduce users to services they might otherwise use only when required by a course.

The limits are clear. Boyle does not present new data, evaluate a local program, describe a collection budget or policy, report usage statistics, or test outcomes. The article relies on existing literature and broad demographic arguments. It should therefore be cited as a conceptual and advocacy source rather than as evidence of measured impact. Its strongest role in the review is to clarify why urban academic libraries may have distinctive reasons to collect and program with video games.
