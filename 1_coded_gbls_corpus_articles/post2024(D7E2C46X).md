## Purpose & Scope

This 2024 study documents a critical systemic challenge facing academic libraries: the shift from physical to digital game distribution makes it nearly impossible for libraries to collect games using existing methods. Drawing on interviews with librarians at 13 academic libraries in the US beginning to explore digital game collection, Post, Reed, and Lanham identify key considerations, challenges, and barriers that will shape libraries' ability to continue supporting game-based programming and curricula.

The work is urgent: as major game publishers (Sony, Microsoft, Valve) transition to digital-only distribution, libraries face a fundamental access crisis. Unlike books and audiovisual materials where publishers have created licensing models for libraries, game publishers have not—and may resist—establishing comparable licensing arrangements.

## The Core Problem

**Right of First Sale vs. Licensing Model:**
- **Physical Media:** Libraries use "right of first sale" doctrine—libraries can circulate purchased physical media to patrons
- **Digital Games:** No right of first sale for digital downloads; libraries must negotiate licenses with copyright holders
- **Current Reality:** As of 2024, "game platform licenses are in the same place they were in 2009 with limited or no institutional licensing available for libraries"

**Industry Trend:**
- Steam (launched 2003) pioneered digital game distribution; industry rapidly following
- Sony and Microsoft now offer digital-only console editions (no disc drives)
- Indie game development heavily relies on digital distribution (platforms like Steam, Epic Games)
- Gaming community anticipates future will be "all digital"

**Why Digital-Only is Profitable for Publishers:**
- Eliminates intermediaries needed for physical production/distribution
- Eliminates secondary market for used games
- Maximizes publisher control and revenue

**Why This Destroys Library Collections:**
- Libraries cannot legally circulate digital games they don't own; must license from publishers
- No vendors/platforms currently cater to libraries in ways comparable to Ebook vendors
- Publishers have no incentive to license games to libraries—direct-to-consumer digital sales more profitable
- Without licensing models, libraries cannot provide access to digital-only releases

## Key Findings from Interviews (13 Academic Libraries)

### Librarian Perspectives & Priorities:
- Strong interest in building game collections
- Recognition that physical media collecting will become obsolete
- Uncertainty about how to approach digital game licensing
- Desire for vendor/platform solutions similar to Ebook licensing
- Need for guidance on copyright/licensing issues

### Current Workarounds (Partial/Inadequate Solutions):
1. **Continuing Physical Media:** Some libraries still building physical collections, but facing inventory challenges and limited availability
2. **Personal Patron Libraries:** Some libraries provide subscriptions to personal game libraries (e.g., Xbox Game Pass), but doesn't work for all platforms or games
3. **Emulation/Preservation:** Limited discussion of archival/preservation approaches to keeping older games playable
4. **Partnerships:** Some exploration of institutional partnerships to share licensing costs
5. **Advocacy:** Librarians recognizing need for collective advocacy to game publishers

### Unmet Needs:
- Standardized licensing models for libraries
- Vendor platforms comparable to Ebook vendors (Ebsco, Overdrive, etc.)
- Clear guidance on copyright and institutional licensing
- Affordable solutions for smaller libraries
- Support for preserving games as cultural artifacts

- **Systemic Infrastructure Problem:** Identifies collection development as dependent on institutional/industry structures—individual library action insufficient

## Policy & Industry Challenges

### Copyright/Licensing Issues:
- Traditional library models (right of first sale) don't apply to digital goods
- No established precedent for institutional digital game licensing
- Publishers can restrict access, impose geographic limitations, set expiration dates
- DMCA and anti-circumvention provisions limit options for preservation

### Economic Factors:
- Game publishers have no incentive to license to libraries (lower revenue than direct sales)
- Library budgets unlikely to compete with individual consumer spending
- Vendor development requires librarian demand AND publisher willingness to negotiate
- Cost structures uncertain for institutions of varying sizes

### Industry Coordination:
- Would require coordination among publishers, platforms, and libraries
- Past success (Ebooks) shows possible but difficult; took years to establish viable models
- Gaming industry less familiar with library models than publishing industry
- Indie game publishers typically have no institutional licensing experience

## Current State Assessment (2024)

**Status:** Academic libraries are at critical juncture
- Physical media collections becoming obsolete
- Digital licensing models not yet viable
- Libraries cannot support growing demand for game-based teaching/learning
- Without action, libraries will lose capability to provide game access

**Parallel with Past Transitions:**
- Similar crises occurred when libraries transitioned to Ebooks and digital video
- Solutions required librarian advocacy, vendor development, and publisher negotiation
- Took 10+ years to establish functional models

## Recommendations (Implicit in Article)

1. **Librarian Advocacy:** Libraries must collectively advocate to publishers for licensing models
2. **Vendor Development:** Publishers should recognize library market; develop vendor solutions
3. **Standards Development:** Library community needs to develop standards and best practices
4. **Copyright Reform:** May require copyright/licensing reform to enable library preservation and access
5. **Experimentation:** Libraries should experiment with available solutions while working toward better models

## Identified Limitations & Gaps

- **Interview-Based Only:** Does not provide quantitative data on extent of libraries' digital game collection efforts
- **Limited Solution Development:** Article identifies problems more than proposing solutions
- **Publisher Perspective Missing:** No interviews with game publishers to understand their hesitations/priorities
- **Preservation Questions:** Limited discussion of archival/preservation approaches for born-digital games
- **Equity Analysis:** Could more explicitly address how this impacts under-resourced libraries and communities
- **Timeline:** Unclear timeline for when viable licensing models might emerge
- **Emerging Solutions Not Addressed:** Does not examine potential solutions (e.g., open-source games, independent publisher partnerships)

## Urgency & Calls to Action

Post et al. frame this as urgent problem requiring action:
- Status quo unsustainable as industry continues shift to digital
- Individual library solutions insufficient
- Requires collective action: librarian advocacy, vendor development, possibly copyright reform
- Window is closing—need to establish models before digital games completely displace physical media

## Broader Implications

This article is critical for understanding structural barriers to GBLS at scale:
- **Service Ecology:** GBLS success depends not just on library effort but on institutional/industry structures
- **Conditional Benefits:** Game-based services conditional on viable collection/access models
- **Professional Capacity:** Librarians need expertise in digital licensing and vendor negotiation
- **Systemic Change Required:** Individual library innovation insufficient; requires industry/policy change
- **Equity Risk:** Digital-first approach may concentrate game access in well-funded institutions
