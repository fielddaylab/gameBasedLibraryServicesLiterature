### Summary
# **Cormier, J., & Fail Faster. (2024). *LSHM: Design Your Destiny*. Google Docs.**

This Low Stress High Mayhem handout supports a GameRT session for Jay Cormier and Fail Faster's *Design Your Destiny: Running Out of Time*. The document describes the title as an interactive time-travel story, multiple games, and an activity book in one package. In the story, players encounter games that are broken in some way, receive guidance on how the problems might be fixed, implement their own ideas, and playtest revised versions as they help the characters through the narrative.

The handout is especially relevant as a practical artifact because it addresses teachers and librarians directly and links to resources for libraries to spread the word about *Design Your Destiny*. It also points to Jay Cormier's BoardGameGeek profile, Fail Faster's online game-design course, Cormier's other work, a Kickstarter preview actual play, and a broad set of game-design readings. These include resources on tabletop role-playing in schools and public libraries, indie game development, game jam history and practice, game jam guides, the International Conference on Game Jams, and Carnegie Mellon University Press's free *Game Jam Guide* curriculum ebook.

The game-design-tool section widens the item beyond one title. It points to Twine, RPG Maker, Unity, Godot, DIY adventure-game tools, and a one-page RPG generator. The play-alikes include *The Ultimate Micro-RPG Book*, *Cloud Dungeon*, *Honey Heist*, *Lasers and Feelings*, and Oliver Darkshire's one-page RPG template. The watch-alikes and read-alikes gather time-loop and time-travel media such as *Doctor Who*, *Russian Doll*, *Groundhog Day*, *Palm Springs*, *Slaughterhouse-Five*, *Before the Coffee Gets Cold*, *This Is How You Lose the Time War*, *A Wrinkle in Time*, *11/22/63*, and *The 7 1/2 Deaths of Evelyn Hardcastle*.

The standard GameRT facilitation layer is present as well: *Script Change*, lines and veils, *The Door Is Open*, the TTRPG Safety Toolkit, Twitch policy language, GameRT links, the GameRT Zotero library, and contact information. Together these elements show a service pattern that combines a streamed game session, creator/course links, game-design pedagogy, development tools, advisory lists, and safety norms.

For the larger GBLS project, this item is useful as a primary artifact for thinking about game-design programming and library-friendly game creation resources. It suggests how a library-adjacent program can move from playing a title to discussing design repair, playtesting, game jams, tools, and curriculum resources. That practical pattern may be useful later for appendices or program templates.

This source should remain outside the main literature review. It is not scholarship, not a formal program evaluation, and not a detailed library-service case study. It may support future practical guidance on game design workshops, game jams, and advisory pathways, but it should not be cited as evidence for GBLS outcomes.
