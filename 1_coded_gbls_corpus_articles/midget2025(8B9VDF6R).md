# **Midget, R. (2025). Intellectual property or modern folklore? The problem with open gaming licenses in tabletop gaming. *Seattle University Law Review, 48*, 1129.**

Midget's law review Note addresses open gaming licenses attached to tabletop role-playing games, especially the way publishers may use "open" licensing language to suggest accessibility, creator freedom, and community goodwill while retaining or expanding control. The abstract frames open gaming licenses as legally and culturally important because TTRPGs depend on a distinctive interplay between publishers and players: published rule systems, settings, and brands circulate through player interpretation, homebrew creation, third-party publishing, streaming, actual play, and community norms.

The Note appears to proceed in six parts. First, it explains what TTRPGs are, including the hobby's defining features and surrounding culture. Second, it examines which elements of a TTRPG system may be copyrightable. Third, it applies those questions to *Dungeons & Dragons* as a case study. Fourth, it compares Wizards of the Coast's Open Gaming License 1.0 and Paizo's Open RPG Creative License, with attention to contract and licensing issues and possible copyright misuse. Fifth, it reviews Wizards of the Coast's history of legal action against other creators and the tension between that history and the brand image it projects. Finally, it proposes legal responses.

The abstract's doctrinal proposals are direct. Midget argues that courts should treat licenses that seek to expand publisher control over noncopyrightable game elements as copyright abuse and bar infringement claims while such licenses are in effect. In the alternative, courts should find such licenses unenforceable as contracts if they lack traditional elements such as consideration and notice. The Note also calls for sanctions or regulatory responses to meritless lawsuits and anticompetitive behavior, while urging players and publishers to promote open licenses that actually fulfill the promise of openness.

For GBLS, the source is adjacent but not central. It could matter to libraries that run TTRPG design workshops, collect tabletop role-playing materials, advise patrons about third-party game ecosystems, host creator talks, or support open culture, because licensing shapes what creators believe they can reuse, remix, publish, teach, and share. It also helps explain why tabletop games are not only books or event scripts but legal-cultural ecosystems involving rules, brands, fan production, creator communities, and contested openness.

Even so, this source should remain outside the main literature review. It is legal scholarship, not library literature; it does not examine library collections, programming, access, instruction, facilitation, or assessment. Its relevance to GBLS is indirect and would be best reserved for a future appendix or background note on tabletop publishing, creator ecosystems, and intellectual-property issues. It should not be cited as evidence for game-based library service outcomes.
