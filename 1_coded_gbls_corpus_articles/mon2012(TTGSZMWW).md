### Summary
# **Mon, L. M. (2012). Professional avatars: Librarians and educators in virtual worlds. *Journal of Documentation, 68*(3), 318-329. https://doi.org/10.1108/00220411211225566**

Mon examines librarianship and education in virtual world settings, focusing on how librarians and educators establish professional identity and trust while teaching and providing information services as avatars in Second Life. The study treats virtual worlds as highly graphical, immersive, and "game-like" settings where the professional self is not only textual or institutional but visibly embodied. For GBLS, this article helps explain why library work inside game worlds or game-adjacent virtual platforms requires more than simply opening a virtual branch: staff must manage avatar appearance, spatial cues, credibility, patron expectations, and platform culture.

The study uses a qualitative, ethnographic approach. Mon spent more than a year and a half as a participant observer in Second Life librarian and educator communities, learning the platform through roles such as patron, librarian, student, educator, group member, event organizer, meeting leader, conference attendee, tour leader, and virtual workplace builder. The interview phase included 12 in-world semi-structured interviews with seven librarians and five educators, conducted in Second Life workplaces or research facilities, along with visits to 50 virtual world workplaces. The analysis is framed through Goffman's concepts of presentation of self, face, role, setting, frame, and control over information and conversation.

The article's central finding is that librarians and educators had to establish professional identity, credibility, and trust for two different audiences: "newbies" and "residents." Newer users could be reassured by conservative, humanoid, friendly, professional avatar appearances and familiar workplace imagery such as books, bookshelves, computers, classrooms, and reference desks. Experienced Second Life residents, however, often read avatar quality as cultural competence. For them, a default or visibly novice avatar could reduce credibility, so librarians and educators sometimes purchased higher-quality skins, clothing, animations, and accessories to signal that they belonged in the virtual world.

Mon's findings are especially useful because they show that virtual-world library service is embodied and performative. Librarians and educators described wanting avatars that would be "taken seriously," and female avatars often struggled to find professional clothing in a marketplace oriented toward entertainment and display. Some participants changed avatar gender, ethnicity, or style depending on community expectations. Others used avatars as part of learning scenarios, historical reenactments, or case-study performances, making the avatar itself part of the informational environment rather than merely a pointer to a real-world professional.

The study also shows how virtual work unsettled traditional library space. In the 50 observed Second Life libraries, common features included photos and posters, books and bookshelves, computers, and reference desks. These cues helped visitors recognize scholarly purpose and trust that they were interacting with a "real" librarian, but participants also questioned whether virtual libraries should simply rebuild physical libraries. Some worked anywhere rather than at a stable desk, served international users across time zones and languages, used translation tools, placed reference desks outside buildings, or experimented with open areas and nontraditional environments. The key problem was not whether Second Life could imitate a library, but how visible setting and avatar cues could frame credible information interactions.

Alternative identity is another important finding. Five of the 12 participants maintained separate "alt" identities. Alts could be used for educational videos, role-playing characters, backup inventory, research, money management, or uninterrupted work when a main professional avatar was visible to contacts and likely to receive instant messages. This helps GBLS think about staff presence in online game worlds: persistent identity can build relationships, but it also creates interruption, privacy, labor, and boundary-management issues.

For the larger GBLS project, Mon contributes to the virtual-platform strand rather than to game programming narrowly. The article does not study a game night, game collection, or designed game-based learning program. Its relevance is that Second Life was widely perceived as a game or game-like environment, and librarians had to make professional service legible within that frame. Mon therefore gives the review vocabulary for avatar-mediated service, professional identity, credibility, trust, workplace staging, and platform-native expectations. These issues remain relevant for libraries considering services inside Minecraft, Roblox, social VR, Meta Horizon Worlds, Discord-linked game communities, or other persistent virtual environments where library staff appear through avatars, handles, or designed spaces.

The article's limits are also clear. It is based on a small interview sample from 2008 and a platform moment that has changed substantially. It does not measure patron outcomes, assess long-term service sustainability, or compare virtual-world library services with other online channels. Still, its ethnographic detail makes it one of the stronger sources for understanding the social mechanics of library work in game-like virtual environments, especially the tension between being recognizably professional and being fluent enough in the platform culture to be trusted by residents.

# Jedlicka, C. M., & Eaton, M. E. (2024). Creating a homemade mobile augmented reality game in a community college library: An open source approach. *Journal of Web Librarianship*. https://doi.org/10.1080/19322909.2024.2391851

Unique identifier: `jedlickaCreatingHomemadeMobile2024`; Zotero item key `CQK5X98J`; attachment key `7KHWCHSP`.

Jedlicka and Eaton (2024) describe how librarians at Kingsborough Community College built a mobile augmented reality orientation game for new students using open-source tools and existing library infrastructure. The project began from a practical outreach problem: the library wanted a fun, welcoming activity that would orient students to library locations and services, reduce unease about the academic library, and provide a positive early experience. Instead of adopting a commercial AR platform, the librarians built a browser-based Web AR game using AR.js, A-Frame, JavaScript, and LibGuides.

The article is especially valuable because it documents the constraints behind the design. KCC is a public community college library with no budget for the activity, limited staff time, no large programming team, weak Wi-Fi, and a two-month launch window. Those constraints made a commercial app less attractive: students might need to download another app, storage and Wi-Fi could become barriers, vendors might change terms or discontinue platforms, and subscription costs might not fit library values or budgets. An open-source, browser-based approach let the game load once, run client-side without network calls during play, avoid app downloads, and remain shareable through openly available code.

The game itself was a self-guided AR tour. Students scanned a QR code, enabled their phone camera, and walked around the library looking for printed visual markers. Scanning each marker triggered a virtual shape and checked off a location in a drop-down progress list. After all eight markers were found, students saw a congratulations screen and returned to librarians for a small candy reward and praise. The locations were limited to the library's second floor, where service points and student-centered amenities such as the Reference Desk, Circulation, computers, printers, leisure books, and study rooms were located. A donated tablet and a paper checklist were available for students without compatible devices or for accessibility support.

The results are modest but concrete. In initial drop-in beta testing during two afternoon reference shifts, 12 students completed the activity and a similar number began but did not finish, often because they passed by with a class that lacked time. Slow Wi-Fi sometimes delayed the initial load, but once the game loaded it ran smoothly; 10 of 12 completers used their own phones and two used the library tablet. Students quickly adapted to the game, mostly ignored the paper checklist, and gave positive comments such as "fun," "cool," "pretty clear," and "pretty entertaining." One student said the activity helped him get to know the library and discover study rooms.

The second phase changed the service model. When librarians integrated the AR activity into introductory library instruction sessions, completion and engagement improved substantially: all 34 students across three sessions completed the activity. Student and faculty feedback remained positive, and students described the activity as engaging, fun, a good starter for new people, and useful even for someone who had been enrolled for years but did not know everything in the library. The authors conclude that the AR game worked better as a class-based activity than as an elective standalone game.

For the larger GBLS project, this source contributes practical implementation knowledge. It shows that AR orientation does not require a commercial platform or professional developer, but it does require a technically curious librarian with JavaScript skill, planning time, iterative testing, device contingencies, marker printing, staff facilitation, and a willingness to accept imperfect technology. It also shows how game-based orientation can be tied to actual library space rather than abstract service information. The strongest design lesson is to match the game format to the workflow: a self-guided drop-in activity may seem flexible, but classroom integration can produce better participation and completion.

The limitations are significant. Data collection was informal, based on observation and brief verbal feedback rather than formal surveys, learning tests, anxiety measures, or long-term use data. Accessibility remained a concern because AR is heavily visual and paper workarounds are not equivalent to the AR experience. Technical sustainability is also unresolved: JavaScript libraries change quickly, AR.js development has slowed, LibGuides analytics were limited, and future functionality would require more coding. Even so, the case is useful because it makes the hidden infrastructure of a small, open-source GBLS project visible.
