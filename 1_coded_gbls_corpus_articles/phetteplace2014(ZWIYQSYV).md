## Summary
Phetteplace and Felker provide an accessible introduction to gamification in library contexts, defining gamification as the application of game mechanics (points, badges, leaderboards, progress tracking, challenges) to non-game activities to encourage participation and engagement. The article surveys a range of librarian-developed examples across public, academic, and school library contexts, demonstrating how gamification has been adapted to support various library services.

Examples discussed include:
- **Circulation challenges** where patrons earn points or badges for checking out materials, encouraging use of specific collections
- **Reading contests and summer-reading programs** where progress tracking and rewards motivate sustained reading
- **Reference and instruction** applications using point systems to encourage patron learning and participation
- **Library loyalty programs** with tiered rewards for patron behaviors

The authors emphasize that gamification must be aligned with library goals and patron interests. They caution against gamification as a substitute for poorly-designed services: the underlying service must be appealing before gamification mechanics will generate sustained engagement. Effective gamification requires understanding patron motivation, defining clear goals and rules, providing meaningful feedback, and allowing patrons agency in their choices.

The article acknowledges both benefits and risks. Benefits include increased engagement, visibility of library services, and data collection on patron behavior. Risks include overuse of extrinsic rewards (potentially undermining intrinsic motivation), complexity in system design, potential for excluding patrons who dislike gaming mechanics, and questions about sustainability and return on investment.

The article is primarily descriptive and practitioner-oriented rather than empirical. It does not present original research but synthesizes existing library-based applications of gamification. Phetteplace and Felker position gamification as one tool among many for library service design, useful when aligned with clear objectives but not universally applicable.

## Productive Incongruences and Challenges

The article does not distinguish between games and gamification clearly for all examples. Some described systems (like reading contests) predate the concept of gamification and could be understood as traditional library programs with some game-like features rather than formal gamification. The authors do not engage deeply with game design theory or learning science—they treat gamification primarily as a practical engagement tool without examining mechanisms or outcomes rigorously.

The article is also somewhat dated relative to contemporary GBLS literature. Since 2014, library gaming has expanded significantly beyond gamification to include full games (digital and tabletop), escape rooms, virtual reality, and more sustained gaming programs. Gamification remains relevant but represents only one modality within the broader GBLS landscape.

## Suggested Review Contributions

**Target Section: Programming Formats and Service Models**

Phetteplace and Felker contribute a foundational, practitioner-friendly overview of gamification as a service design approach. Their examples and framework can support the review's discussion of why libraries adopt games and gaming mechanics—particularly the emphasis that successful implementation requires alignment between game mechanics, patron motivation, and library goals. Their caution about gamification as a "veneer" on poor services also aligns with the review's emphasis on context-dependent outcomes and the importance of underlying service quality.

**Target Section: Collections, Circulation, and Equitable Access**

The authors' discussion of gamified circulation and collection promotion demonstrates how game mechanics can increase visibility and use of existing materials. However, their acknowledgment of potential exclusion risks also contributes to conversations about who gamification serves and who it might exclude—an important equity consideration for GBLS design.
