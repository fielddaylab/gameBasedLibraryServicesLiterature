### Summary
# **Webber, S., & Nahl, D. (2011). Sustaining learning for LIS through use of a virtual world. *IFLA Journal, 37*(1), 5-15. https://doi.org/10.1177/0340035210397137**

Webber and Nahl argue that Second Life and other virtual worlds offered library and information science a sustainable environment for teaching, continuing professional development, outreach, research, curriculum development, and professional collaboration. The article is grounded in the authors' own practice: Webber taught LIS and information-literacy students through the University of Sheffield's Infolit iSchool island, while Nahl taught University of Hawaii MLIS courses, mentored students in Second Life, and helped develop the LIS Student Union and Community Virtual Library activities. Their claim is not that Second Life is a conventional game, but that its avatar-based, persistent, three-dimensional, user-created environment became a new LIS learning laboratory.

The article first identifies Second Life affordances that made it attractive for LIS education. These include avatars and social presence, text and voice communication, object building and scripting, interactive content, live web pages, simulated environments, community events, volunteer reference services, and geographically dispersed collaboration. Webber and Nahl connect these affordances to online LIS education problems: distance students may need flexible access and community, librarians may need professional-development opportunities without travel, and future information professionals may need to understand virtual worlds as information grounds used by patrons, educators, and professional communities.

The authors also map librarian activity in Second Life. Librarians supported academic staff and students, taught virtual classes, ran book groups and author readings, recreated historical or fictional settings, built interactive learning objects, provided inquiry and reference services, mocked up buildings or services, offered virtual study spaces, organized CPD, and participated in the broader in-world economy and community. They describe the Community Virtual Library as a volunteer-maintained service and note that virtual-world librarianship could generate new roles, including paid virtual-library work in health-information settings.

Webber's teaching examples are especially relevant to information literacy and research training. Undergraduate students interviewed Second Life residents about their information behavior, analyzed transcripts, and reflected on interviewing in a virtual environment. Students also displayed swine-flu information-literacy presentations on three-dimensional models of the SCONUL Seven Pillars and guided visitors through the exhibits during Information Literacy Week in Second Life. Master's students studied Second Life as a learning environment, visited educational sites, attended a Virtual Worlds Best Practices in Education conference session, created posters, and presented them inside a virtual village. Webber emphasizes access to international interviewees and educators, concrete exploration of abstract learning concepts through 3D models, and a stronger sense of place and institutional culture than conventional two-dimensional online tools often provide.

Nahl's University of Hawaii examples make virtual-world librarianship into professional practice. Students in MLIS courses completed reference-shadowing assignments at the Info Island reference desk, planned and produced professional events on Info Islands and ALA Island, worked with librarian mentors, created subject guides and interactive exhibits, practiced reference, communication, marketing, participant observation, and building skills, and integrated Google Groups, Google Docs, Flickr, YouTube, sloog, machinima, and web archives into their virtual-world projects. Student-produced events included an LIS career fair, Banned Books Week programming, an ACRL Information Literacy and Web 2.0 panel, a Hawaiian statehood exhibit, and an LIS student poster conference judged by librarian adjudicators.

The CPD section shows why Second Life mattered as a professional learning platform. Webber and Nahl describe large virtual conferences, tours of specialized environments, themed events such as Information Literacy Week, recurring discussion series, professional meetings, training workshops, sandbox-based skill classes, and interactive exhibits. The benefits they identify include access to international speakers and audiences, no travel cost, flexible attendance across time zones, stronger social presence than many web tools, and opportunities for librarians from different sectors to meet, collaborate, present, and learn. They also acknowledge barriers: participants need reliable broadband, adequate graphics and processing power, time to learn how to function in a virtual world, and willingness to contribute if they want full value from the professional community.

For GBLS, the article extends the virtual-world strand from public-facing service into LIS education, staff development, and professional infrastructure. It shows that game-like virtual platforms can support library work by becoming places where librarians learn, teach, prototype, mentor, exhibit, host reference interactions, and build professional networks. It also complicates later VR and metaverse discussions by reminding the review that many current questions about immersive library services have older roots in Second Life: access barriers, staff legitimacy, training demands, platform labor, international collaboration, virtual reference, in-world collections, and the need to design activities that use spatial and social affordances rather than merely reproducing a website in 3D.
