## Purpose & Scope

*The Atlas of New Librarianship* is a foundational philosophical and theoretical work by R. David Lankes that articulates a new vision of librarianship grounded in community engagement and information reform. Rather than treating libraries as repositories of materials, Lankes proposes that libraries are fundamentally about improving the flow and access of information in service to community betterment.

The work provides a conceptual framework for reimagining library practice in the 21st century, establishing what Lankes calls a "new social compact" between libraries and their communities. The Atlas addresses fundamental questions about library philosophy, community engagement, and the role of librarians as facilitators of knowledge and information rather than merely gatekeepers of collections.

## Key Findings & Arguments

- **Librarianship as Information Reform:** Lankes positions librarians as facilitators of information and knowledge work who help communities address information needs and improve outcomes
- **Community-Centered Practice:** The framework emphasizes that library success is measured by community improvement and engagement, not collection size
- **Conceptual Foundations:** The work synthesizes philosophical traditions including sense-making theory, motivation theory, constructivism, and postmodernism to establish theoretical grounding for practice
- **Service Ecology Perspective:** Implicitly presents libraries as interconnected systems where collections, programs, spaces, and expertise work together

## Contribution to GBLS

While *The Atlas* does not directly address games, it provides essential theoretical grounding for games-based library services by:
- Establishing that libraries should be responsive to community interests and needs (games are a community interest)
- Positioning librarians as facilitators rather than gatekeepers, supporting game programs and collections
- Articulating a philosophical framework that values experiential learning and community engagement—both central to games-based services
- Setting a conceptual stage where games-based services align with larger library missions of information and community improvement

This work is foundational for understanding why and how libraries embrace games as legitimate service modalities.

## Identified Gaps & Notes

- The Atlas predates widespread library adoption of games; games are not addressed in the content examined
- The theoretical framework, while supportive of games-based services, requires interpretation and application to game-specific contexts
- High-level conceptual work requires grounding in specific implementation and evaluation literature
