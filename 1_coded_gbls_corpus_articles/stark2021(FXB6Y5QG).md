### Summary
# **Stark, R. K., Opuda, E., McElfresh, J., & Kauffroath, K. (2021). Scavenging for evidence: A systematic review of scavenger hunts in academic libraries. *The Journal of Academic Librarianship, 47*(3), Article 102345. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.acalib.2021.102345**

Stark, Opuda, McElfresh, and Kauffroath systematically review academic-library scavenger hunts as outreach and instruction tools. Their starting point is that academic libraries often use active learning, play-based exercises, and gamification to make orientation and information-literacy encounters more engaging, especially for students first encountering the library. Scavenger hunts are treated as activities in which participants physically move through a defined library space to encounter information relevant to later use of the library. The authors focus on physical or hybrid scavenger hunts, excluding virtual-only activities and studies without any form of assessment.

The study is framed as evidence-based practice. The authors initially hoped to determine whether librarian-led scavenger hunts effectively oriented students to academic libraries and whether they served as effective information-literacy instruction. Because the available literature was too thin to answer that question directly, they broadened the review to explore examples, methodological quality, and effectiveness claims. Their protocol used PRISMA-P guidance, Cochrane recommendations, SPICE and PICO frameworks, searches across databases, grey literature, conference proceedings, Google Scholar, citation chasing, and a pre-submission search rerun.

Thirteen case studies met the final inclusion criteria. All used some form of formal or informal assessment, but the reporting was uneven. Only four articles explicitly stated the number of participants in the assessment, eight described the participant population, and only one clearly stated the length of the scavenger hunt. The authors found that desired outcomes included fun or engagement, orientation to library spaces, information-literacy learning, technology use, orientation to materials and resources, library policies, and campus partners. The most common learning objectives were space orientation, materials and resources orientation, and use of library technologies; fun and engagement were also common, while information literacy, policies, and campus partners appeared less often.

The included scavenger hunts varied widely in design. Twelve used technology in some form, ten included a theme, eleven used teams, just over half used a time element or race structure, and more than half revised the hunt during the reported project. Only a few assessment tools were reviewed or tested before use. Findings reported by the case studies often included fun or engagement, space familiarity, technology familiarity, familiarity with materials, resources, services, and policies, confidence or comfort using the library, and improvement of scavenger-hunt methods. However, the authors emphasize that these findings often did not align cleanly with stated objectives and were frequently based on informal observation, informal feedback, worksheets, or local surveys.

The review's strongest contribution is its critique of the evidence base. Stark et al. conclude that the published literature does not provide enough evidence to make an evidence-based decision about whether scavenger hunts are effective for academic-library orientation or instruction. The problems include poor research design, unclear reporting, inconsistent data collection, missing or incomplete participant counts, lack of demographic data beyond class year, lack of validated instruments, mixed pilot and non-pilot data, unreported study timeframes, and studies that describe creation and implementation more than outcomes. They also note operational barriers: scavenger hunts can require significant planning, staff coordination, communication across library workers, technology support, scalable design, and partnerships with student affairs or other campus units.

For the larger GBLS project, Stark et al. are directly relevant because scavenger hunts, library races, clue games, and orientation quests are common game-based library service formats. The article validates why these formats are appealing: they can introduce students to library spaces, resources, services, technologies, and partners while setting a welcoming tone. At the same time, it prevents the review from overclaiming. A scavenger hunt that feels fun, draws participants, or helps students move through a building is not automatically evidence of information-literacy learning, durable library confidence, transfer, or later use of resources. GBLS programs need objectives, populations, intervention designs, measures, timeframes, and findings to line up.

The article also gives concrete research guidance for future GBLS assessment. Future studies should collect and report meaningful demographics, participant counts for each iteration, replicable and methodologically sound designs, stable measures across the data-collection period, outcome measures tied to research questions, separately reported data for each intervention, and both short-term and long-term benefit assessment. Stark et al. acknowledge that control groups, blinding, validated measures, and richer demographics may be difficult in ordinary library settings, but they treat those constraints as reasons to improve reporting rather than reasons to rely indefinitely on informal case description.

Proposed text: "Stark, Opuda, McElfresh, and Kauffroath (2021) synthesize this scavenger-hunt and race-style orientation literature through a systematic review of 13 assessed academic-library cases. The review shows why the format is popular: hunts can introduce spaces, resources, services, technologies, policies, partners, and a welcoming tone through themed, team-based, often technology-supported movement through the library; but it also shows that most published cases emphasize fun, engagement, and implementation more than tested information-literacy or orientation outcomes."

Proposed text: "Stark et al. (2021) make this assessment problem field-wide for scavenger hunts: only four of 13 included cases reported assessment population counts, only one reported a clear timeframe, no study combined a stated sample size with a stated study length, and no included article used a validated measure. Their recommendations give GBLS a basic reporting agenda: collect demographics, participant counts, stable measures, intervention-specific data, aligned outcomes, and short- and long-term evidence rather than relying on local enthusiasm, fun, or familiarity claims alone."
