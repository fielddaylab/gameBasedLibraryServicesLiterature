## Purpose & Scope

This 2023 study investigates gamification services implemented by university libraries across China. Rather than studying a single institution, Zhou and Yang conducted a systematic content analysis of 147 Chinese university libraries' publicly announced gamification services (on websites, Weibo, WeChat, and other platforms) over 14 months (July 2021–August 2022).

The research analyzes gamification service construction across four dimensions: functions served, forms employed, game elements used, and game mechanics implemented. This provides a broad empirical snapshot of how contemporary Chinese university libraries are applying game design principles to library services.

## Key Findings

- **Primary Functions Served by Gamification:**
  - Scientific and cultural knowledge popularization
  - Information literacy education
  - Reading promotion
  - Library service promotion and awareness
  - Library orientation/entrance education

- **Common Game Forms/Mechanics:**
  - Q&A (questions and answers contests)
  - Points contests/leaderboard competitions
  - Level-based progression (passing game levels)
  - Treasure hunts/scavenger hunts
  - Multiple other forms combining mechanics

- **Game Elements Employed:**
  - Points systems (most common)
  - Leaderboards/rankings
  - Grades/ratings
  - Combinations of multiple elements

- **Mechanics Implementation:**
  - Often combine points with other elements to enhance participation
  - Design varies significantly across institutions; "sometimes arbitrary" and not optimally designed
  - Lack of systematic design principles or evidence-based approach

## GBLS-Specific Contributions

- **Global Evidence:** Demonstrates gamification adoption in non-Western context (China); shows this is not exclusively Anglo-American practice
- **Widespread Service Adoption:** Shows gamification is being used across multiple library service functions, not just single applications
- **Practical Implementation Mapping:** Provides taxonomy of gamification forms and mechanics used in real library settings
- **Service Ecology:** Documents how gamification bridges multiple library functions (collection discovery, orientation, literacy, promotion)
- **Engagement Function:** Shows libraries using gamification explicitly to increase attendance, participation, and patron engagement

## Design Patterns Identified

- **Gamification Functions:** Can support diverse library goals—not just engagement but learning, discovery, and literacy
- **Element Combinations:** More sophisticated gamifications combine multiple elements (points + leaderboards + grades) for richer incentive structures
- **Accessibility:** Most gamifications appear relatively accessible (not requiring specialized equipment or technology)

## Identified Implementation Challenges

- **Design Quality:** Many implementations lack systematic design principles
- **Outcome Evaluation:** No evidence presented about whether gamified services achieve stated learning or engagement goals
- **Mechanics Optimization:** Authors suggest Chinese libraries could benefit from more rigorous game design and evidence-based approach
- **Transfer Potential:** Success of gamification in one context may not transfer to another without adaptation

## Research Limitations & Gaps

- **Descriptive Only:** No evaluation data on outcomes or efficacy of gamified services
- **No Participant Data:** Study analyzes service announcements; does not include patron experiences or feedback
- **Geographic Specificity:** Findings limited to Chinese university libraries; transferability to other contexts unclear
- **Temporal Snapshot:** Data represents moment in time; does not track evolution or sustainability
- **Surface Analysis:** Does not deeply examine implementation processes, facilitator training, or institutional conditions enabling success
- **Missing Evaluation Framework:** No proposed methods for assessing whether gamification achieves learning or engagement outcomes

## Broader Implications

- Gamification is adopted across diverse library contexts and services globally
- Implementation varies significantly; lack of standardization or evidence-based practice
- Opportunity exists for libraries to systematically evaluate and improve gamification design
- Potential for translation of successful gamification models across institutional and geographic contexts
