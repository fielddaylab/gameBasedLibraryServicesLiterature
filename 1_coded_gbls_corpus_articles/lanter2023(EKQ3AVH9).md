### Summary
# **Lanter, J., Norton, S., Wright, A., Porter, M., & Costello, D. (2023). RTRT: Addressing Censorship. *Google Docs*. https://docs.google.com/document/u/1/d/13OhgA3ODQFRkl6MrG2miUghXfHjRKMqm5NJEgwKZgOc/edit?usp=drive_web&ouid=104872546336741826329&usp=embed_facebook**

This item is a four-page Round Table Round Table resource document on addressing censorship, associated with Julia Lanter, Shawn Norton, Amie Wright, Molly Porter, and Danielle Costello. It appears to support a professional conversation involving the Graphic Novels & Comics Round Table and GameRT rather than serve as a research article or program report. The document is structured as a resource list with sections for toolkits, articles, webinars and courses, podcasts, LibGuides, books, websites, videos, additional notes, GameRT Twitch policy, and round table contact information.

The censorship resources are relevant to game-based library services because games, comics, graphic novels, role-playing games, and other media formats often share challenge and moral-panic contexts. The sheet points to the GNCRT Addressing Challenges Toolkit, the Urban Libraries Unite Drag Story Hour Support workbook, ALA and Booklist resources about comic-book bans and challenges, articles on Dungeons & Dragons and the 1980s Satanic Panic, articles on video games and violence claims, a National Coalition Against Censorship timeline of video-game controversies, and webinars on what to do before and during a challenge. It also lists books such as Joseph Laycock's *Dangerous Games*, Jon Chad's *Pinball*, and David Hajdu's *The Ten-Cent Plague*, along with broader banned-books and anti-censorship resources.

The most directly practical part of the document is the additional-notes section. It emphasizes having a collection development plan, treating community outreach and public support as important resources, working on legislative alternatives, lobbying state and local officials, and using the GNCRT Addressing Challenges Toolkit for collection development plans and challenge policies. The GameRT Twitch policy also names conduct expectations for online spaces: kindness and respect, no hate speech, moderator authority, reporting harassment to moderators, and private messaging for sensitive concerns.

For the larger GBLS project, the resource is useful as implementation scaffolding. It reminds librarians that game collections and programs are not only logistical or recreational services; they may require intellectual-freedom preparation, collection-development policy language, challenge procedures, community allies, public communication, online moderation norms, and awareness of older controversies around comics, D&D, video games, and violent media. This context matters for libraries offering TTRPGs, video-game collections, gaming streams, graphic-novel-adjacent programs, cosplay or fandom events, or teen media programming.

However, this PDF should not be added to `review.md` as substantive literature. It does not present original research, a library case study, a challenge-response analysis, or an evaluated GBLS program. It does not explain how a specific library handled a challenge to games or game programming, nor does it offer enough sustained argument to cite as evidence for outcomes, service models, or policy effectiveness. The review already includes stronger direct sources for policy and challenge issues, such as Sanders (1982) on local controversy around a public-library D&D club and Cross, Mould, and Smith (2015) on challenged-materials policies within an academic library game collection. This item is better kept as a resource-trail note for future implementation work.
