## Purpose and Scope

This comprehensive scholarly monograph examines how digital technologies are transforming scholarly practices, communication, and infrastructure across academic disciplines. Rather than focusing on technology alone, Borgman emphasizes the sociotechnical dimensions of digital scholarship—the interplay of technical systems, social practices, policies, and economic arrangements that shape how scholars produce, share, and access information.

The work is organized into three thematic sections covering: (1) foundational issues in scholarly infrastructure; (2) the continuity of scholarly communication systems alongside discontinuity in publishing; and (3) the challenges of managing research data and building infrastructure across disciplinary contexts.

## Key Findings and Arguments

### Core Thesis: Infrastructure as Sociotechnical System
Borgman argues that scholarly information infrastructure should be understood not primarily as technical systems but as integrated sociotechnical arrangements encompassing people, technology, policies, institutions, and economic models. The grand visions of e-Science and cyberinfrastructure, while grounded in legitimate technical advances, rest on assumptions about scholarly behavior that vary significantly across disciplines and research communities.

### The Data Deluge and Scale
One major driver of current infrastructure initiatives is the exponential growth of digital data generated by instrumentation, sensors, surveys, and digital archiving. However, Borgman notes that problems of scale affect both collection and retrieval: as databases grow larger, search effectiveness declines, requiring increasingly sophisticated ranking algorithms, postprocessing capabilities, and visualization tools. These scaling challenges affect data management, description, preservation, and discovery.

### Continuity and Discontinuity in Scholarly Communication
- **Continuity**: Despite technological change, the functions of scholarly communication remain stable—legitimization through peer review, dissemination of findings, access and preservation, and author roles (writer, citer, linker, submitter, collaborator).
- **Discontinuity**: The mechanisms for publishing these functions have become unstable, with tensions between print and digital forms, questions about what constitutes a "publication," and proliferation of document versions and formats.

### Disciplinary Variation
Rather than a universal model, Borgman documents how scholarship varies significantly across sciences, social sciences, and humanities in:
- Types of data and documents produced
- Information-seeking behaviors and practices
- Incentives and disincentives for sharing information
- Readiness for standardization and infrastructure investment

Sciences have matured toward "big science" with collaborative, standardized practices; social sciences are becoming increasingly data-intensive; humanities are building computational models and digitizing cultural records. Each requires different infrastructure support.

### Digital Libraries as Community Infrastructure
Digital libraries are not merely electronic versions of traditional collections but community-built, community-supported infrastructure. They encompass data, documents, metadata (describing the data and relationships among data), and exist as distributed networks supporting specific user communities. This represents a shift from viewing digital libraries as primarily about digitizing print to viewing them as integral to research and learning ecosystems.

### Information as Process, Knowledge, and Infrastructure
Borgman emphasizes that information in digital scholarship operates at multiple levels: as a process of becoming informed, as knowledge communicated, and as the infrastructure of systems, standards, policies, and agreements that enable scholarly work. Understanding information infrastructure requires attention to all three dimensions.

## Contributions to Games-Based Library Services (GBLS)

While this work predates contemporary GBLS initiatives and does not address games explicitly, it provides critical conceptual and practical frameworks relevant to GBLS scholarship:

1. **Service Ecology Framework**: The notion that outcomes depend on interconnections among collections, programs, spaces, expertise, and policies is directly applicable to designing GBLS services. Games cannot be understood in isolation but must fit within the broader library ecology.

2. **Sociotechnical Design**: Borgman's emphasis on integrating social research with technical design is valuable for GBLS implementation. Understanding how patrons actually engage with games (not assumed behavior) should guide tool, space, and service design.

3. **Information Behavior and Practice**: The detailed analysis of how different communities seek, use, and produce information provides models for understanding patron engagement with games as information resources (learning tools, cultural objects, decision-making simulations, etc.).

4. **Scaling and Discovery**: Solutions to problems of scale and discovery in digital collections have direct implications for games collections. As GBLS programs grow, retrieval and discovery mechanisms must evolve; metadata standards become increasingly important.

5. **Conditional Benefits**: Borgman's argument that benefits of infrastructure investments are conditional—depending on discipline, funding, institutional support, and broader policy context—applies directly to GBLS. Benefits depend on skilled facilitation, community assessment, inclusive design, and sustainable operations rather than technology alone.

6. **Disciplinary and Institutional Variation**: Just as different academic fields have different information practices, different library types (academic, public, school, cultural institutions) will need different GBLS models. A one-size-fits-all approach to games-based services is unlikely to succeed.

## Gaps and Limitations

1. **Temporal Focus**: Published in 2007, the work reflects pre-mobile, pre-social-media scholarship ecosystem. Contemporary GBLS scholarship may need to address different infrastructure challenges (APIs, interoperability with proprietary platforms, etc.).

2. **Limited Attention to Learning**: While Borgman addresses e-Learning briefly, she does not deeply examine learning outcomes or pedagogical aspects of digital scholarship, which are central to GBLS.

3. **Cultural Heritage and Museums**: Though mentioned, the work does not fully explore how cultural institutions (museums, archives) manage information resources in ways that might inform GBLS approaches to games as cultural objects.

4. **Practical Guidance**: The work is primarily analytic and conceptual; it offers less practical guidance on implementation decisions for specific library contexts.

5. **Games Absent**: Most significantly, games do not appear in this analysis. The framework must be extended to address games as information resources, cultural media, and service mechanisms—not merely traditional documents and datasets.

## Research Questions for GBLS

This work raises important questions for GBLS scholarship:

- How does games-based learning fit within the broader information ecology of academic libraries?
- What are the "information practices" of game players and designers? How do they differ across disciplines and communities?
- What metadata standards and discovery mechanisms best serve games as information resources?
- How do GBLS services scale as game collections grow? What retrieval mechanisms work at different scales?
- What constitutes "scholarly work" in game studies? How do games function as research data, documents, or other information forms?
- How do incentives and disincentives affect game sharing and access across institutional boundaries?

## Methodological Notes

**Evidence_Type:** This is an evidence synthesis work—Borgman surveys and synthesizes existing scholarship across information science, library science, sociology of science, and policy studies. While she references research projects and case studies, she does not present original empirical data.

**Conceptual Grounding:** Heavily informed by sociotechnical systems theory, information behavior research, and critical perspectives on technology and society. The analysis emphasizes context-dependency and conditional outcomes rather than universal principles.

**Disciplinary Scope:** Primarily addresses research and scholarly communication, with less attention to K–12, public library, or informal learning contexts. GBLS applications in these contexts may not be directly addressed.
