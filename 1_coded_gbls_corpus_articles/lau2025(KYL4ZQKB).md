## Purpose & Scope

This 2025 viewpoint article proposes a novel approach to heritage preservation: using immersive virtual environments (IVEs) to archive and collect heritage gameplay activities in digital libraries. Rather than treating games as static objects to be cataloged, Lau argues for capturing gameplay *practices* and *activities*—the lived, embodied, cultural heritage of how games are played in specific communities and historical periods.

The article is exploratory and conceptual, proposing a framework for thinking about how IVEs could preserve fading heritage game activities (traditional games, cultural games) in ways that center experience, presence, and embodied learning rather than just artifact documentation.

## Key Arguments & Framework

- **Preservation Challenge:** Traditional games and heritage gameplay activities are fading as globalization and digital entertainment shift cultural practices
- **IVE Capabilities:** Immersive virtual environments can:
  - Create vivid, multisensorial records of gameplay practices
  - Enable telepresence experiences that recreate the feeling of participating
  - Preserve not just artifacts but the embodied experience of play
  - Provide engaging learning experiences that evoke memories and emotional connection

- **Visitor-Centered Approach:** Modern digital library users expect not just to study collections, but to *experience* them
  - Seek sensorial experiences and emotional engagement
  - Want personalized, autonomous exploration
  - Expect multisensorial and spatial interaction
  - Value experiential dimensions beyond information transmission

- **Three Knowledge Scopes Addressed:**
  1. Applications of IVEs and telepresence for heritage collection
  2. Heritage and historical study of traditional gameplay activities in digital libraries
  3. Conservation and inheritance studies in digital libraries

## GBLS-Specific Contributions

- **Games as Cultural Heritage:** Treats games—particularly heritage/traditional games—as legitimate cultural artifacts worthy of preservation and research access
- **Archival Innovation:** Proposes new methods for preserving not just games themselves, but gameplay practices and cultural contexts
- **Experience-Centered Preservation:** Values embodied, experiential dimensions of gameplay, not just rule systems or physical artifacts
- **Participatory Archives:** IVEs could facilitate community participation in heritage preservation (e.g., recording elders playing traditional games)
- **Educational Access:** Makes heritage games accessible to new generations through immersive experiences

## Implementation Framework (Proposed)

- **Activity-Based Collections:** Archives focus on heritage gameplay *activities* (how games are played, social contexts, cultural meanings)
- **Telepresence Design:** Recreate physical and social experience of gameplay, not just visual appearance
- **Community Engagement:** Involve communities in documenting and preserving their own gaming traditions
- **Emotional Connection:** Design experiences that evoke memories and cultural pride among heritage communities
- **Accessibility:** Make heritage games discoverable and experiential to broader audiences

## Service Conditions & Considerations

- **Infrastructure:** IVE technology requires significant technical investment and expertise
- **Cultural Sensitivity:** Preservation of heritage games requires community collaboration and respectful representation
- **Facilitation:** Librarians need training in IVE design, cultural heritage practices, and community engagement
- **Sustainability:** Long-term maintenance of immersive experiences and technological infrastructure challenging
- **Openness:** Success depends on librarian and visitor willingness to embrace new preservation modalities

## Identified Gaps & Limitations

- **Purely Conceptual:** No empirical implementation data or evidence of IVE effectiveness for heritage preservation
- **Technology Barriers:** Prohibitive costs and technical expertise limit accessibility for smaller institutions
- **Community Questions:** Unclear how to navigate questions of cultural ownership, representation, and benefit-sharing
- **Sustainability Concerns:** IVEs require ongoing maintenance and technological updating; long-term viability uncertain
- **Evaluation Framework:** No proposed methods for assessing whether IVE preservation adequately captures heritage gameplay
- **Geographic/Cultural Specificity:** Examples and frameworks may not translate across different cultural traditions

## Theoretical Contributions

- Extends preservation and archive studies beyond physical/digital objects to lived experiences
- Positions games as legitimate cultural heritage requiring specialized preservation approaches
- Proposes experience-centered rather than purely descriptive archival methodology
- Challenges traditional notions of what constitutes valuable heritage documentation
