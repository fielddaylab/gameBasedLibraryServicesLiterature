## Summary

Crowe and Sclippa's edited volume synthesizes practitioner expertise from 17 academic library contributors documenting the rapid expansion of game-based services in college and university libraries since 2008. The book's publication responds to a notable shift in academic librarianship: while the previous decade (2000–2008) saw emphasis on video games and digital gaming, the 2008–2020 period witnessed a cultural renaissance in tabletop board games, leading academic libraries to diversify their collections and instructional approaches.

The editors distinguish games from gamification: games are structured activities with rules, goals, feedback, and consequences; gamification applies game design elements to nongame activities. Both approaches are represented across the four-section structure.

**Section I: Overview of Games and Gamification** presents institutional case studies demonstrating holistic integration. Oxford College Library (Emory) exemplifies one pathway: beginning with game nights as community events, the library expanded into collections, instruction, and accessibility work. University of Nebraska Omaha Libraries emphasizes playfulness and creativity as outcomes, strengthening partnerships with student organizations. Winona State University's Krueger Library demonstrates gamification of staff training through badging systems and escape room onboarding. The section concludes with an accessibility framework emphasizing task analysis and inclusive design principles for gaming events and gamified instruction.

**Section II: Game Collections** covers practical challenges of acquiring, organizing, and circulating games as library materials. Contributors describe strategies for building collections from single departmental requests (e.g., game design courses) into institution-wide services. Chapters address cataloging best practices, maintenance, and evidence of social and recreational benefits, while noting faculty adoption of games in courses. The emphasis is on understanding collection development as a service planning effort, not novelty purchasing.

**Section III: Games and Gamification in Information Literacy Instruction** contains five chapters on integrating games into threshold concepts and learning outcomes. Contributions include gamified one-shot information literacy sessions that reduce anxiety through the expectation of failure built into game mechanics; online gamified grand challenges embedded in first-year experience programs; semester-long credit courses built around cooperative board games; and escape room and breakout activities designed to teach information literacy competencies, explicitly connected to the ACRL Framework for Information Literacy for Higher Education. The authors emphasize the value of designing learning outcomes first, then mapping game mechanics to those outcomes.

**Section IV: Programming and Outreach** demonstrates games as relationship-building tools. Case studies include game-based library services fairs that replace traditional marketing with mystery games; board game nights designed to foster student-faculty interaction and reduce library anxiety; and custom-designed immersive escape rooms that covertly teach library tool use while building positive first-year associations with library spaces. Authors note that games' natural capacity for interaction and communication makes them particularly valuable for outreach and engagement.

Across all sections, themes emerge: (1) games are effective when purposefully connected to institutional and learning goals, not adopted as novelty; (2) accessibility and inclusive design require deliberate planning and are achievable through task analysis; (3) games support multiple outcomes (learning, engagement, relationship-building, staff motivation); (4) success depends on collaboration across departments and sustained institutional commitment; (5) evaluation and iteration improve outcomes over time.

For the GBLS project, this volume supplies practical models of games integrated into academic library work and demonstrates the maturation of the field since 2008. The breadth of implementation models—from collections to instruction to programming—illustrates the service ecology concept: games function effectively when embedded in supportive collections, trained staff, clear outcomes, and accessible infrastructure.

## Productive Incongruences and Challenges

The book's scope is explicitly academic library focused, which limits generalizability to public, school, and special library contexts. While the introduction acknowledges the board game renaissance as a cultural trend, the book does not engage games-based learning literature or the broader media literacy scholarship that informs games in other library contexts. The "gamification" chapters sometimes conflate extrinsic reward systems (points, badges) with game-based learning, a distinction the metadata schema identifies separately. No chapters address rural academic libraries or serve as reminders about infrastructure and access barriers. The collection is primarily descriptive—reporting what libraries did and what outcomes they observed—rather than comparing implementation models, assessing long-term sustainability, or measuring causal relationships between games and learning outcomes. This aligns with the "practitioner reflection" evidence type but signals a research gap the review should address.
