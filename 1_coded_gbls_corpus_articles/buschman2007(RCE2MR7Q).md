## Summary
Buschman and Leckie's edited collection examines libraries as physical and social spaces, moving beyond the view of libraries as primarily collections or information systems. The book draws on historical, sociological, cultural, and architectural perspectives to explore how library spaces function as community gathering places, sites of social practice, and cultural institutions.

The collection includes chapters addressing:
- **Historical perspectives:** How libraries have evolved as public spaces from their origins through contemporary transformations
- **Community and social functions:** Libraries as places for community building, civic engagement, and democratic participation beyond information provision
- **Cultural meaning:** Libraries as repositories of cultural memory and public institutions supporting cultural participation
- **Design and space:** How physical architecture, furnishings, acoustics, and spatial arrangement affect user experience and library functions
- **Equity and access:** Libraries as spaces that should be welcoming and accessible to diverse communities
- **Libraries and place-based communities:** Connections between library development and neighborhood change

The editors argue that understanding libraries as places requires attending to how physical, social, and symbolic dimensions interact. Libraries are not neutral containers for collections; their spaces shape how people experience information, interact with others, and understand themselves as members of communities.

The work is multidisciplinary and primarily theoretical/conceptual rather than empirical, though it draws on case studies and scholarly research across multiple fields. Contributions range from historical analysis to contemporary observations about library use and design.

## Productive Incongruences and Challenges

This book does not address games, play, or game-based services. However, its framework—treating libraries as spaces that enable social practices, shape community experiences, and support particular ways of being together—is conceptually relevant to GBLS. Games-based services operate within and shape library spaces; understanding libraries as places where multiple social practices coexist is relevant to how GBLS fits within the broader library environment.

The book predates contemporary discussions of makerspaces, gaming areas, and playful design, so it does not directly address contemporary trends in library space design that support games and play.

## Suggested Review Contributions

**Target Section: Libraries as Playful Spaces and Service Environments**

Buschman and Leckie's emphasis on libraries as places where diverse social practices occur, and where spatial design shapes the types of engagement that are possible, contributes conceptual grounding to the review's discussion of playful library environments. Their attention to how architecture, design, and atmosphere affect what becomes possible—and for whom—aligns with the review's emphasis that space is not neutral but actively shapes service accessibility and outcomes.

The book also supports the principle that games-based services should be understood not as isolated programs but as part of a library's overall environment and mission to serve diverse community needs and foster belonging.
