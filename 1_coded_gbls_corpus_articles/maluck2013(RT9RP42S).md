### Summary
# **Maluck, T. (2013). Tales from Play It Loud. *Library Trends, 61*(4), 779-789. https://doi.org/10.1353/lib.2013.0014**

Maluck reports on introducing and managing the Play It Loud gaming program while serving as supervising young adult librarian at Richland Library Northeast in Columbia, South Carolina. The article is a practitioner case report rather than a formal experiment, but it is unusually concrete about program design, audience fit, facilitation, observed patron behavior, and how library gaming can be interpreted through youth-development goals. The program began from local evidence of demand: teens were already playing games in the building, gaming magazines circulated visibly, and the library system had Wii consoles, Wii games, and tabletop games available for branch programming.

The design choices are central to the article. Play It Loud was intentionally marketed as a loud, social program rather than a quiet library activity, and the branch's meeting room made that possible without disturbing other users. The room layout placed the Wii and projector in the center, with tabletop games along the sides. The library selected multiplayer Wii titles such as *New Super Mario Bros. Wii*, *Super Smash Bros. Brawl*, *Mario Kart*, *Wii Sports Resort*, *Just Dance*, and *Kirby's Return to Dreamland*, along with tabletop games such as chess, checkers, *Clue*, *Scrabble*, *Munchkin*, *Uno*, *Zombie Dice*, and *Jenga*. Maluck drew explicitly on Scott Nicholson's advice that library gaming should offer frequent participation opportunities and a mix of Strategy, Narrative, Action, Knowledge, and Social game archetypes.

Play It Loud was originally conceived as a teen program, but in practice it became a family and intergenerational program. It met monthly on Saturday afternoons, later twice monthly in the summer, and averaged twenty-one participants across the fiscal year, usually ages nine to sixteen with occasional younger children and adults. Patrons began bringing controllers from home, asking about the program when it was not scheduled, and treating it as part of the branch's weekend identity. Maluck also describes operational boundaries: no snacks near controllers, no M-rated or first-person shooter games, attention to safety during active Wii play, and later an age limit of ten and older because very young children could derail the teen-oriented experience.

A second program, Go Your Own Way, emerged from Play It Loud when the board game Go proved difficult to sustain beside the noise and immediacy of Wii play. With support from the American Go Foundation, the library obtained equipment, instructional materials, promotional materials, and related manga. Teen volunteers helped teach the game, and First Capture Go allowed new players to begin with short, low-frustration rounds before moving to larger boards and full rules. Attendance for the standalone Go program was smaller, often ten or fewer participants, but Maluck emphasizes the quality of interaction: parent-child play, cross-age learning, teen tutors, and a game structure that allowed different reasoning strengths to become visible.

The article's assessment frame comes from the Search Institute's 40 Developmental Assets for adolescents. Maluck maps observed behaviors from Play It Loud and Go Your Own Way onto assets such as family support, positive family communication, other adult relationships, caring neighborhood, community valuing youth, service to others, safety, family boundaries, positive peer influence, youth programs, honesty, responsibility, and cultural competence. Examples include parents learning games with children, teens setting up the room and teaching games, staff and security guards relating to youth positively, players enforcing fair play, youth being invited into other library programs, and teens moving from being unsupervised library users toward volunteering or joining the Teen Advisory Board.

For GBLS, Maluck is valuable because it treats public-library gaming as relational infrastructure rather than a simple entertainment draw. The case shows how multiplayer digital and analog games can create repeated contact between youth, parents, staff, volunteers, and peers; how facilitation practices such as turn-taking, handshake rituals, content selection, age boundaries, and room layout shape the social outcomes of play; and how a recreation-first program can become a bridge to advisory boards, volunteering, other library programs, and more explicitly educational activities. The article also cautions that one program cannot serve every age group equally well, that analog and digital games may require different acoustic and attentional environments, and that educational extensions may meet patron resistance if they feel bolted onto a beloved recreation program.

# Intawong, K., Puritat, K., Leenaraj, B., & Arayaphan, W. (2023). A gamified mobile application for first-year student orientation to promote library services. *Journal of Librarianship and Information Science, 55*(1), 137-150. https://doi.org/10.1177/09610006211067273

Unique identifier: `leenarajGamifiedMobileApplication2023`; Zotero item key `6G4UFF6N`; attachment key `QKQJJU7W`.

Intawong, Puritat, Leenaraj, and Arayaphan (2023) test CMU Journey, a gamified mobile application for first-year student orientation at Chiang Mai University. The study addresses a familiar academic-library problem: first-year students need to learn library services, OPAC searching, call numbers, and physical library navigation, but traditional orientations and e-learning modules can feel passive, disconnected, or forgettable. The authors propose mobile gamification as a way to make orientation more exploratory, memorable, and motivating.

CMU Journey adapts a Pokémon Go-style loop to the university environment. Students use mobile devices and geolocation to explore important campus places, capture monsters, answer place-based questions, and collect rewards. For the library portion, the game focuses on the main library. The core loop is explore the important place, capture the monster, answer the monster's question, and collect the reward. The mechanics include score points, a leaderboard, inventory, choice, time pressure, and map navigation. Questions require students to investigate the physical environment, so the app links digital play to library space rather than replacing the library with a standalone quiz.

The study uses an explanatory sequential mixed-method design. Sixty first-year students were recruited and randomly assigned to two groups of thirty. The control group completed an e-learning orientation, while the experimental group used CMU Journey; both activities lasted three hours and used content created by main-library staff. Students completed a pre-test, an immediate post-test, a one-month delayed post-test, and a modified short Intrinsic Motivation Inventory covering perceived competence, interest, and effort. The researchers also collected open-ended feedback from both groups.

The quantitative results are usefully mixed. Both groups improved from pre-test to post-test. The e-learning group moved from a mean of 3.73 to 12.20, and the mobile-gamification group moved from 3.66 to 14.40. The immediate between-group comparison had p = .036, which the authors treat as not significant at the stricter .01 level; therefore, the game did not clearly outperform e-learning for immediate knowledge acquisition. The delayed one-month scores were more distinctive: the e-learning group averaged 10.60 and the mobile-gamification group averaged 13.76, with p < .001. The authors conclude that the mobile orientation supported longer knowledge retention better than e-learning.

Motivation results were similarly specific rather than sweeping. The control group showed no significant change in perceived competence, interest, or effort. The experimental group showed a significant gain only for Interest, rising from 3.00 to 4.30 with p < .001; perceived competence and effort did not significantly improve. Qualitative feedback helps explain the result. Students often described the mobile orientation as fun, innovative, and a good introduction to the library, and one respondent said it made them want to explore the main library even though they had not initially understood why they needed to visit it.

For the larger GBLS project, this is a strong orientation source because it evaluates a library-service game against an alternative orientation method and includes delayed retention. It supports a careful claim: gamified mobile orientation may not automatically improve immediate learning beyond e-learning, but it can improve retention and interest when it asks students to move through real library space, answer service questions, and connect rewards to exploration. The article also reinforces the importance of comparative assessment and of measuring outcomes beyond satisfaction or attendance.

The limitations are practical and important. The sample was small, came from one university in Thailand, and included only sixty participants. The authors note Android hardware and OS problems, GPS accuracy issues, battery drain, requests for charging access, and student suggestions for richer mechanics, multiplayer features, deeper questions, and improved library spaces. These limits make the article useful for GBLS implementation planning: mobile orientation games require not only game design, but also device support, charging infrastructure, reliable geolocation, accessible alternatives, content tuning, and library-space responsiveness.
