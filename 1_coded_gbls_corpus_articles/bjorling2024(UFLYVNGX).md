## Purpose & Scope

This 2024 study addresses the teen mental health crisis in the United States through a novel library-based intervention: engaging teens (n=30) in co-designing virtual reality experiences intended to support mental health. Rather than experts designing for teens, the research centers teen voices and expertise in creating VR environments.

Conducted across three diverse geographic settings (urban Seattle, urban Washington D.C., rural Texas), the study engaged diverse groups of teens in 13-session, after-school co-design programs led by local library staff. The research investigated how teens perceive mental health, experience VR, and envision VR as a tool for mental health support.

This work is important not just as a game-based service model but as evidence of libraries responding to acute social needs and centering youth voice in solution design.

## Key Findings

### Teen Perceptions of Mental Health & VR:
1. **Mental Health Interest:** Teens are interested and willing to discuss their own mental health and wellbeing
2. **Nature & Animals:** Teens find nature and animals relaxing and helpful for mental health
3. **VR as Tool:** Teens envision VR as a medium to manage or avoid stress and overwhelming feelings
4. **Design Agency:** As designers, teens enjoyed active interaction and iterating on designs
5. **VR Content Preferences:** Teen-designed VR consistently included nature settings and interactions with animals

### Implications for Library Services:
- **Mental Health is Youth Priority:** Demonstrates genuine teen interest in mental health support (contrary to assumptions that topics might be "taboo")
- **Nature-Based Design:** Teen-centered design revealed nature and animals as preferred content/themes
- **Creative Engagement:** Participatory design process itself was engaging and therapeutic (not just the VR product)
- **Library as Safe Space:** Public libraries perceived as appropriate, accessible sites for sensitive mental health discussions and programming

## GBLS-Specific Contributions

- **Wellness & Resilience Outcomes:** Documents games/VR as tool for mental health support and stress management—distinct from traditional learning/engagement outcomes
- **Participatory Design:** Models co-design approach centering teen voice and expertise in technology development

- **Social Equity:** Uses technology to provide mental health resources to youth who might otherwise lack access

- **Creative Production:** VR design itself becomes creative, therapeutic activity—not just content consumption

## Research Design & Methodology

- **Quasi-Experimental:** 13-session after-school programs across three sites
- **Participatory Design Approach:** Teens as active designers, not just test subjects
- **Diverse Geographic Sampling:** Urban and rural sites to capture geographic variation
- **Qualitative Data:** Interviews, observations, design artifacts
- **Location:** Public libraries in three settings

## Service Conditions & Implementation Framework

- **Skilled Facilitation:** Requires librarians trained in:
  - Participatory design methods
  - Mental health literacy and trauma-informed practice
  - VR technology
  - Youth engagement and facilitation
  
- **Mental Health Support:** Programs should be paired with actual mental health services, not positioned as treatment
- **Safe Space:** Library environment critical for fostering trust and honest discussion
- **Creative Expression:** Design process itself therapeutic; not just VR product
- **Accessibility:** Multiple ways to participate (tech-comfortable and tech-hesitant teens both engaged)
- **Sustainable Staffing:** After-school programming requires sustained staffing commitment

## Theoretical Grounding

- **Crisis Responsiveness:** Libraries stepping into mental health space in response to documented crisis
- **Participatory Justice:** Centering youth voice in designing solutions to problems affecting them
- **Technology as Therapeutic:** VR and design process as therapeutic modalities, not just entertainment

## Important Caveats & Limitations

- **Not a Therapeutic Intervention:** Authors emphasize this is NOT a mental health treatment; it's an educational opportunity and engagement program
- **Small Sample:** n=30 across three sites; limited generalizability
- **Self-Selected Participants:** Teens who elected to participate likely differ from broader teen population
- **No Long-Term Outcomes:** Does not measure sustained mental health improvements or clinical outcomes
- **Feasibility Questions:** Unclear how many libraries can support VR co-design programs with required expertise and resources
- **Missing Equity Analysis:** Limited discussion of how program served (or excluded) teens of color, LGBTQ+ teens, and other marginalized groups

## Broader Implications

- **Library Role Expansion:** Libraries increasingly responding to social crises beyond traditional information services
- **Youth Engagement:** Participatory design demonstrates power of centering youth voice
- **VR Potential:** Immersive technology can support mental health goals through both designed experiences and creative process
- **Implementation Challenges:** Feasibility and scaling questions remain; not all libraries have VR expertise/resources
- **Ethical Considerations:** Libraries stepping into mental health space must do so carefully with appropriate partnerships and boundaries

## Identified Research Gaps

- No control/comparison group; unclear if VR co-design more effective than other approaches
- No longitudinal follow-up on sustained mental health outcomes
- Limited outcome measurement beyond qualitative feedback
- Questions about scalability and resource requirements
- Unclear who benefits most (what teen characteristics predict benefit?)
- Missing equity analysis in teen participation and outcomes
