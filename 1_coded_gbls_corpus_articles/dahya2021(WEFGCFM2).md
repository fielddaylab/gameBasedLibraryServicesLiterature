## Purpose & Scope

This 2021 qualitative study examines how library patrons and librarians perceive and experience virtual reality (VR) when introduced as drop-in programming in public libraries. Conducted across seven Washington State libraries in 2018 (before COVID-19 pandemic), the research takes a critical equity perspective, investigating whether VR serves to expand access and inclusion or reproduces existing technology inequities.

Using interviews with librarians and patrons, user experience surveys, and observational field notes, the research investigates both explicit user perceptions of VR and actual experiences during library programs, framing analysis through the concept of "sociotechnical imaginaries"—culturally situated ideas about relationships between society and technology.

## Key Findings

- **Perception vs. Reality Gaps:**
  - Patrons often held idealized perceptions of VR before experiencing it
  - Actual experiences revealed both engagement possibilities and technical/experiential limitations
  - Immersion levels varied significantly based on content, user comfort, and technical execution

- **Positive Experience Markers:**
  - VR created novelty and excitement; attracted patrons to libraries
  - Immersive experiences facilitated emotional engagement and empathy
  - Informal learning opportunities emerged through experiential engagement
  - Some patrons found VR relaxing and escapist

- **Social Inequities in VR:**
  - Gender disparities in engagement and comfort with VR technology
  - Racial and ethnic representation in VR content limited; primarily White-centered narratives
  - Class barriers to access reflected in high equipment costs and limited public availability
  - Rural/urban divides in technology access
  - Disability accessibility concerns not adequately addressed

- **Critical Insight:** Libraries risk reproducing technology inequities by enthusiastically adopting cutting-edge VR without attending to barriers, representation, and inclusive design

## GBLS-Specific Contributions

- **Equity-Centered Perspective:** Models critical approach to assessing whether game/immersive technology services advance equity or replicate privilege
- **Conditional Benefits:** Explicitly demonstrates that VR benefits are context-dependent—effectiveness depends on content design, user support, and inclusive implementation
- **Service Ecology:** Shows how VR programs succeed or fail based on librarian expertise, facility conditions, and community match
- **Informal Learning:** Documents unplanned learning happening through VR exploration and social interaction
- **Sociotechnical Analysis:** Provides framework for interrogating how technology services intersect with broader social structures

## Service Conditions Identified

- **Infrastructure Requirements:** Expensive VR equipment; high facility cost barriers
- **Inclusive Design Necessity:** Content must represent diverse populations; accessibility features required
- **Community Assessment:** Library success depends on understanding local community interests and cultural contexts
- **Skilled Facilitation:** Librarians needed to support user comfort, interpret experiences, and connect to learning goals
- **Evaluation/Reflection:** Outcome assessment should examine both engagement and equity impacts

## Critical Limitations & Recommendations

- Dahya et al. emphasize that libraries should not uncritically adopt VR as solution
- Equity concerns require sustained attention: representation in content, accessibility design, and intentional inclusion of marginalized groups
- Infrastructure costs may limit access to well-funded libraries, deepening disparities
- Informal learning outcomes important but difficult to measure systematically
- Libraries need to explicitly address whether VR expands or restricts whose voices and stories are centered

## Identified Gaps

- Limited outcome data on learning or long-term impact
- Data collected pre-pandemic; unclear how shifts to online/hybrid programming affected VR adoption
- Does not examine sustained program models or cost-benefit analyses
- Relatively small sample (7 libraries); geographic specificity to Washington State
