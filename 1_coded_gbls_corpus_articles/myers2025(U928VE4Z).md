## Purpose & Scope

This 2025 study addresses a critical gap in library metadata infrastructure: the absence of standardized, patron-friendly cataloging systems for video games in public libraries. While the Library of Congress MARC format has long been the standard for library cataloging, it was developed in the 1960s for book-based materials and cannot adequately accommodate the unique descriptive needs of video games (platforms, game engines, content ratings, gameplay features, etc.).

Myers presents research on developing a user-friendly metadata schema and fillable form template specifically designed for public libraries to catalog and display video game information in ways that support both patron discovery and librarian workflow. The work combines literature review of existing game cataloging authorities, template development and testing against real game metadata, and patron/staff feedback through focus groups and interviews.

## Key Findings & Contributions

- **Accessibility Problem:** Current MARC-based systems are "visually complex and confusing to the untrained eye," creating barriers to game collection development in libraries
- **Unique Video Game Metadata:** Video games require distinct descriptive elements not present in traditional library materials (platforms, storage media, online/offline requirements, content maturity ratings, gameplay mechanics, genre specificity)
- **User-Centered Design:** Patron and staff feedback identified metadata elements most valuable for game discovery and selection
- **Practical Template:** The resulting fillable form template is intuitive and accessible for both patron and professional use
- **Standardization Need:** The work demonstrates that game collections will not scale in public libraries without standardized metadata infrastructure comparable to MARC for print materials

## GBLS-Specific Contributions

- **Collections & Access:** Provides practical tools for libraries implementing game collections
- **Discovery & Advisory:** Emphasizes patron-centered metadata that supports meaningful game selection and recommendations

## Implementation Conditions & Design Principles

- Metadata schema must accommodate games' unique technical and content attributes
- Design must be intuitive for patrons unfamiliar with specialized game cataloging vocabulary
- System must integrate with existing library infrastructure (alongside or separate from MARC)
- Professional development needed to train librarians on new system
- Ongoing feedback loops with both patrons and staff support iterative improvement

## Identified Gaps & Limitations

- Focus on public libraries; academic and school library contexts not addressed
- Implementation costs and system integration challenges not fully explored
- Limited discussion of how schema would scale across multiple library systems
- Evaluation limited to focus groups; no longitudinal data on implementation outcomes
- Question of adoption remains—schema alone insufficient without institutional commitment
