### Summary
# **Nelson, S. B., & Matthews, A. (2025). Roll for initiative: Balancing player and party in library D&D games. *Public Library Quarterly*, 1-22. https://doi.org/10.1080/01616846.2025.2522620**

Nelson and Matthews study public-library Dungeons & Dragons programs through the paired concepts of individuality and community. Their central questions are how library D&D games foster individuality and how they foster community. The article begins from the history and structure of D&D as a collaborative tabletop role-playing game, then connects play to motivation, interest development, belonging, group membership, democratic individuality, and the hybrid individual/relational self. The authors note that much library D&D literature is practical advice for librarians, so their observations and interviews add empirical evidence about what happens in library-hosted games.

The study examined D&D games hosted by two Midwestern public libraries, identified with pseudonyms. Ulfgar Public Library ran online D&D games every two weeks through Zoom breakout rooms, with four games occurring simultaneously; the researchers observed two Ulfgar sessions and interviewed nine participants. Quarion Public Library hosted in-person adult and all-ages games every two weeks, usually with two or three all-ages tables as needed; the researchers observed one adult game, three all-ages sessions, and interviewed five participants. Interviewees included players, DMs, a library director, a young adult librarian, a D&D coordinator, library staff, and one teen participant with parental consent. The authors analyzed fieldnotes and interview transcripts through qualitative coding.

The findings on individuality show that players use D&D to experiment with action, self-expression, heroism, moral choice, and character attachment. Young players sometimes acted in deliberately silly or exploratory ways, while the young adult librarian/DM worked to support expression and bring scattered players back toward collective play. Other players described or enacted spotlight moments: solving a crisis, taking a risk, achieving something dramatic, or being recognized by the party. The authors emphasize that good DMs help each player have time in the spotlight, encourage shy players, allow character subplots, and sometimes use the rule of cool to permit a memorable action even when it bends strict rules.

The findings on party and community show DMs and players treating the game as collaborative storytelling rather than as the DM's fixed plot. Interviewees described the DM as narrator, supporting cast, and facilitator, and they criticized overly rigid story control. Observations showed players helping one another in combat, assisting newer players with leveling up, voting on major decisions, consulting quieter members, and bonding not only with player characters but also with non-player characters. The authors frame this as creative teamwork: players should have fun, speak up, try new things, leave room for others, and work together to make the story.

The library setting matters in several concrete ways. First, the library provides a reliable time and place, physical or virtual, which helps campaigns continue when private friend groups might fall apart. Second, libraries provide DMs or ensure that DMs are available, which solves one of the hardest logistical problems in D&D play. Third, libraries provide resources: D&D books, Roll20 access, 3D printers for figures, staff support, gaming spaces, and related materials. Fourth, the library can function as a safer and more welcoming alternative to game stores or private groups. Participants, especially women and newer players, described library games as friendly to beginners, patient, and welcoming to women and LGBTQ+ players; library staff and spaces supplied a stable, neutral, intentional environment where players could ask for help or raise concerns.

Nelson and Matthews' strongest GBLS contribution is their account of library D&D as democratic community practice. Players want both individuality and party belonging: they want to experiment with characters, have heroic moments, make friends, and feel part of a group. The library deepens this balance by drawing participants from a broader community than family or friend networks, making games more diverse than many private or store-based D&D spaces, and sustaining interest through advertising, stable scheduling, resources, and staff facilitation. The article argues that public libraries communicate that they are spaces for gaming and spaces for everyone, keeping the figurative tavern door open for people who might otherwise be excluded or intimidated.

The article's limitations are clear: it is a small qualitative study of two public libraries, with 14 interview participants, and findings are not necessarily generalizable. Still, it gives public libraries practical implications: D&D can strengthen the library's identity as a third place and community space; it can promote print and digital game-related collections; it can connect with makerspace tools; and it can encourage libraries to learn about staff hobbies and community interests that might translate into programs beyond D&D. The authors call for future research on library-run D&D, safe spaces for all players, marginalized communities, character creation that lets patrons explore identities beyond their own, and other games or events that bring the public together.
