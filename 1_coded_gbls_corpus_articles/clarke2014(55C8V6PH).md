### Summary
# **Clarke, R. I., Lee, J. H., Jett, J., & Sacchi, S. (2014). Exploring relationships among video games. In *2014 IEEE/ACM Joint Conference on Digital Libraries* (pp. 481-482). IEEE. https://doi.org/10.1109/JCDL.2014.6970235**

Clarke, Lee, Jett, and Sacchi present a short JCDL poster on relationship metadata for video games. The poster belongs to the GAMER Group's broader work with the Seattle Interactive Media Museum to develop a user-centered metadata schema and conceptual model for video games and interactive media. Its central claim is that relationships among video games are too complex to be represented adequately by conventional bibliographic models such as FRBR, especially when games appear across platforms, editions, distribution methods, series, additional content, and influence networks.

The poster frames relationship metadata as a user-access problem. Video games are gaining cultural, economic, educational, and scholarly importance, but existing library and commercial descriptions struggle to represent game editions, re-releases, digitally distributed versions, additional content, and the relationships users care about when they search. The authors note that their earlier metadata work had produced elements and controlled vocabularies, but that test cataloging continued to expose unresolved relationship problems among games, editions, local releases, and additional content.

The method is preliminary and exploratory. The authors combined consultation with video game experts and enthusiasts with examination of sample game sets and their existing descriptive metadata. The sample sets were chosen because they had complicated relationship structures and included Shin Megami Tensei: Persona, Disgaea, Final Fantasy, Bejeweled, Civilization V, and related games and additional content. This design let the authors inspect real series, expansions, platform variants, and influence relationships rather than theorizing relationships only from abstract bibliographic models.

The poster identifies four main challenge areas. Edition relationships are difficult because video-game editions may vary by platform, source code, visual presentation, gameplay experience, physical or digital format, and local release, making print-oriented definitions of edition inadequate. Series relationships are also complex because series may contain subseries, spinoffs, shared characters, shared settings, related media, soundtracks, strategy guides, and works that eventually become their own series. Additional content raises another layer: expansions, downloadable content, mods, patches, security updates, and gameplay-altering additions need to be distinguished because they affect what a user can play or understand. Finally, influence relationships such as inspired by or based on matter to scholars, designers, and players tracing game history.

For the larger GBLS project, this poster reinforces the cataloging and discovery argument: game collections need relationship metadata that can distinguish versions, platforms, editions, series, subseries, expansions, mods, re-releases, and influence chains. Patrons may ask for a specific version, the next game in a narrative order, games connected to a universe, or a title inspired by another title. Researchers and preservationists may need to reconstruct how games, platforms, media objects, and downloadable content fit together. However, the poster is a preliminary two-page exploration rather than the strongest version of this contribution.

The source overlaps substantially with the existing Lee, Clarke, Sacchi, and Jett (2014) relationship-metadata article already summarized and incorporated into `review.md`. That fuller source provides the four grouping entities and 11 relationship types that the review currently cites. This poster should therefore be retained as provenance for the GAMER relationship-metadata thread but not added as a separate review paragraph.
