## Purpose & Scope

This 2025 quasi-experimental study examines the effectiveness of mixed reality (MR) technology for library orientation tours at a university academic library. Researchers compared two groups of first-year students (n=96 total): one group experienced a traditional librarian-led tour while the other used HoloLens 2 mixed reality devices for their tour.

The study measures three key outcomes: library anxiety (comfort/confidence navigating library resources), knowledge acquisition (understanding of library resources and organization), and student motivation (engagement, interest, effort). This addresses the practical question of whether immersive technology can improve first-year student orientation outcomes.

## Key Findings

### Mixed Reality Tour Outcomes:
- **Motivation & Engagement:** MR tours significantly enhanced students' perceived competence, interest, and effort
- **Higher Engagement:** MR provided more compelling, immersive experience than traditional tours
- **Knowledge Comparable:** No statistically significant difference in knowledge acquisition between MR and traditional tours
- **Library Anxiety:** MR did not significantly reduce library anxiety

### Traditional Tour Outcomes:
- **Better Anxiety Reduction:** Traditional, in-person librarian-led tours were more effective at reducing library anxiety
- **Social Connection:** Particularly effective for anxiety reduction related to interactions with librarians (human connection)
- **Knowledge Comparable:** Achieved same knowledge outcomes as MR without technology

### Comparative Insights:
- **Engagement vs. Anxiety:** MR and traditional tours optimize different outcomes
- **Equity Considerations:** MR training gap—some students found technology challenging or uncomfortable
- **Cost-Effectiveness Trade-off:** MR equipment costs high; benefits for knowledge/anxiety reduction not proportionally greater than traditional approaches

## GBLS-Specific Contributions

- **Immersive Technology Effectiveness:** Provides empirical evidence that XR/MR technology increases engagement but does not necessarily improve learning or anxiety reduction
- **Conditional Benefits Model:** Demonstrates that technology benefits are context-specific—effective for engagement but not universal solution
- **Service Ecology:** Shows technology success depends on combining immersive elements with human facilitation and support
- **Accessibility Considerations:** Highlights barriers (motion sickness, training complexity, cost) alongside benefits
- **Evidence-Based Evaluation:** Models rigorous outcome assessment for technology-enhanced library services

## Implementation Conditions Identified

- **Technology Infrastructure:** Significant equipment costs (HoloLens 2 devices); institutional investment required
- **Skilled Facilitation:** Users need training on technology; some barriers to adoption for less tech-confident students
- **Content Development:** MR experiences require significant planning and content creation investment
- **Accessibility Accommodations:** Must address motion sickness, visual/cognitive accessibility concerns
- **Hybrid Approach:** Best model may combine MR immersion with human librarian support for anxiety reduction

## Critical Insight: Balanced Approach

The authors argue that MR represents a "promising solution" that bridges VR's immersion with AR's accessibility, but:
- Hardware costs remain significant barrier for many institutions
- Benefits must be carefully evaluated; cost may not justify outcomes in all contexts
- Traditional approaches should not be abandoned—they excel at anxiety reduction and relationship-building
- Hybrid models (combining technology with human support) likely most effective

## Identified Limitations & Gaps

- **Single Institution:** Study at one Thai university; transferability across contexts unclear
- **Immediate Outcomes Only:** No longitudinal data on sustained knowledge retention or anxiety reduction
- **Small Sample:** n=96; limited generalizability
- **Technology Specificity:** Results specific to HoloLens 2; other MR/VR devices may perform differently
- **Cultural Context:** University context and student population may shape findings; unclear how results apply to other library types
- **Cost-Benefit Analysis Missing:** No analysis of cost per student or long-term cost-effectiveness
- **Accessibility Not Fully Addressed:** Limited discussion of accommodations for vision/hearing/motor disabilities

## Broader Implications

- Immersive technology can enhance engagement but is not a universal solution for library services
- Libraries must align technology investments with specific, measurable outcomes
- Human librarian support remains essential; technology should enhance, not replace
- Equity and accessibility must be central design considerations, not afterthought
- Careful evaluation distinguishes between engagement and learning outcomes
