### Summary
# **Virello, M. (2019). Putting the "Extra" in Extraordinary: Creating a Library Comic Con. *Children & Libraries: The Journal of the Association for Library Service to Children, 17*(1), 31-34. https://doi.org/10.5860/cal.17.1.31**

Virello offers a practitioner account of developing Southington Library ComiCon from an inherited Free Comic Book Day program into a large-scale public-library fandom event. The article begins from an access argument: commercial comic cons can be expensive, and libraries close access gaps not only for materials but also for experiences. A library comic con can therefore give children and families a low-cost way to encounter characters, stories, performances, activities, and community enthusiasm that they might otherwise miss.

The program began modestly in 2013 as a two-hour passive Free Comic Book Day event in one lower-level room, with a craft, coloring sheets, photos with a cardboard standee, and a scavenger hunt. About thirty people attended. In the following year, Virello added live-action characters through friends who cosplayed as Wolverine, Robin, Spider-Man, and Wonder Woman, and replaced passive activities with hands-on stations such as Kryptonite Disposal, Professor X's Guess, and Spidey's Web. A local comic shop donated comics, families and staff were encouraged to dress up, and attendance rose to seventy.

The article then traces how community participation, staff enthusiasm, and local connections turned the program into a full Southington Library ComiCon. Virello attended commercial comic conventions, learned from panels, exhibits, cosplayers, and performers, volunteered at a superhero day, and built relationships that later produced free publicity and program contacts. By 2015, the Free Comic Book Day program drew more than two hundred attendees and moved from the lower level to the main floor for visibility and space.

The 2016 full ComiCon required a year of planning, budgeting, organizing, brainstorming, and safety preparation. The event used an All Things Pokemon theme in response to patron interest and the rise of Pokemon Go, while also drawing on broader pop culture and library collections. Publicity included local newspapers, a Facebook event page, paid Facebook advertising, a large exterior sign, online event listings, email blasts, Facebook teasers, desk signage, superhero storytimes, and displays of superhero materials that also boosted circulation.

The article is especially useful because it makes event operations visible. Staff brainstormed around in-house strengths, built Pinterest boards, selected affordable crafts and activities, borrowed or partnered for exhibits, booked a face painter, princess sing-along, and balloon twister, solicited donations from comic shops, worked with the police department on traffic and safety, blocked off parking lots, monitored building capacity, and adapted food access with a free water tent and a local food truck. Policy mattered too: staff enforced food and dress-code rules, limited exposed skin, restricted replica weapons and projectile props, avoided overly scary costumed characters, and borrowed safety norms from other comic cons rather than inventing them from scratch.

For GBLS, the program matters because it blends game-based service with fandom, collection promotion, and community event design. Virello lists all-day activities such as scavenger hunts, coloring, video game tournaments, tabletop gaming, storytimes, crafts, Superhero Training Camp, rotating half-hour or hourly activities, costumed character interactions, displays, giveaways, and themed performances. The program gave patrons active, embodied, social experiences rather than only passive media consumption. It also shows how games can sit inside a broader pop-culture festival: video game tournaments and tabletop gaming are part of a larger constellation of comics, cosplay, character play, crafts, storytimes, performances, and collection discovery.

The outcomes are anecdotal but concrete. Attendance grew from roughly thirty people in the passive FCBD version to seventy after live characters and hands-on stations, more than two hundred after broader con-inspired redesign, and nearly nine hundred at the first official Southington Library ComiCon. Virello does not provide survey data or learning assessment, so the article should be read as an implementation case rather than impact evidence. Its value for the review is practical: large-scale fandom events require a long planning horizon, community partnerships, publicity, internal staff creativity, hands-on activity design, policy and safety planning, rotating schedules, visibility, and a willingness to connect staff passions with patron interests.
