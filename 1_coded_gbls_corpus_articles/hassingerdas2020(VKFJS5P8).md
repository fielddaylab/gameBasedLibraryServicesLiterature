### Summary
# **Hassinger-Das, B., Zosh, J. M., Hansen, N., Talarowski, M., Zmich, K., Golinkoff, R. M., & Hirsh-Pasek, K. (2020). Play-and-learn spaces: Leveraging library spaces to promote caregiver and child interaction. *Library & Information Science Research, 42*(1), 101002. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.lisr.2020.101002**

Hassinger-Das, Zosh, Hansen, Talarowski, Zmich, Golinkoff, and Hirsh-Pasek study how children's library spaces can be redesigned to promote playful learning, caregiver-child interaction, and discourse associated with language, literacy, and STEM development. The article is not about games in the narrow sense, but it is highly relevant to GBLS because it treats play as library infrastructure. It asks whether a public library's built environment can be redesigned so that families naturally engage in richer talk, movement, story creation, and shared attention during ordinary library visits.

The project responds to two linked problems. First, children spend most of their time outside formal school, so everyday spaces can help address language, reading-readiness, spatial-skill, and opportunity gaps. Second, public libraries are reimagining themselves as more than repositories for books, increasingly becoming community, activity, maker, health, and family-learning spaces. The Play-and-Learn Spaces project joined developmental science with this changing library role by embedding playful learning prompts directly into children's library areas.

The study took place in four Free Library of Philadelphia branches: three Play-and-Learn libraries and one control library. The design process included Studio Ludo, Smith Memorial Playground & Playhouse, the architecture firm DIGSAU, library staff, patrons, and community feedback sessions. Participants drew, built, painted, and discussed the kinds of play-and-learn spaces they wanted. Three major community themes emerged: more child-friendly physical spaces because children spent time after school doing homework; the library as a safe space while waiting for caregiver pickup; and more interactive activities for families with young children in the morning.

The resulting installations transformed children's areas into architectural prompts for play and learning. A climbing wall let children create words by climbing through different letters. Seating became large movable Tangram-like blocks that fit into reading nooks. A stage with magnetic play surfaces and letters invited children to create stories and engage in sociodramatic play. A perching tower offered space to climb, play, hide away, and read quietly. Each branch received a different combination of these elements.

Methodologically, the study used naturalistic observation rather than surveys or tests. Researchers compared Play-and-Learn and Non-Play-and-Learn conditions using data from pre-installation periods, a control library, and post-installation Play-and-Learn observations from December 2017 through August 2018. The sample included 72 observed groups in the Play-and-Learn condition and 83 groups in the Non-Play-and-Learn condition, with children roughly ages 1 to 10 and accompanying caregivers or child groups. Observers were trained, blind to hypotheses, and had to reach 85% agreement on interaction scoring.

The results are unusually useful for GBLS because they go beyond attendance. Children's programming attendance increased at all three Play-and-Learn branches after installation: 190% at Library A, 158% at Library B, and 201% at Library C, for an average increase of 189%, while the control library decreased during the same period. Groups also spent longer in Play-and-Learn spaces than in Non-Play-and-Learn spaces. Child interaction scores and combined caregiver/child interaction scores were significantly higher in the Play-and-Learn condition, though caregiver-only interaction scores did not reach significance.

The observed interaction quality also shifted. Children in Play-and-Learn spaces used technology less often, interacted physically with the space more frequently, and showed more laughter. Caregivers and children used more spatial-related and letter/sound-related language in the Play-and-Learn condition, while number and color language did not show the same pattern. The number of conversational turns did not increase, which the authors interpret as a change in the content and tone of conversations rather than simply more talk.

For the larger GBLS project, this source broadens the review's understanding of play. It shows that a library can design for playful learning without scheduling a formal game, buying a board game collection, or adding digital game mechanics. The service object is the space itself: movable objects, letters, climbing routes, stages, magnetic surfaces, reading nooks, and sightlines that invite children and caregivers to talk, move, laugh, imagine, and notice literacy or spatial concepts. This matters for GBLS because many game-based services depend on permissions that the room communicates before staff say anything. A children's area can tell families that play, noise, movement, and shared discovery belong in the library.

The limits are important. The study does not measure child learning outcomes directly, cannot isolate which installation elements produced which effects, does not know whether participants made repeat visits, and uses non-paired naturalistic observations rather than random assignment. Observers could see whether a Play-and-Learn space was present, even though they were blind to hypotheses. Still, the article is one of the stronger empirical sources for library play as spatial service design, especially because it links design, community input, attendance, interaction quality, language use, affect, physical engagement, and technology displacement.
