# **9th Level Games. (2024). *LSHM: TTRPG Kobolds Ate My Baby*. Google Docs.**

This short Low Stress High Mayhem handout supports a streamed GameRT session of *Kobolds Ate My Baby!* by 9th Level Games. The document gives a brief game description, links to the Twitch VOD, identifies the creator, points viewers to a how-to-play video, gathers actual-play examples, and lists play-alikes, read-alikes, watch-alikes, safety tools, GameRT links, Twitch policy, a stream outline, and a character-sheet example.

The advisory structure is the clearest feature of the item. It positions the game through adjacent tabletop titles such as *Goblin Quest*, *Paranoia*, *Mork Borg*, *Fiasco*, *Dread*, *Ten Candles*, and *Munchkin*, while the read-alikes and watch-alikes extend the session outward into fiction and online actual-play culture. This is less a rules document than a small discovery record: it helps a viewer understand the game's tone, find similar games, and connect the session to related media.

The handout also keeps safety and moderation visible. It lists *Script Change*, *The Door Is Open*, and the TTRPG Safety Toolkit, and it includes a GameRT Twitch policy that asks participants to be kind and respectful, disallows hate speech, permits moderator removal, and gives viewers harassment-reporting and private-contact options. The stream outline is spare but service-relevant: it moves from a tech check to an introduction and rules, actual play, and discussion.

For the larger GBLS project, the item is useful as a primary example of how a library-adjacent gaming community packages a streamed tabletop program. It demonstrates an advisory pattern that combines creator information, how-to-play support, actual-play references, play/read/watch-alikes, safety tools, moderation policy, and a structured event sequence. Those features may be helpful for future practical guidance on online TTRPG programming or game advisory records.

This source should remain outside the main literature review. It is not scholarship, not an assessment, and not a detailed program report. It can be retained as an excluded practitioner artifact, especially for later appendix work on advisory metadata, safety-resource bundling, or streamed game facilitation, but it should not be cited as evidence for GBLS outcomes.
