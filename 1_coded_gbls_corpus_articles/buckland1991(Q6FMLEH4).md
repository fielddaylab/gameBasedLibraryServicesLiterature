## Purpose and Scope

This seminal theoretical paper establishes a conceptual framework for understanding "information" in information science and library studies by distinguishing three principal meanings of the term and demonstrating how each relates to information systems and information work.

Buckland argues that while theorists have often dismissed or downplayed the attributive use of "information" to refer to tangible objects (documents, data, artifacts), this third meaning—*information-as-thing*—deserves careful examination because it is the only form of information with which information systems can actually deal directly. Libraries, databases, museums, and archives all handle physical information: books, data records, objects. Their purpose may be to inform users (information-as-process) or to help them acquire knowledge (information-as-knowledge), but their actual function is to store, organize, and retrieve information-as-thing.

## Key Arguments and Conceptual Framework

### Three Meanings of Information

**1. Information-as-Process:** The act of informing; the communication of knowledge or news. When someone becomes informed, their knowledge changes. This is dynamic and cognitive.

**2. Information-as-Knowledge:** That which is communicated in the process of informing—the actual knowledge, beliefs, or understanding conveyed. This is intangible; you cannot touch or directly measure it.

**3. Information-as-Thing:** Objects or artifacts—data, documents, texts, images, objects, events—that are regarded as informative because they have the quality of "imparting knowledge or communicating information." This is tangible; it has physical form.

### The Paradox of Information Systems

Buckland identifies a fundamental paradox: leading theorists of information (Wiener, Machlup) have restricted information to the first two meanings, dismissing information-as-thing as merely metaphorical. Yet *all actual information systems deal directly only with information-as-thing*. They cannot store or retrieve information-as-process or information-as-knowledge directly; they can only store and retrieve physical representations of these—documents, data, objects.

Therefore, claims about "knowledge-based" expert systems or "knowledge access" systems are technically inaccurate. What these systems actually provide is access to physical representations of knowledge.

### Information as Evidence

Rather than a tedious definitional debate, Buckland proposes examining what things people learn from. He identifies that people become informed through interaction with:
- Messages and communications (texts, signals)
- Data (processed, formatted information)
- Documents (text-bearing objects; can be extended to images, sounds, any representation)
- Objects (museum artifacts, fossils, models, specimens)
- Events (experiments that can be replicated, historical occurrences)
- Direct observation (natural phenomena)

The common thread: things people learn from can be understood as *evidence*. Information-as-thing is evidence—"the appearance from which inferences can be drawn; ground for belief; facts tending to prove or disprove some conclusion."

### Varieties of Information-as-Thing

**Data:** Plural of datum ("things given"). Information processed for use, typically stored in computational systems.

**Documents:** Text-bearing objects—letters, books, journals, manuscripts, papers. The term can be extended to include diagrams, maps, pictures, sound recordings—any artifact intended to represent meaning. Early documentalists (Paul Otlet, Suzanne Briet) argued that "document" should be a generic term for any potentially informative thing, including museum objects.

**Objects:** Non-document objects that are informative—fossils, specimens, models, buildings, artifacts. These are collected and studied because they provide evidence and learning. University collections (rock collections, herbariums, museums of artifacts, skeletons) exist because objects are information resources.

**Events:** Things from which we learn through experience or replication. Historical events, experiments (which can be repeated for verification), performances. Events themselves cannot be stored, but their effects can be (physical evidence, records, replications).

### The Challenge of Definition

Buckland explores the problem of overly narrow definitions. If we restrict "information" to only intentionally communicated, processed, meaningful things, we exclude fossils, footprints, natural phenomena—all of which are informative. If we make the definition too broad, saying anything *could* be informative in some hypothetical situation, then "information" becomes a meaningless category.

He proposes that the key is *situational* determination: whether something is informative depends on context, the inquirer's expertise, and the specific situation. Information is situational in the same way that document relevance is situational—not absolute properties of the thing itself.

### Representation and Copies

Buckland addresses copies and representations of information:
- Representations are necessarily incomplete (photographs don't show color accurately or movement; written descriptions lose details; films show only one perspective)
- Representations are made for convenience (easier to store, understand, search)
- Representations can be representations of representations, indefinitely
- Copies vary in authenticity; printed books are equally authentic copies of the same type, but manuscripts and digital records may have variations

The distinction between "types" (the original work) and "tokens" (copies) is important for understanding information systems.

## Contributions to Games-Based Library Services (GBLS)

While Buckland's work predates digital games and does not address games explicitly, it provides essential conceptual foundations relevant to GBLS:

### 1. **Games as Information-as-Thing**
Games are fundamentally *information-as-thing*—they are physical objects (board games, cards, miniatures), digital artifacts (video game files, data structures), or experienced events (live action roleplay, facilitated play). Whatever information systems (libraries, game databases, collection management systems) do with games, they are storing, organizing, retrieving, and providing access to these tangible forms.

### 2. **Multiple Information Dimensions of Games**
Games can be understood through multiple information lenses:
- As **documents**: rulebooks, design documentation, narrative texts embedded in games
- As **data**: digital games as data files, game mechanics as data structures, player data as information resources
- As **objects**: physical game components (dice, cards, boards, miniatures) as cultural artifacts worthy of collection and study
- As **events**: game play sessions, tournaments, facilitated experiences as informative events people learn from
- As **direct evidence**: experiencing a game is a form of evidence-gathering (about mechanics, design, player behavior, learning outcomes)

### 3. **Games for Learning as Information Work**
Buckland's framework helps clarify what happens in games-based learning. When a student learns from playing a game, they are:
1. Interacting with information-as-thing (the game)
2. Engaging in information-as-process (becoming informed through play)
3. Acquiring information-as-knowledge (new understanding, skills, perspectives)

The library's role is to provide access to the information-as-thing (the game) in ways that facilitate the process and knowledge acquisition. This helps distinguish between the game itself (an information resource) and the learning outcomes (which depend on how the game is facilitated and contextualized).

### 4. **Games Collections as Information Systems**
Libraries managing games collections are information storage and retrieval systems. Like museums that collect objects for evidence and learning, game collections exist to provide evidence and learning resources. This means:
- Games collections are not primarily about entertainment (though that may be incidental)
- Games are culturally and intellectually significant objects worthy of preservation and organization
- Discovery mechanisms, metadata, and curation practices matter as much for games as for traditional library materials
- Games can be studied as artifacts (design, mechanics, representations) not just played

### 5. **Metadata and Description**
Buckland's discussion of how different types of information require different organization and representation strategies is relevant to games metadata. A digital game, a board game, a LARP, and a game design document require different descriptive approaches. Metadata schema for games cannot be one-size-fits-all.

### 6. **Evidence and Assessment**
If games are understood as evidence—things from which people learn—then assessment of games-based services might focus on what evidence games provide:
- What can players infer from playing?
- What becomes visible or knowable through play that wasn't before?
- How do games function as evidence for learning outcomes, design principles, cultural meanings?

### 7. **Games as Physical and Digital Objects**
Buckland's framework accommodates both physical and digital information-as-thing, which is crucial for GBLS:
- Board games, card games, physical models = traditional objects
- Digital games, virtual worlds = information as data/digital files
- Live action roleplay = events (informative experiences)
- Hybrid games (AR, pervasive games) = combinations of multiple forms

## Gaps and Limitations for GBLS

1. **No Engagement with Play or Experience**: Buckland's framework treats information-as-thing as essentially static—objects to be studied, collections to be organized. It does not address the dynamic, experiential dimension of games as *play*. The process of playing (not just the game object) is the source of learning and information.

2. **Limited Attention to Situated Learning**: While Buckland acknowledges situationality, he focuses on the determiner (the inquirer, their expertise) rather than the full context of learning. GBLS scholarship must examine how facilitation, community, space, and pedagogical framing shape whether and how games become informative.

3. **No Consideration of Intentionality and Agency**: Games are designed objects with intentional mechanics, aesthetics, and learning goals (or lack thereof). Buckland's framework doesn't address how designer intent affects information value or how players' agency in interpreting games shapes informativeness.

4. **Representation vs. Direct Experience**: The framework emphasizes representations (descriptions, photographs, recordings) of information. But games are experiential—the information value comes from direct engagement, not representations of games. A rulebook is not the same as playing.

5. **Missing Power and Ethics Dimensions**: What counts as informative, what gets collected and preserved, whose perspectives are represented—these are not neutral organizational decisions. Contemporary frameworks would need to address equity, bias, and whose knowledge games represent.

## Relevance to GBLS Scholarship Questions

- **How should games be categorized in library systems?** Buckland's framework suggests games might be understood not as a single category but as multiple types of information-as-thing (objects, documents, events, data).

- **What makes a game "informative" in library contexts?** Situational frameworks would suggest informativeness depends on the user, the learning context, the facilitation, and what the user is seeking to learn.

- **How should games collections be organized and discovered?** Different game formats (tabletop, digital, LARP, etc.) and different purposes (entertainment, learning, research, cultural heritage) may require different organizational approaches.

- **What is the relationship between the game and learning outcomes?** Buckland's distinction between information-as-thing, information-as-process, and information-as-knowledge helps clarify that games provide information, but learning outcomes depend on additional factors (facilitation, design, user engagement).

## Methodological Notes

**Evidence_Type:** This is primarily theoretical and conceptual work. Buckland does not present empirical research but develops a conceptual framework through philosophical analysis and examination of definitions. He uses examples and historical references to illustrate concepts.

**Approach:** The paper uses reverse reasoning—instead of trying to define information a priori, Buckland asks "what are people informed by?" and works backward to characteristics of informative things. This inductive, pragmatic approach may be useful for GBLS scholarship.

**Disciplinary Grounding:** Located in information science and library studies, drawing on philosophy (semiotics, theory of signs), documentation theory, and practical considerations of information systems. The work bridges theoretical and practical concerns.
