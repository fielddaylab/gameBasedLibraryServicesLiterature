## Summary
Scott Bennett's report, commissioned by the Council on Library and Information Resources, examines how academic libraries can reimagine their physical spaces and roles in an increasingly digital world. Rather than treating the decline of print-based collections as a loss, Bennett argues that libraries have an opportunity to redefine themselves as places where community, learning, and intellectual work happen—regardless of media format.

Bennett's central argument is that libraries must move beyond the "warehousing" model of collections and space, where buildings primarily house materials and reading tables. Instead, libraries should become "learning commons" or "intellectual commons"—actively designed environments that support collaboration, conversation, teaching, learning, group work, and social interaction. Physical space becomes a service in itself, not merely an accommodation for collections.

Key themes include:
- **Rethinking library roles:** From collection custodians to facilitators of community and learning
- **Designing for social interaction:** Creating spaces that encourage conversation, collaboration, and informal learning
- **Multiple uses:** Designing flexibility so spaces can accommodate various concurrent activities (quiet study, group work, events, exhibitions)
- **Visibility and permeability:** Making libraries visibly integrated with campus/community life rather than fortified information silos
- **Space as pedagogical:** Recognizing that how space is designed teaches values and norms about knowledge, community, and acceptable uses

Bennett emphasizes that contemporary libraries must offer experiences and affordances that digital resources cannot provide—primarily the opportunity for physical gathering, informal interaction, and embodied presence with others. Buildings matter because they enable forms of sociality, serendipity, and community that matter to people.

The report draws on case studies of library renovations and new construction, historical analysis of how libraries have evolved as spaces, and theory about place, community, and learning. Bennett's recommendations are practical yet grounded in broader arguments about what libraries should aspire to do.

## Productive Incongruences and Challenges

Like Buschman & Leckie, this work does not address games or play directly. However, Bennett's argument that libraries should be designed as spaces where diverse practices can coexist—including informal learning, social interaction, exploration, and group activity—is foundational to understanding how games-based services can fit within library missions and spaces.

The report predates contemporary maker spaces, gaming areas, and digital media production spaces in libraries, but its principle that libraries should be designed to enable diverse forms of intellectual and social engagement applies directly to how spaces for games and play should be understood within libraries.

## Suggested Review Contributions

**Target Section: Libraries as Playful Spaces and Service Environments; Space and Infrastructure**

Bennett's framework for reimagining library space as actively supporting social interaction, learning, and community-building directly informs the review's discussion of libraries as playful environments. His argument that space itself is a service, and that physical design choices enable or constrain certain types of engagement, provides theoretical grounding for understanding why the design of gaming areas, seating arrangements, acoustic treatment, and access to equipment matter for games-based services.

Additionally, Bennett's emphasis that libraries must offer what digital resources cannot—embodied, social, serendipitous community experience—aligns with the review's positioning of games-based services as part of libraries' unique value proposition in supporting belonging, identity exploration, and community formation.

The report also supports the GBLS principle that games-based services require institutional investment not only in collections and programming but also in space and infrastructure design.
