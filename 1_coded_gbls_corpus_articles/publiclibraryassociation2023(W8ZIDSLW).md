### Summary
# **Public Library Association. (2023). *Public Library Services for Strong Communities Report: Results from the 2022 PLA Annual Survey*. Public Library Association.**

The Public Library Association's *Public Library Services for Strong Communities Report* is a national public-library survey report rather than a game-focused article, but it contains one of the strongest field-scale data points for GBLS: games and gaming programs are already common teen services in U.S. public libraries. PLA fielded the Public Library Services for Strong Communities Survey in fall 2022 as part of a rotating annual survey set. The survey was designed to gather better data about programs, services, partnerships, and facilities that help libraries meet community needs and sustain resilient communities.

The sampling frame included all U.S. public libraries at the administrative-entity level, as defined by IMLS. In total, 1,167 libraries completed the survey, a 12.6% response rate. PLA weighted the results to account for differences between responding libraries and all U.S. public libraries. The report organizes findings by community needs, services, programs, partnerships, facilities, evaluation, detailed results, and survey methodology.

For the GBLS review, the most important result appears in the teen-program section. The survey asked whether libraries offered specific program types for teens, defined as ages 12 to 18. Overall, 65.6% of libraries reported offering games and gaming programs for teens. That made games and gaming the second most common teen program category, behind summer reading at 86.1% and ahead of STEAM programs at 59.1%. The pattern also held across locale types, although with large capacity differences: 92.3% of city libraries, 83.3% of suburban libraries, and 58.3% of town/rural libraries reported teen games and gaming programs.

This finding is valuable because much of the GBLS literature consists of individual case reports, practitioner articles, local program descriptions, and small studies. PLA's survey shows that teen gaming programs are not marginal curiosities; they are a widely reported public-library program type. The result also helps locate GBLS inside broader youth services. Teen gaming sits beside summer reading, STEAM, teen advisory boards, book clubs, health and wellness programs, media/news literacy, financial literacy, college preparation, and after-school or homework programs.

The report also provides infrastructure context for GBLS implementation. Libraries reported an average of 15.8 program types across children's, teen, and adult/all-ages categories, with city libraries averaging 23.7 types, suburban libraries 20, and town/rural libraries 14. When asked about challenges limiting program and service offerings, 73.4% of responding libraries named staffing, 40.7% funding, 25.4% space, and 14.3% low attendance. These constraints help explain why gaming services vary across libraries and why case reports often emphasize staff interest, partnerships, equipment, room acoustics, policy, and administrative support.

Facilities data also matters for GBLS. The report found that since 2011, libraries had often added or renovated programming and meeting spaces, including public meeting rooms, age-specific programming spaces, and large community event spaces. Current spaces that can accommodate programs or events included dedicated children's spaces in 86.5% of libraries, dedicated teen spaces in 55.7%, large meeting rooms or auditoriums in 57.4%, mid-size meeting rooms in 72.7%, outdoor programming spaces in 57.1%, fixed makerspaces in 20.4%, and technology training spaces in 32.2%. Overall, 17.5% of libraries reported some form of mobile programming facility. For GBLS, these numbers clarify that games are tied to space: recurring game clubs, tournaments, VR programs, tabletop collections, and large fandom events all depend on rooms, age-specific areas, outdoor or mobile capacity, staff, and equipment.

The report's limits are important. It does not define games and gaming programs, separate video games from tabletop games or TTRPGs, report attendance for gaming programs, identify age subgroups within teen services, describe program design, or measure patron outcomes. It also does not ask about adult or all-ages games and gaming as a named program category. Therefore, it cannot tell the review what kinds of gaming programs libraries offer or whether they achieve learning, belonging, literacy, wellness, or access goals. Its best use is as national context showing that teen gaming programs are common enough to deserve systematic study and shared professional knowledge.
