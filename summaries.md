# **Baker, C. J. (2024). *Research proposal: Games-based library services study & needs assessment* [Unpublished research proposal]. School of Information Studies, University of Wisconsin-Milwaukee.**

Unique identifier: Zotero item key `R55LL85J`; Better BibTeX citation key `bakerResearchProposalGamesbased`; attachment key `PEFLFNYE`.

Baker's proposal is a field-mapping and needs-assessment design for studying games-based library services (GBLS) in public libraries across the United States. It begins from the claim that public libraries are already diversifying their media, programming, and community services, but that multiformat gaming remains under-documented despite the popularity of video games, board games, tabletop role-playing games, virtual and augmented reality games, and collectible or competitive card games. The proposal defines GBLS broadly as library experiences or offerings directly centered on modern gaming media, including programs, circulating collections, in-library use collections, displays, passive-program kits, giveaways, and related services. That definition is useful for the larger review because it frames GBLS as a service ecology rather than a single program type.

The research problem is the absence of extensive evidence about implementation metrics and community reception. Baker argues that libraries face risk in both directions: they may become less relevant if they ignore contemporary patron interests in interactive media, but they may also waste limited time and resources if they build games-based programs and collections without first assessing local demand. The proposed study therefore focuses on two dependent variables, community interest in GBLS and usage of GBLS, and two independent variables, current GBLS offerings and community or patron demographics. The central research questions ask what GBLS offerings currently exist in public libraries, whether there is demand for more, whether the presence and variety of GBLS correlate with community interest and service usage, and how patron demographics relate to interest.

Methodologically, the proposal is ambitious. It calls for two Qualtrics instruments: a staff-facing Games-based Services Implementation Questionnaire and a patron-facing Games-based Library Services Needs Assessment Survey. The staff questionnaire asks about library location, municipality size, community type, staffing, budgets, staff responsibilities, current GBLS formats, spending, funding sources, promotion, perceived popularity, implementation challenges, professional preparation, and training needs. The patron survey asks about demographics, library usage, interests in different modern gaming formats, knowledge of existing GBLS, prior participation, desired services, desired game formats, perceived importance, and future interest. The sampling plan aims at roughly 1,700 public libraries, about ten percent of U.S. public libraries, with approximately 20 patron responses from each participating library; Baker also describes a lower minimum goal of 850 libraries and 17,000 patron responses.

For analysis, the proposal emphasizes descriptive and inferential statistics. It names chi-square tests for categorical variables, t-tests for continuous variables, possible regression analysis, and visual reporting through graphs and flowcharts. Its practical orientation is important: the imagined outputs are meant to help librarians advocate for GBLS funding, decide which gaming formats to support, identify training needs, and determine whether games-based services are wanted in specific communities. The proposal also identifies limits, including response bias, imperfect generalizability, and the difficulty of representing all community contexts even with stratified random sampling.

For the GBLS project, the source is most valuable as a research-agenda artifact and an assessment instrument draft, not as outcome evidence. It provides language for why the field needs broader, more systematic data about GBLS offerings, usage, community demand, staff preparation, funding, promotion, and barriers. It also usefully connects several strands already central to the review: libraries' need to remain responsive to changing media practices; the importance of local patron needs rather than generalized enthusiasm for games; the operational reality of budgets, staffing, training, promotion, and formats; and the evidence gap around national prevalence and reception. Because it is a proposal, it should be cited cautiously: it does not yet report findings, but it offers a concrete model for the kind of survey infrastructure the GBLS literature still lacks.

The proposal also matters because it treats GBLS as simultaneously collections, programming, advisory, display work, passive programming, technology access, professional training, and community assessment. That breadth can help the review avoid reducing games in libraries to any single modality. It suggests that future GBLS research should ask not only whether a program "worked," but also what forms of GBLS exist, how libraries fund and promote them, which staff feel prepared to sustain them, what patrons know about them, and which communities actively want more.

# **Noorda, R., & Inman Berens, K. (2021). Immersive media and books 2020: New insights about book pirates, libraries and discovery, millennials, and cross-media engagement: Before and during COVID. *Publishing Research Quarterly, 37*, 227-240. https://doi.org/10.1007/s12109-021-09810-z**

Unique identifier: Zotero item key `AM8XXKKV`; Better BibTeX citation key `noordaImmersiveMediaBooks2021`; attachment key `ZRYIA5Y2`.

Noorda and Inman Berens summarize findings from *Immersive Media & Books 2020*, a large consumer-behavior survey about books, video games, TV/movies, discovery, libraries, and cross-media engagement before and during COVID-19. The study uses the term "book engagement" rather than simply reading or buying, defining it broadly as buying, borrowing, subscribing to, reading, or gifting a printed book, ebook, or audiobook in whole or in part. That definitional move is important because it treats media use as a wider ecology of practices: people discover, sample, collect, borrow, multitask, gift, display, and move across formats rather than simply consuming one medium in isolation.

The article is especially useful for GBLS because it refuses to separate books, games, and TV/film into discrete cultural silos. It describes "immersive" media as deep involvement in a topic or storyworld rather than as a state of uninterrupted attention. In this model, books, games, TV, and movies are all longform media through which audiences pursue worlds, genres, authors, franchises, and social recommendations. The survey included 4,314 respondents recruited through Qualtrics, with quotas for age, race, gender, and region aligned with U.S. Census data. Respondents had to have engaged with at least one book in the previous twelve months, and the authors note that the broader concept of engagement likely captures more activity than narrow reading measures.

Several findings matter for libraries. Book discovery is highly distributed, with recommendations from friends, favorite authors, family, and social media all playing roles, but no single method dominating. Author brand and genre are major drivers of discovery and purchase. Black and Latinx millennials appear as particularly avid book engagers, complicating industry assumptions about who constitutes the core reading public. The study also finds that book discovery is context-agnostic: people discover books in one setting and acquire or borrow them in another. Libraries are central to that ecosystem. About one-third of respondents bought a book online that they first found in a library, and about one-third bought a book in a bookstore that they first found in a library. Millennials showed even stronger cross-channel movement from library discovery to online or bookstore purchase.

The cross-media findings are the clearest bridge to GBLS. Noorda and Inman Berens report that 60.3% of respondents discover TV/movies or games through books, 61.2% discover books or games through TV/movies, and 33.1% discover books or TV/movies through games. Cross-media discovery is highest among millennials: 72.4% discover TV/movies and games through books, 72.9% discover books or games through TV/movies, and 48.1% discover books or TV/movies through games. The article therefore gives empirical backing to a claim often made in library gaming discourse: patrons do not necessarily experience books and games as separate service worlds. They move across narrative, genre, fandom, platform, and format.

For the larger GBLS project, this source supports collection development, readers' advisory, media advisory, display work, and programming that treats games as part of a connected cultural media environment. It suggests that gaming collections and programs can support book discovery and that book-centered services can also lead patrons toward games, films, and other media. It also reinforces the value of browsing and in-person discovery spaces: public library shelves remain one of the preferred discovery contexts, and library borrowing can encourage rather than cannibalize sales. For libraries, this matters because games-based services need not be justified as a departure from traditional book culture. They can be framed as an extension of how contemporary patrons already follow stories, genres, creators, and communities across media.

The article has limits for GBLS. It is not a study of game collections or game programs specifically, and it does not assess library gaming outcomes. Its emphasis is on consumer behavior around books in a larger media ecosystem. Still, that perspective is valuable because it gives GBLS a stronger audience rationale. If avid book engagers are also active across TV/film and games, then game-based library services can be understood as part of integrated cultural advisory, discovery, and access work rather than as a niche recreational add-on.

# **Watson, A. P., Batte, E., Young, B. W., & Watson, S. A. (2024). Dungeons & Dewey decimals: A D&D-style TTRPG adventure module for outreach and library skills. *College & Undergraduate Libraries, 31*(3-4), 113-129. https://doi.org/10.1080/10691316.2024.2425089**

Unique identifier: Zotero item key `4FELUX62`; Better BibTeX citation key `watsonDungeonsDeweyDecimals2024`; attachment key `BB2MPNIG`.

Watson, Batte, Young, and Watson describe the proof-of-concept creation of a custom tabletop role-playing game (TTRPG) one-shot adventure set in a fantasy version of the University of Mississippi's J.D. Williams Library. The project began from a simple but generative observation: students often get lost in a library building and need help completing tasks, which makes the library itself resemble a dungeon full of rooms, encounters, quests, tools, and guides. The authors asked whether a library could become a D&D-style adventure and whether that adventure could serve scholarly, pedagogical, outreach, or makerspace goals.

The article situates the project within two bodies of prior work: TTRPGs in libraries and gamification or game-based learning in library instruction. It notes that libraries have hosted Dungeons & Dragons and related games for decades, often as teen programming or as a way to invite patrons into the library, while professional and scholarly discussions have also addressed TTRPG collecting, cataloging, and controlled vocabularies. Separately, the article draws on game-based learning literature associated with James Paul Gee and others to argue that games can support learning through meaningful activity, challenge, feedback, and situated problem solving. The authors identify a gap between these traditions: TTRPGs have often been used for outreach or community engagement, and games have often been used for instruction, but bespoke library-centered TTRPG modules for library learning remain underexplored.

The project team included five library employees with relevant experience as game masters, TTRPG players, a map maker, and a graphic designer. They narrowed the project to a one-shot module covering the first floor of the academic library because that floor included several high-use service areas: circulation, public computers, printing and scanning, classrooms, government documents and maps, and the IDEALab makerspace. They chose a fantasy version of the library rather than a strictly contemporary one, both because it made the game materials easier to design and because it leaned into the appeal of the game form. Library spaces became fantasy equivalents: the IDEALab became the Wizard's Workshop, microforms became Scrolls of Antiquity, research and instruction librarians became Information Brokers, and library classrooms became Arcane Classrooms.

The resulting adventure turned real library tasks into quests. Players might need to find a book with a known title, locate a book on a known subject with an unknown title, scan and print a book chapter, find a newspaper article on microfilm, or make a 3D print. The authors also created a map, descriptions to be read aloud by the game master, encounters, non-player characters, enemies, a glossary, simplified Pathfinder-based character sheets, and 3D-printed miniatures. The module therefore functioned not only as a game but as a branded orientation object, makerspace demonstration, and possible small-group instruction tool.

For GBLS, the article is valuable as a concrete design case. It shows how TTRPG programming can be more than a social club or recreational event: it can scaffold library navigation, catalog use, microfilm use, printing, scanning, makerspace services, and student familiarity with specific spaces. It also makes visible the labor and expertise required. The authors emphasize that the module, map, and support materials were more time-consuming than expected; future projects might need smaller maps, simpler modules, experienced facilitators, longer timelines, and modified existing materials where permissions allow. Scheduling was also a real barrier, and the project was not playtested before publication because of illness, workload, and semester timing.

Because the module was not playtested, the article should not be treated as evidence that a library TTRPG improves skills or increases use. Its strongest contribution is as a replicable proof of concept and a design vocabulary for TTRPG-based outreach. It gives the GBLS review a detailed example of how libraries can translate service points into game spaces and common tasks into quests while using local lore, student gaming cultures, and makerspace affordances to make the library feel more navigable and inviting. It also points toward the next research need: evaluation frameworks for custom TTRPG campaigns, including possible use of Bloom's Taxonomy or Gee's learning principles.

# **Kaylor, S. L. B. (2017). *Dungeons and Dragons and literacy: The role tabletop role-playing games can play in developing teenagers' literacy skills and reading interests* [Graduate research paper, University of Northern Iowa]. UNI ScholarWorks.**

Unique identifier: Zotero item key `R29B543T`; Better BibTeX citation key `kaylorDungeonsDragonsLiteracy`; attachment key `7CKEJHKE`.

Kaylor examines how tabletop role-playing games (TRPGs), especially *Dungeons & Dragons*, may support teenagers' literacy skills and leisure reading interests. The study is framed through school library and adolescent literacy concerns: many students, including struggling and reluctant readers, need high-interest reading opportunities, freedom of choice, and nontraditional texts that connect to their interests. Kaylor argues that TRPGs combine narrative, fantasy, social interaction, rulebooks, character creation, improvisation, and problem solving in ways that may make them useful additions to a literacy "toolbox."

The study uses semi-structured interviews with seven people from central Iowa: two teenagers who were then playing TRPGs and five adults who had played TRPGs as teenagers. All seven had played *Dungeons & Dragons*, six had played *Pathfinder*, and five had played other TRPGs. Kaylor groups participants into short-term players, with two to three years of experience, and long-term players, with nine to thirty-five years of experience. The interviews asked about TRPG histories, leisure reading past and present, perceived reading difficulty, reading genres, and the effects of TRPG play on reading, writing, speaking, listening, and other skills.

The findings are modest but useful. Every participant named at least one literacy skill that TRPG play had positively affected. Speaking was the only area named by all seven participants; reading was named by six, listening by five, and writing by four. Long-term players reported broader influence across all four literacy areas, while short-term players reported narrower effects, especially speaking and reading. The reading findings are particularly important for library contexts. One participant who described himself as dyslexic said that the fun and necessity of learning the rules motivated him to read a long rulebook; others described improved visualization, prediction, use of context clues, distinguishing essential from nonessential details, fluency, and comprehension. Kaylor emphasizes that game texts mix epic narrative, technical rules, and immediate practical payoff: readers are rewarded because better comprehension makes play better.

Writing, speaking, and listening appear through the social and narrative mechanics of play. Some participants credited TRPGs with helping them elaborate descriptions, understand audience needs, build characters, pace stories, and think about motivation and dialogue. Speaking skills developed through constant table talk, in-character performance, game mastering, presentations, and confidence in social situations. Listening mattered because players had to attend to other players, remember details, negotiate conflicts, and make the game enjoyable for the group. Participants also described critical thinking, mental math, probability, cooperation, empathy, self-confidence, flexible thinking, creativity, and learning from mistakes.

Kaylor's reading-interest findings are mixed. TRPG play often increased the quantity of leisure reading, and several participants connected the fantasy settings of *Dungeons & Dragons* to fantasy novels or other speculative genres. Six of seven participants read for fun frequently or occasionally as adults. At the same time, TRPGs did not determine genre preferences in any simple way, and some participants saw little connection between gaming and reading. Educators and schools played little role in participants' gaming lives; most were introduced by friends or family, and some merely used school spaces informally. Kaylor concludes that educators, especially teacher librarians, should welcome and support TRPGs, whether by using them instructionally, showing interest in students' gaming, or providing access to expensive core rulebooks.

For the GBLS project, this source supports the literacy rationale for teen and school-library TTRPG programs. It helps explain why D&D-style programming is not just "gaming in the library" but can involve sustained reading, multimodal literacy, oral communication, collaborative storytelling, listening, writing, and social-emotional practice. It also reinforces an access argument: a single library-owned core rulebook can support an entire group of interested teens who might otherwise be priced out. The study's limitations are significant: it is exploratory, very small, geographically narrow, and based on retrospective self-report. Still, it offers vocabulary for connecting TTRPG services to adolescent literacy, reluctant-reader engagement, and school library support for youth-created narrative culture.

# **Torres-Roman, S. A., & Snow, C. E. (2014). *Dragons in the stacks: A teen librarian's guide to tabletop role-playing*. Libraries Unlimited.**

Unique identifier: Zotero item key `MMIYTC9Q`; Better BibTeX citation key `torres-romanDragonsStacksTeen`; attachment key `EBAIKMB6`.

Torres-Roman and Snow's *Dragons in the Stacks* is a professional guide for young adult librarians interested in tabletop role-playing games (RPGs). The attached PDF contains front matter, the introduction, and glossary rather than the full guide, but even this excerpt establishes the book's scope: it is meant to help librarians understand RPGs, select and collect them, catalog them, program with them, respond to objections, and choose games for teen groups. The series foreword frames gaming as an increasingly accepted library service that supports literacy, socialization, strategy, and teen engagement.

The introduction defines the book's focus as tabletop RPGs rather than live action role-playing or computer role-playing games. It explains tabletop RPGs as social, collaborative, improvised narrative games in which players create characters, inhabit roles, and work together toward goals shaped by a game master. The authors emphasize that tabletop RPGs require creative input from players in ways that differ from computer RPGs: players are not simply choosing from programmed options, but describing actions, improvising dialogue, negotiating with others, and imagining worlds together.

For GBLS, one of the most useful parts of the excerpt is the "Why Libraries?" argument. Torres-Roman and Snow describe libraries as repositories of information, education, and entertainment as well as safe, neutral community spaces for studying, reading, exploring, and discovering. They argue that libraries can become "ground zero" for local geek culture because they already offer books, movies, computers, Internet access, music, and programming, much of it free at the point of use. RPGs fit this ecology because they can be run in small meeting rooms, require only a handful of participants, and can be started with a very low initial cost compared with many other teen programs.

The authors also offer a useful facilitation principle: if everyone in the group is having a good time, the group is probably doing it right. This matters for library programming because RPG communities can be full of strong preferences, rules debates, and gatekeeping. The guide counsels librarians not to treat any single system, play style, or fan definition as authoritative. Instead, library RPG programs should prioritize inclusion, enjoyment, engagement, and participation. That framing supports GBLS work that treats facilitation and group culture as core infrastructure, not just logistics.

The table of contents indicates the guide's broader practical coverage: a brief history of tabletop RPGs, selection and collection development, cataloging, programming, and a guide to games. This makes the source especially valuable as practitioner infrastructure. It bridges several GBLS service areas that are often discussed separately: collecting RPG books and materials, describing and cataloging them, running teen programs, making the case to administrators or concerned adults, and matching games to local groups. The guide also includes genre tags, resources, and evaluative criteria for RPG reviews, including accessibility, support, versatility, and genre emulation.

Because the available PDF is an excerpt, this summary should not overstate detailed arguments from chapters that are not present in the attachment. Still, the source is clearly important to the GBLS review as an early professional handbook for RPG services in teen librarianship. It positions tabletop RPGs as affordable, literacy-rich, socially engaging programs that align with library missions and teen service goals. It also helps explain why RPG work belongs in a review of game-based library services not only as programming, but also as collection development, cataloging, advisory, and inclusive facilitation.

# **Sanders, E. R. (1982). Dungeons and Dragons in a public library. *Indiana Libraries, 2*(3), 93-98.**

Unique identifier: Zotero item key `MPX3T4U5`; Better BibTeX citation key `sandersDungeonsDragonsPublic`; attachment key `H73CBZ75`.

Sanders offers one of the early practitioner accounts of Dungeons & Dragons programming in a public library. Writing from Michigan City Public Library, he describes a D&D club that had been running for nearly a year and remained popular with young adults from grade school through college sophomores despite mixed local media attention. The article is brief, but it is valuable because it captures several issues that still recur in GBLS work: how to reach teens, how to organize a game club, how to rely on peer expertise, how to respond to public concern, and how to understand game play as imaginative story creation rather than passive entertainment.

The club began after Sanders heard from another librarian that young adults were highly interested in D&D and that libraries could draw strong attendance by hosting it. He placed a newspaper release asking interested players to come to the library to discuss forming a club, and twenty young adults attended the first meeting. Rather than lecture about the game, the group decided that the best way to learn was to play. At the next meeting, the room was arranged with tables for multiple groups, and more experienced players served as dungeon masters for new, intermediate, and advanced players. This peer-led structure is important: the librarian did not need to be the sole expert, and the program drew on the knowledge and enthusiasm already present in the youth community.

The program also shows how game duration and library scheduling shape service design. Because sessions lasted about four hours, the library held them on Sundays. When monthly meetings were not enough for some players, Sanders and the dungeon masters encouraged participants to use the library sessions as a place to meet new players and form home groups. The club eventually grew to nearly fifty players. It also hosted a tournament, with prizes donated by a regional game store. Forty young adults were lined up outside the library before opening on the day of the tournament, and the event generated local news coverage and public curiosity.

The article is especially useful for understanding controversy and advocacy. A local editorial criticized the game as potentially obsessive, financially entrapping, and associated with demonic or deceptive thinking. Sanders reports that the immediate effect on the club was limited, though public schools removed library flyers advertising meeting dates. He responded to concerned parents by explaining the game as fantasy adventure, comparable in risk to reading fantasy literature or watching adventure films. He also cites favorable public commentary from educators who described D&D as collaborative, mentally challenging, and potentially useful for working through conflicts. This makes the article an early example of a librarian defending a game-based service by translating its practices into educational and developmental language.

For GBLS, Sanders's strongest contribution is historical and conceptual. The article shows that public libraries were already experimenting with role-playing game clubs in the early 1980s and that many of the core issues remain recognizable: teen outreach, community service, adult concern, peer facilitation, publicity, tournaments, and game-based social space. Sanders concludes that the club gave young adults an outlet for interest, a place to learn the game, and a reason to come to the library. His final point is especially resonant for the review: instead of using the library primarily to read materials, young people used it as a place where they created their own stories.

The limitations are those of a practitioner report. Sanders does not present systematic assessment, demographics beyond rough age range, or learning outcomes. The article also reflects the language and assumptions of its moment. Still, it is important as an early GBLS precedent and as evidence that D&D programming in libraries has long been tied to teen participation, imagination, community controversy, and library identity as a space for collaborative creation.
