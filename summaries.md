# **Wedlake, S., Coward, C., & Lee, J. H. (2024). How games can support misinformation education: A sociocultural perspective. *Journal of the Association for Information Science and Technology, 75*(13), 1480–1497. https://doi.org/10.1002/asi.24954**

Unique identifier: Zotero item key `HUVU4YGP`; Better BibTeX citation key `wedlakeHowGamesCan2024`; attachment key `KYACJU4W`.

Wedlake, Coward, and Lee extend prior work on *The Euphorigen Investigation*, an online misinformation escape room hosted by public libraries. The article asks how games can support misinformation education when viewed through a sociocultural perspective on media and information literacy. This theoretical lens matters because it shifts attention away from literacy as an individual checklist of cognitive skills and toward literacy as embedded in everyday practices, social contexts, platform infrastructures, emotional responses, and cultural meaning-making. The study examines 53 game sessions hosted by 10 public libraries, involving 211 players. Data came from post-game debrief recordings, player surveys, librarian session reports, and librarian focus groups.

The game places players in a fictional misinformation scenario about Euphorigen, a brain-boosting supplement that may be added to the public water supply. Players encounter misleading headlines, manipulated charts, social media bots, deepfake images, and deepfake videos. The game is designed to make players inadvertently share a deepfake video and then confront that mistake in the debrief. This design is central to the article’s contribution. The authors intentionally avoided a narrow skills-building intervention and instead created a collaborative narrative experience that makes players feel uncertainty, vulnerability, and emotional investment. The public-library context also mattered: participation was voluntary, so the game had to be enjoyable and appealing rather than a disguised lecture.

The findings show that the game supported three kinds of learning. First, players engaged in infrastructural meaning-making by understanding how social media platforms and AI-generated content can support the spread of misinformation. Second, players reflected on psychological and emotional dimensions of misinformation, including vulnerability, shock, embarrassment, skepticism, cynicism, and the feeling that online information may be impossible to verify alone. Third, players connected their own online behaviors to the broader information environment, recognizing that even small acts of sharing or passive platform participation can matter. The article is especially strong on debriefing: the debrief is described as the moment when the game experience is turned into learning, and librarians’ facilitation is critical for steering players away from cynicism and toward reflective agency.

For GBLS, this article is one of the strongest sources for game-based information literacy because it combines program design, library implementation, theory, and assessment. It strengthens the claim that games can do more than teach discrete skills. They can create shared experiences through which patrons rehearse judgment, discuss vulnerability, and connect fictional experiences to real-world information practices. It also sharpens the role of collaboration. The authors note that most misinformation games are single-player, even though misinformation is a social-media-fueled problem. Their escape-room model makes misinformation education collaborative: players solve together, fail together, debrief together, and learn from one another’s examples and contexts.

The article also provides implementation cautions. Because the study ran in real public libraries, the authors gave up some control over recruitment, facilitation, and participant demographics. Librarians varied in personality, facilitation skill, and relationships with players. The study did not include delayed follow-up, so it cannot show long-term behavior change. Some players concluded that they did not need to change their behavior because they already avoided politics, rarely shared online, or only used social media for family or entertainment. For future GBLS research, the article points toward longitudinal studies, targeted narratives for specific communities, co-design across cultural and linguistic contexts, and research on how librarian facilitation shapes outcomes.
