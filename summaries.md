# **Baker, C. J. (2024). *Research proposal: Games-based library services study & needs assessment* [Unpublished research proposal]. School of Information Studies, University of Wisconsin-Milwaukee.**

Unique identifier: Zotero item key `R55LL85J`; Better BibTeX citation key `bakerResearchProposalGamesbased`; attachment key `PEFLFNYE`.

Baker's proposal is a field-mapping and needs-assessment design for studying games-based library services (GBLS) in public libraries across the United States. It begins from the claim that public libraries are already diversifying their media, programming, and community services, but that multiformat gaming remains under-documented despite the popularity of video games, board games, tabletop role-playing games, virtual and augmented reality games, and collectible or competitive card games. The proposal defines GBLS broadly as library experiences or offerings directly centered on modern gaming media, including programs, circulating collections, in-library use collections, displays, passive-program kits, giveaways, and related services. That definition is useful for the larger review because it frames GBLS as a service ecology rather than a single program type.

The research problem is the absence of extensive evidence about implementation metrics and community reception. Baker argues that libraries face risk in both directions: they may become less relevant if they ignore contemporary patron interests in interactive media, but they may also waste limited time and resources if they build games-based programs and collections without first assessing local demand. The proposed study therefore focuses on two dependent variables, community interest in GBLS and usage of GBLS, and two independent variables, current GBLS offerings and community or patron demographics. The central research questions ask what GBLS offerings currently exist in public libraries, whether there is demand for more, whether the presence and variety of GBLS correlate with community interest and service usage, and how patron demographics relate to interest.

Methodologically, the proposal is ambitious. It calls for two Qualtrics instruments: a staff-facing Games-based Services Implementation Questionnaire and a patron-facing Games-based Library Services Needs Assessment Survey. The staff questionnaire asks about library location, municipality size, community type, staffing, budgets, staff responsibilities, current GBLS formats, spending, funding sources, promotion, perceived popularity, implementation challenges, professional preparation, and training needs. The patron survey asks about demographics, library usage, interests in different modern gaming formats, knowledge of existing GBLS, prior participation, desired services, desired game formats, perceived importance, and future interest. The sampling plan aims at roughly 1,700 public libraries, about ten percent of U.S. public libraries, with approximately 20 patron responses from each participating library; Baker also describes a lower minimum goal of 850 libraries and 17,000 patron responses.

For analysis, the proposal emphasizes descriptive and inferential statistics. It names chi-square tests for categorical variables, t-tests for continuous variables, possible regression analysis, and visual reporting through graphs and flowcharts. Its practical orientation is important: the imagined outputs are meant to help librarians advocate for GBLS funding, decide which gaming formats to support, identify training needs, and determine whether games-based services are wanted in specific communities. The proposal also identifies limits, including response bias, imperfect generalizability, and the difficulty of representing all community contexts even with stratified random sampling.

For the GBLS project, the source is most valuable as a research-agenda artifact and an assessment instrument draft, not as outcome evidence. It provides language for why the field needs broader, more systematic data about GBLS offerings, usage, community demand, staff preparation, funding, promotion, and barriers. It also usefully connects several strands already central to the review: libraries' need to remain responsive to changing media practices; the importance of local patron needs rather than generalized enthusiasm for games; the operational reality of budgets, staffing, training, promotion, and formats; and the evidence gap around national prevalence and reception. Because it is a proposal, it should be cited cautiously: it does not yet report findings, but it offers a concrete model for the kind of survey infrastructure the GBLS literature still lacks.

The proposal also matters because it treats GBLS as simultaneously collections, programming, advisory, display work, passive programming, technology access, professional training, and community assessment. That breadth can help the review avoid reducing games in libraries to any single modality. It suggests that future GBLS research should ask not only whether a program "worked," but also what forms of GBLS exist, how libraries fund and promote them, which staff feel prepared to sustain them, what patrons know about them, and which communities actively want more.

# **Noorda, R., & Inman Berens, K. (2021). Immersive media and books 2020: New insights about book pirates, libraries and discovery, millennials, and cross-media engagement: Before and during COVID. *Publishing Research Quarterly, 37*, 227-240. https://doi.org/10.1007/s12109-021-09810-z**

Unique identifier: Zotero item key `AM8XXKKV`; Better BibTeX citation key `noordaImmersiveMediaBooks2021`; attachment key `ZRYIA5Y2`.

Noorda and Inman Berens summarize findings from *Immersive Media & Books 2020*, a large consumer-behavior survey about books, video games, TV/movies, discovery, libraries, and cross-media engagement before and during COVID-19. The study uses the term "book engagement" rather than simply reading or buying, defining it broadly as buying, borrowing, subscribing to, reading, or gifting a printed book, ebook, or audiobook in whole or in part. That definitional move is important because it treats media use as a wider ecology of practices: people discover, sample, collect, borrow, multitask, gift, display, and move across formats rather than simply consuming one medium in isolation.

The article is especially useful for GBLS because it refuses to separate books, games, and TV/film into discrete cultural silos. It describes "immersive" media as deep involvement in a topic or storyworld rather than as a state of uninterrupted attention. In this model, books, games, TV, and movies are all longform media through which audiences pursue worlds, genres, authors, franchises, and social recommendations. The survey included 4,314 respondents recruited through Qualtrics, with quotas for age, race, gender, and region aligned with U.S. Census data. Respondents had to have engaged with at least one book in the previous twelve months, and the authors note that the broader concept of engagement likely captures more activity than narrow reading measures.

Several findings matter for libraries. Book discovery is highly distributed, with recommendations from friends, favorite authors, family, and social media all playing roles, but no single method dominating. Author brand and genre are major drivers of discovery and purchase. Black and Latinx millennials appear as particularly avid book engagers, complicating industry assumptions about who constitutes the core reading public. The study also finds that book discovery is context-agnostic: people discover books in one setting and acquire or borrow them in another. Libraries are central to that ecosystem. About one-third of respondents bought a book online that they first found in a library, and about one-third bought a book in a bookstore that they first found in a library. Millennials showed even stronger cross-channel movement from library discovery to online or bookstore purchase.

The cross-media findings are the clearest bridge to GBLS. Noorda and Inman Berens report that 60.3% of respondents discover TV/movies or games through books, 61.2% discover books or games through TV/movies, and 33.1% discover books or TV/movies through games. Cross-media discovery is highest among millennials: 72.4% discover TV/movies and games through books, 72.9% discover books or games through TV/movies, and 48.1% discover books or TV/movies through games. The article therefore gives empirical backing to a claim often made in library gaming discourse: patrons do not necessarily experience books and games as separate service worlds. They move across narrative, genre, fandom, platform, and format.

For the larger GBLS project, this source supports collection development, readers' advisory, media advisory, display work, and programming that treats games as part of a connected cultural media environment. It suggests that gaming collections and programs can support book discovery and that book-centered services can also lead patrons toward games, films, and other media. It also reinforces the value of browsing and in-person discovery spaces: public library shelves remain one of the preferred discovery contexts, and library borrowing can encourage rather than cannibalize sales. For libraries, this matters because games-based services need not be justified as a departure from traditional book culture. They can be framed as an extension of how contemporary patrons already follow stories, genres, creators, and communities across media.

The article has limits for GBLS. It is not a study of game collections or game programs specifically, and it does not assess library gaming outcomes. Its emphasis is on consumer behavior around books in a larger media ecosystem. Still, that perspective is valuable because it gives GBLS a stronger audience rationale. If avid book engagers are also active across TV/film and games, then game-based library services can be understood as part of integrated cultural advisory, discovery, and access work rather than as a niche recreational add-on.

# **Watson, A. P., Batte, E., Young, B. W., & Watson, S. A. (2024). Dungeons & Dewey decimals: A D&D-style TTRPG adventure module for outreach and library skills. *College & Undergraduate Libraries, 31*(3-4), 113-129. https://doi.org/10.1080/10691316.2024.2425089**

Unique identifier: Zotero item key `4FELUX62`; Better BibTeX citation key `watsonDungeonsDeweyDecimals2024`; attachment key `BB2MPNIG`.

Watson, Batte, Young, and Watson describe the proof-of-concept creation of a custom tabletop role-playing game (TTRPG) one-shot adventure set in a fantasy version of the University of Mississippi's J.D. Williams Library. The project began from a simple but generative observation: students often get lost in a library building and need help completing tasks, which makes the library itself resemble a dungeon full of rooms, encounters, quests, tools, and guides. The authors asked whether a library could become a D&D-style adventure and whether that adventure could serve scholarly, pedagogical, outreach, or makerspace goals.

The article situates the project within two bodies of prior work: TTRPGs in libraries and gamification or game-based learning in library instruction. It notes that libraries have hosted Dungeons & Dragons and related games for decades, often as teen programming or as a way to invite patrons into the library, while professional and scholarly discussions have also addressed TTRPG collecting, cataloging, and controlled vocabularies. Separately, the article draws on game-based learning literature associated with James Paul Gee and others to argue that games can support learning through meaningful activity, challenge, feedback, and situated problem solving. The authors identify a gap between these traditions: TTRPGs have often been used for outreach or community engagement, and games have often been used for instruction, but bespoke library-centered TTRPG modules for library learning remain underexplored.

The project team included five library employees with relevant experience as game masters, TTRPG players, a map maker, and a graphic designer. They narrowed the project to a one-shot module covering the first floor of the academic library because that floor included several high-use service areas: circulation, public computers, printing and scanning, classrooms, government documents and maps, and the IDEALab makerspace. They chose a fantasy version of the library rather than a strictly contemporary one, both because it made the game materials easier to design and because it leaned into the appeal of the game form. Library spaces became fantasy equivalents: the IDEALab became the Wizard's Workshop, microforms became Scrolls of Antiquity, research and instruction librarians became Information Brokers, and library classrooms became Arcane Classrooms.

The resulting adventure turned real library tasks into quests. Players might need to find a book with a known title, locate a book on a known subject with an unknown title, scan and print a book chapter, find a newspaper article on microfilm, or make a 3D print. The authors also created a map, descriptions to be read aloud by the game master, encounters, non-player characters, enemies, a glossary, simplified Pathfinder-based character sheets, and 3D-printed miniatures. The module therefore functioned not only as a game but as a branded orientation object, makerspace demonstration, and possible small-group instruction tool.

For GBLS, the article is valuable as a concrete design case. It shows how TTRPG programming can be more than a social club or recreational event: it can scaffold library navigation, catalog use, microfilm use, printing, scanning, makerspace services, and student familiarity with specific spaces. It also makes visible the labor and expertise required. The authors emphasize that the module, map, and support materials were more time-consuming than expected; future projects might need smaller maps, simpler modules, experienced facilitators, longer timelines, and modified existing materials where permissions allow. Scheduling was also a real barrier, and the project was not playtested before publication because of illness, workload, and semester timing.

Because the module was not playtested, the article should not be treated as evidence that a library TTRPG improves skills or increases use. Its strongest contribution is as a replicable proof of concept and a design vocabulary for TTRPG-based outreach. It gives the GBLS review a detailed example of how libraries can translate service points into game spaces and common tasks into quests while using local lore, student gaming cultures, and makerspace affordances to make the library feel more navigable and inviting. It also points toward the next research need: evaluation frameworks for custom TTRPG campaigns, including possible use of Bloom's Taxonomy or Gee's learning principles.

# **Kaylor, S. L. B. (2017). *Dungeons and Dragons and literacy: The role tabletop role-playing games can play in developing teenagers' literacy skills and reading interests* [Graduate research paper, University of Northern Iowa]. UNI ScholarWorks.**

Unique identifier: Zotero item key `R29B543T`; Better BibTeX citation key `kaylorDungeonsDragonsLiteracy`; attachment key `7CKEJHKE`.

Kaylor examines how tabletop role-playing games (TRPGs), especially *Dungeons & Dragons*, may support teenagers' literacy skills and leisure reading interests. The study is framed through school library and adolescent literacy concerns: many students, including struggling and reluctant readers, need high-interest reading opportunities, freedom of choice, and nontraditional texts that connect to their interests. Kaylor argues that TRPGs combine narrative, fantasy, social interaction, rulebooks, character creation, improvisation, and problem solving in ways that may make them useful additions to a literacy "toolbox."

The study uses semi-structured interviews with seven people from central Iowa: two teenagers who were then playing TRPGs and five adults who had played TRPGs as teenagers. All seven had played *Dungeons & Dragons*, six had played *Pathfinder*, and five had played other TRPGs. Kaylor groups participants into short-term players, with two to three years of experience, and long-term players, with nine to thirty-five years of experience. The interviews asked about TRPG histories, leisure reading past and present, perceived reading difficulty, reading genres, and the effects of TRPG play on reading, writing, speaking, listening, and other skills.

The findings are modest but useful. Every participant named at least one literacy skill that TRPG play had positively affected. Speaking was the only area named by all seven participants; reading was named by six, listening by five, and writing by four. Long-term players reported broader influence across all four literacy areas, while short-term players reported narrower effects, especially speaking and reading. The reading findings are particularly important for library contexts. One participant who described himself as dyslexic said that the fun and necessity of learning the rules motivated him to read a long rulebook; others described improved visualization, prediction, use of context clues, distinguishing essential from nonessential details, fluency, and comprehension. Kaylor emphasizes that game texts mix epic narrative, technical rules, and immediate practical payoff: readers are rewarded because better comprehension makes play better.

Writing, speaking, and listening appear through the social and narrative mechanics of play. Some participants credited TRPGs with helping them elaborate descriptions, understand audience needs, build characters, pace stories, and think about motivation and dialogue. Speaking skills developed through constant table talk, in-character performance, game mastering, presentations, and confidence in social situations. Listening mattered because players had to attend to other players, remember details, negotiate conflicts, and make the game enjoyable for the group. Participants also described critical thinking, mental math, probability, cooperation, empathy, self-confidence, flexible thinking, creativity, and learning from mistakes.

Kaylor's reading-interest findings are mixed. TRPG play often increased the quantity of leisure reading, and several participants connected the fantasy settings of *Dungeons & Dragons* to fantasy novels or other speculative genres. Six of seven participants read for fun frequently or occasionally as adults. At the same time, TRPGs did not determine genre preferences in any simple way, and some participants saw little connection between gaming and reading. Educators and schools played little role in participants' gaming lives; most were introduced by friends or family, and some merely used school spaces informally. Kaylor concludes that educators, especially teacher librarians, should welcome and support TRPGs, whether by using them instructionally, showing interest in students' gaming, or providing access to expensive core rulebooks.

For the GBLS project, this source supports the literacy rationale for teen and school-library TTRPG programs. It helps explain why D&D-style programming is not just "gaming in the library" but can involve sustained reading, multimodal literacy, oral communication, collaborative storytelling, listening, writing, and social-emotional practice. It also reinforces an access argument: a single library-owned core rulebook can support an entire group of interested teens who might otherwise be priced out. The study's limitations are significant: it is exploratory, very small, geographically narrow, and based on retrospective self-report. Still, it offers vocabulary for connecting TTRPG services to adolescent literacy, reluctant-reader engagement, and school library support for youth-created narrative culture.

# **Sanders, E. R. (1982). Dungeons and Dragons in a public library. *Indiana Libraries, 2*(3), 93-98.**

Unique identifier: Zotero item key `MPX3T4U5`; Better BibTeX citation key `sandersDungeonsDragonsPublic`; attachment key `H73CBZ75`.

Sanders offers one of the early practitioner accounts of Dungeons & Dragons programming in a public library. Writing from Michigan City Public Library, he describes a D&D club that had been running for nearly a year and remained popular with young adults from grade school through college sophomores despite mixed local media attention. The article is brief, but it is valuable because it captures several issues that still recur in GBLS work: how to reach teens, how to organize a game club, how to rely on peer expertise, how to respond to public concern, and how to understand game play as imaginative story creation rather than passive entertainment.

The club began after Sanders heard from another librarian that young adults were highly interested in D&D and that libraries could draw strong attendance by hosting it. He placed a newspaper release asking interested players to come to the library to discuss forming a club, and twenty young adults attended the first meeting. Rather than lecture about the game, the group decided that the best way to learn was to play. At the next meeting, the room was arranged with tables for multiple groups, and more experienced players served as dungeon masters for new, intermediate, and advanced players. This peer-led structure is important: the librarian did not need to be the sole expert, and the program drew on the knowledge and enthusiasm already present in the youth community.

The program also shows how game duration and library scheduling shape service design. Because sessions lasted about four hours, the library held them on Sundays. When monthly meetings were not enough for some players, Sanders and the dungeon masters encouraged participants to use the library sessions as a place to meet new players and form home groups. The club eventually grew to nearly fifty players. It also hosted a tournament, with prizes donated by a regional game store. Forty young adults were lined up outside the library before opening on the day of the tournament, and the event generated local news coverage and public curiosity.

The article is especially useful for understanding controversy and advocacy. A local editorial criticized the game as potentially obsessive, financially entrapping, and associated with demonic or deceptive thinking. Sanders reports that the immediate effect on the club was limited, though public schools removed library flyers advertising meeting dates. He responded to concerned parents by explaining the game as fantasy adventure, comparable in risk to reading fantasy literature or watching adventure films. He also cites favorable public commentary from educators who described D&D as collaborative, mentally challenging, and potentially useful for working through conflicts. This makes the article an early example of a librarian defending a game-based service by translating its practices into educational and developmental language.

For GBLS, Sanders's strongest contribution is historical and conceptual. The article shows that public libraries were already experimenting with role-playing game clubs in the early 1980s and that many of the core issues remain recognizable: teen outreach, community service, adult concern, peer facilitation, publicity, tournaments, and game-based social space. Sanders concludes that the club gave young adults an outlet for interest, a place to learn the game, and a reason to come to the library. His final point is especially resonant for the review: instead of using the library primarily to read materials, young people used it as a place where they created their own stories.

The limitations are those of a practitioner report. Sanders does not present systematic assessment, demographics beyond rough age range, or learning outcomes. The article also reflects the language and assumptions of its moment. Still, it is important as an early GBLS precedent and as evidence that D&D programming in libraries has long been tied to teen participation, imagination, community controversy, and library identity as a space for collaborative creation.

# **Cho, Y., Coward, C., Lackner, J., Windleharth, T. W., & Lee, J. H. (2023). The use of an escape room as an immersive learning environment for building resilience to misinformation. *Journal of Librarianship and Information Science, 57*(2), 524-538. https://doi.org/10.1177/09610006231208027**

Unique identifier: Zotero item key `9VKUZV88`; Better BibTeX citation key `choUseEscapeRoom2023`; attachment key `4Y2Q7E9P`.

Cho, Coward, Lackner, Windleharth, and Lee report an exploratory pilot study of *The Euphorigen Investigation*, a misinformation escape room developed for public-library settings. The article begins from a critique of conventional information literacy instruction: misinformation is not only a skills deficit that can be solved through checklists, because belief and sharing are also shaped by emotion, cognitive bias, personal identity, social media design, and community trust. The authors therefore test whether an immersive, collaborative game can make players experience the dynamics of misinformation in ways that more direct instruction may not.

The escape room was designed collaboratively with input from misinformation literature, librarians who had developed or delivered escape rooms, students, researchers, and a professional escape room developer. The designers intentionally avoided a didactic skills-building format and instead built puzzles around vulnerability, confirmation bias, emotional triggers, misleading headlines, manipulated charts, deepfake images and videos, social media bots, and influencers. They also avoided trigger terms such as "fake news" and "misinformation" in promotion because prior librarian interviews suggested those terms could repel some of the audiences most likely to benefit. The fictional scenario asks players to investigate Euphorigen, a brain-boosting supplement being considered for public water supplies. The game culminates when players unwittingly share a video that is later revealed to be a deepfake.

The study took place in five Washington public-library systems from February to March 2021. Librarians, not researchers, hosted the games, led the debrief discussions, and submitted session data after receiving project, gameplay, game-host, and debrief training. The game experience included 45-60 minutes of collaborative online play, a 15-20 minute librarian-led debrief, and an optional participant survey. Across 17 sessions, 80 participants played in teams of three to six; 50 completed usable surveys. Data included debrief recordings, surveys, librarian session reports, and a focus group with six librarians.

The findings are encouraging but appropriately limited. Debrief analysis suggested that the game increased or reinforced awareness of misinformation tactics, especially deepfake images and videos. Players discussed misleading headlines, manipulated charts, bots, computer-generated images, and their own strategies for checking information. The game also prompted reflection on vulnerability: players often began with suspicion toward the fictional company and missed early evidence that challenged their expectations, illustrating confirmation bias. The deepfake reveal created a designed moment of failure, making players confront how easily sharing can spread misinformation. Participants then reflected on how difficult it is to "put the toothpaste back in the tube" once misinformation circulates online.

For GBLS, the most important contribution is the combination of immersive experience, collaborative play, and librarian-led debriefing. The game gave players a safe place to fail, a shared fictional context for discussing a polarizing topic, and a social structure in which players could contribute different observations. The debrief was not a minor add-on; the authors argue it transformed subjective game experience into transferable learning. Librarians used their trusted institutional role, local relationships, and facilitation skills to keep discussions civil, probe productively, invite quieter players, and connect the game to real information practices. The article therefore makes a strong case that facilitation is core infrastructure for game-based library learning.

The article's limitations are also important for the review. The study was exploratory, used a prototype, included a small survey sample, and took place only in Washington State. The authors do not make generalizable statistical claims. Still, the study offers one of the strongest empirical examples in the GBLS literature because it examines real public-library implementation, player reflection, librarian experience, and design implications. It supports the argument that escape rooms can do more than entertain or teach discrete search skills: they can create emotionally memorable, collaborative, reflective learning environments for difficult civic information problems.

Wedlake, Coward, and Lee extend the public-library misinformation game work represented by *The Euphorigen Investigation*, but they frame the project through a sociocultural perspective on media and information literacy. The article argues that misinformation education should not be treated only as an "autonomous" set of individual evaluation skills. Instead, belief, sharing, and interpretation are situated in social identities, platform infrastructures, communities, emotions, and everyday information practices. From that perspective, games can matter because they create shared situations in which people experience misinformation dynamics together, talk about them, and reflect on how their own practices fit larger systems.

The study examines *The Euphorigen Investigation*, an online escape-room game about a fictional brain-enhancing supplement, across a much larger public-library implementation than the earlier pilot. The game places players in a narrative investigation where they encounter misleading headlines, bots, manipulated charts, deepfakes, influencer content, and other misinformation tactics. The learning design depends on collaboration and a librarian-facilitated debrief, not on puzzles alone. The debrief helps players connect the fictional experience to real online environments, their own assumptions, and the social consequences of sharing.

The authors use multiple data sources, including debrief recordings, player surveys, librarian session reports, and librarian focus groups. They analyze how players and facilitators made meaning from the game and ask how game attributes supported misinformation education. The empirical scope makes the article especially useful for GBLS because it studies an actual library-hosted program across many sessions rather than presenting only a design concept or small demonstration.

Three game attributes are central to the findings: narrative, collaboration, and debriefing. Narrative gave misinformation a context that felt less abstract than a checklist or lecture. Players could see how emotional stakes, uncertainty, suspicion, and urgency affected their own judgments. Collaboration mattered because players brought different observations, assumptions, and online experiences to the table, turning misinformation evaluation into a social process. The debrief was the clearest bridge to transfer. It gave players a structured opportunity to name what happened, compare interpretations, revisit their vulnerability to confirmation bias, discuss platform features, and think about future behavior.

The findings show that the game supported reflection on several dimensions of misinformation. Players discussed platform and media infrastructures, including how bots, influencers, social media metrics, and manipulated media can shape attention and trust. They also discussed psychological and emotional dimensions, including confidence, surprise, suspicion, embarrassment, and concern about being fooled. Survey responses suggested that many players found the game helpful, and 73.23% reported being more concerned about misinformation in society after playing. Importantly, the authors do not claim that the game inoculated participants or produced durable behavioral change. The stronger claim is that it created a memorable, social, reflective learning environment aligned with the sociocultural character of misinformation.

For the GBLS project, this article is a key source because it makes the library role visible in a way that many game-based learning studies do not. Libraries are not merely venues for a game designed elsewhere. Librarians host sessions, prepare participants, manage group interaction, lead debriefs, and translate game experience into local information-literacy conversation. The article therefore supports a service model in which game-based library services require facilitation, staff training, program design, and assessment practices that look beyond attendance. It also helps connect escape rooms to civic information literacy, misinformation resilience, and public-library trust.

The article's limitations are useful for the review as well. The study relies heavily on self-report and immediate reflection, and it does not establish long-term learning or behavior change. The intervention also required a purpose-built game, librarian training, and coordinated implementation, which may be challenging for smaller libraries. Even so, Wedlake et al. provide one of the strongest empirical and theoretical accounts of how a library-based game can address a difficult public problem by combining narrative experience, social play, and reflective facilitation.

# **Lee, K. J., King, W. E., Dahya, N., & Lee, J. H. (2020). Librarian perspectives on the role of virtual reality in public libraries. *Proceedings of the Association for Information Science and Technology, 57*(1), e254. https://doi.org/10.1002/pra2.254**

Lee, King, Dahya, and Lee examine how librarians understood and implemented virtual reality (VR) programming in public libraries. The article is useful for GBLS because it focuses less on the technical promise of VR in the abstract and more on what library workers learned while trying to make VR work as a public service. The authors frame public libraries as venues for equitable access to emerging technology, informal learning, and community engagement, while also warning that successful adoption depends on staff preparation, local context, content selection, outreach, space, accessibility, and the meanings librarians attach to learning and inclusion.

The study was part of a larger Washington State Libraries and Oculus project involving seven library sites. The larger project used surveys, patron interviews, librarian interviews, and site observations; this article focuses on librarian interview data, supplemented by field notes. The authors interviewed 23 librarians before and/or after VR programming, yielding 36 interviews. Participating libraries included urban, regional, and rural sites with different resources and communities. The libraries used Oculus Rift headsets and high-powered computers supplied through the project, and each library was responsible for its own programming. Oculus provided a suggested list of educational and entertainment applications, but most applications came with little guidance for library-based learning.

Before implementation, librarians tended to imagine VR as a powerful tool for informal learning because of immersion, interactivity, and imagination. They imagined patrons standing in historical scenes, experimenting safely, exploring places they could not otherwise visit, building empathy, practicing skills, and learning in ways that might engage people who are not drawn to text-based materials. Librarians also saw possible benefits for financially marginalized patrons because libraries could provide free access to expensive technology. Some imagined VR as a way for immigrant, Native, or non-dominant communities to see their experiences represented, although this depended heavily on whether relevant content existed and whether outreach reached those communities.

The post-program findings complicate these expectations. Librarians were often skeptical that VR programming had reached historically marginalized communities beyond patrons who lacked money or technology access. Waiver forms for children created friction even when translated, and one librarian described the permission cycle as unjust for working-class families. Librarians also noticed gendered and age-based participation patterns: young adult men and people already comfortable with games were often more likely to volunteer first, while older or female patrons sometimes needed to see others using VR before trying it themselves. This makes the article important for GBLS equity discussions because it shows that simply placing new technology in a library does not guarantee inclusive participation.

Space and accessibility were recurring implementation issues. A private room could protect patrons from feeling watched while wearing a headset, but a public setup could attract unlikely users by making VR visible and socially legible. Site observations showed that even single-user VR could become social when spectators watched the mirrored display, coached the user, reacted to the scene, and talked across age groups. At the same time, the headset-and-controller setup could be difficult for patrons with mobility issues, cognitive disabilities, or age restrictions. Librarians sometimes worked around barriers by guiding users more closely or controlling the experience while a patron gave verbal instructions, but the available VR content did not always include meaningful accessibility options.

The strongest finding for program design is that content mattered more than novelty. Librarians found that many available "educational" applications were enjoyable and memorable but did not necessarily support learning beyond exposure to VR itself. Some applications simply put text inside a headset, reproducing familiar instruction rather than using immersion and interaction meaningfully. As a result, many librarians concluded that patrons mainly learned how to use VR technology rather than learning content within VR. The authors therefore recommend connecting VR to existing library programs and valued local interests, such as STEM, art, design, or music, and moving toward content creation rather than passive consumption. Examples include Seattle Public Library projects on the Duwamish River and the Great Seattle Fire, which used VR to represent local histories and communities.

For GBLS, this article supplies a practical framework for immersive technology services. VR can support curiosity, informal learning, social engagement, access to expensive technologies, and creative production, but only when librarians make intentional decisions about audience, recruitment, space, accessibility, content, and wraparound programming. The article also pairs well with Lee et al. (2024): the 2020 study shows the general library-service challenges of VR implementation, while the 2024 study shows how co-design and wellness goals can make the technology more participatory and locally meaningful.

The limitations are appropriate to the genre and venue. The article centers librarian perspectives rather than patron outcomes, and it does not establish durable learning effects. It also studied a funded implementation where hardware was supplied, which may not match ordinary library conditions. Still, it is one of the clearest sources for understanding VR as a GBLS service model because it treats implementation as a social, spatial, pedagogical, and equity problem rather than a hardware demonstration.

Lee, Schmalz, Keating, and Ha examine the organizational and access problems surrounding video game development artifacts. The paper begins from the premise that digital games are cultural products whose history cannot be understood only through finished games. Like drafts, notebooks, production stills, and correspondence in other cultural industries, game development materials reveal processes, intentions, design choices, abandoned features, and production contexts. These materials include game design documents, technical design documents, art bibles, style guides, musical scores, test builds, voice-over auditions, marketing materials, and other born-digital ephemera. Yet these artifacts are endangered because game preservation work has focused more on finished products than on the development ecosystems that produced them.

The study uses a user-centered approach to understand how different stakeholders conceptualize, organize, and search for game development artifacts. The authors conducted in-depth, semi-structured interviews with 29 participants: 12 game industry professionals, six game researchers, and 11 participants from memory institutions such as museums, libraries, and archives. The interviews asked participants about their needs, current practices, search expectations, and challenges. Transcripts were inductively coded using a consensus model. The article reports preliminary findings from the first phase of a larger two-year project aimed at producing practical solutions for organizing game development artifacts.

Participants unanimously reported challenges in organizing and finding development artifacts. One set of problems came from game-industry culture. Developers often valued documentation in principle but did not experience it as central to the work of making a game. Documentation was seen as time-consuming, unrewarded, and secondary to production. The pressure to create new products, the pace of development, and the limited incentives for preserving process materials all made documentation fragile. Companies also had reasons not to share some materials, including concern about cut features, internal process, budgeting, and proprietary information. Platform creators, who needed to share technical materials with external developers, tended to be more invested in organizing documentation than game studios focused on shipping a specific product.

Technical work environments created a second set of problems. Agile development practices, changing specifications, living games, open-office communication, and tools such as Confluence, Dropbox, Google Drive, wikis, and platform-specific systems all shaped what was documented and how it was stored. In the short term, asking a nearby colleague could be faster than describing an artifact in a durable system. Over time, however, staff turnover, project complexity, and changing tools made retrieval difficult. The paper shows how practices that work inside a studio during active production can become major barriers for later reuse, preservation, research, or transfer to a memory institution.

The third problem was the lack of standard vocabulary and naming practices. Participants described inconsistent terminology for document types, game features, and development objects. Memory institutions also reported inadequate controlled vocabularies for describing game-related materials and had often created local vocabularies that were not shared across institutions. This fractured language makes it difficult for researchers, curators, archivists, librarians, and industry workers to know what exists, describe it consistently, or connect related materials.

The paper's most direct library contribution is its discussion of the disconnect between libraries, archives, and special collections. Participants described institutions where a finished game might be in a library collection, a related publication in another system, and development artifacts in an archive or museum system, with no explicit relationships among them. Systems such as WorldCat, Argus, and PastPerfect serve different collection types, and institutional units may not communicate enough to know that related items exist. For users seeking all materials related to a game, these organizational boundaries become discovery barriers even within a single institution.

For the GBLS project, this source is important because it pushes beyond games as circulating objects, programs, or learning tools. It treats games as complex cultural media with production histories that libraries, archives, and museums may need to collect, describe, preserve, and connect. It also helps explain why cataloging and metadata are not peripheral technical concerns: without shared vocabularies, relationship models, and cross-collection practices, libraries cannot make game-related cultural materials discoverable for scholars, designers, students, fans, or communities. The article also reinforces the need for partnerships with developers and memory institutions because no single library system can solve documentation, rights, vocabulary, and preservation problems alone.

The study is preliminary and short, and it does not present a completed taxonomy or best-practice model. It also focuses on specialized game development artifacts rather than everyday public-library game services. Still, it is highly relevant to a GBLS literature review because it names the infrastructure needed to support games as cultural heritage. Its implications extend to other born-digital and interactive media, including software, e-books, digital images, film, animation, and apps, where process artifacts may be as important as final products.

The study has limits. Its survey sample came from game-interested venues and is not necessarily representative of all public-library users. The appeals were derived from self-report, and the article proposes rather than tests advisory workflows. Even so, it gives GBLS a clear language for integrating games into readers' advisory and recommendation systems. Development artifacts such as design documents, style guides, test builds, prototypes, source code, art files, marketing materials, financial documents, quality-assurance records, and company ephemera reveal how games were made, why design choices happened, how production cultures worked, and what creators intended. Organizing and preserving these materials can enrich game history and inspire future creators, but the materials are fragile, scattered, heterogeneous, and often poorly described.

Collection, Her Interactive, Inc. Collection, Minnesota Educational Computing Corporation Collection, and Jordan Mechner Papers. This combination of stakeholder interviews and direct collection analysis gives the article much more evidentiary weight than a purely conceptual metadata proposal.

One major contribution is the article's description of the game development artifact lifecycle. Games often move from proof of concept to production, builds, testing, milestone versions, release, patches, and later archival or institutional accession. Contemporary development also includes minimum viable products, live updates, and frequent patches, while older packaged games had very different constraints. After release, development artifacts often lose priority as teams shift to new products. When collections are accessioned by cultural heritage institutions, they are assigned identifiers, legal and administrative files, locations, inventories, archival housing, descriptions, and finding aids. The article therefore makes clear that development artifacts move through different regimes of value: production utility, company memory, legal risk, scholarly evidence, and cultural heritage.

Creators faced unclear responsibilities, restructured teams, fluid roles, intellectual-property and nondisclosure concerns, weak file-naming practices, inconsistent storage locations, and pressure to prioritize production over documentation. Information professionals faced missing context, mixed physical and digital formats, obsolete media, copyright restrictions, and uncertainty over which standards to apply. Game researchers struggled with collection-level finding aids that were too broad to reveal whether a trip to an archive would be worthwhile, catalog systems that returned too much irrelevant material, and access restrictions that prevented online consultation of copyrighted or proprietary files.

The taxonomy is organized into three broad sections: development, organization-related material, and marketing. Development includes design, engineering, quality assurance and control, art, audio, writing, and production; marketing includes advertising, public relations, and promotional materials. The best-practice recommendations are equally important. The authors call for consistent controlled vocabulary, naming conventions, documentation standards, central locations for files, and regularly maintained practices that help creators and later information professionals. They also recommend stronger communication between game companies and cultural heritage institutions, arguing that creators may not know existing descriptive frameworks while information professionals may lack domain knowledge. Participatory archival processing could help bridge this gap by allowing creators to explain connections, context, and significance during accessioning or processing. For access, the authors discuss onsite terminals, restricted copying, donor agreements, rights-holder referrals, and the need to check copyright and NDA issues before accepting donations.

# **Abrams, S. S., & Van Eck, R. N. (2021). Checked out? Videogame programming in U.S. libraries and implications for videogame literacies. *Journal of Adolescent & Adult Literacy, 65*(3), 227-236. https://doi.org/10.1002/jaal.1197**

Abrams and Van Eck investigate the presence, forms, barriers, and literacy implications of videogame programming in U.S. libraries. Their central argument is that videogames fit naturally within libraries because videogame play involves digital, multimodal, social, and participatory literacies. Videogame literacies include understanding how games work, using digital and nondigital resources, navigating social interaction, collaborating, sharing knowledge, and making meaning with players, spectators, screens, controllers, rules, spaces, and game systems. The article therefore treats videogame programming as a possible literacy service, not simply as recreation or teen entertainment.

The study draws from a larger mixed-methods project involving more than 1,000 U.S. librarians from all 50 states. The survey was administered through Qualtrics between August 2017 and May 2018 and included questions about library setting, programming, roles, videogame programming, videogame play, device ownership, and barriers to adoption. A total of 1,016 U.S. librarians responded. Respondents were roughly distributed across urban, suburban, and rural settings; most held library science graduate degrees and had worked in their libraries for about 12 years. The article combines quantitative survey findings with qualitative open-ended responses.

Abrams and Van Eck use the framework of first-order and second-order barriers. First-order barriers are external constraints such as funding, hardware, software, space, furniture, training, technical support, and policies. Second-order barriers are internal constraints such as beliefs, confidence, perceived value, assumptions about youth, comfort with games, and concerns about behavior or noise. This framing is especially useful for GBLS because it shows that game programming depends both on material infrastructure and on professional culture.

The prevalence findings complicate assumptions that gaming is already widespread in libraries. Of 804 librarians who answered the relevant question, 384, or 48%, reported having some form of videogame programming, while 420, or 52%, did not. Among libraries with programming, most programs were recent: 63% had existed for less than five years, and 35% had existed for less than three years. Nearly half of libraries with programming relied only on scheduled access, about one-fifth relied only on open play, about one-fifth offered both, and a small group reported other models such as games or consoles for checkout. The authors argue that programming structure matters for literacy. Scheduled play fits familiar library event models but may restrict the iterative, ongoing, peer-supported nature of videogame literacies; open play enables access but may miss opportunities for tournaments, group play, or structured reflection.

First-order barriers were substantial. Librarians tended to agree that the difficulty of supporting videogames was a significant barrier. They reported that general funding for new programming could be difficult, and many thought current library structures would not support videogame programs because of limited funds or lack of institutional value. Training was especially uneven. Informal training was the most common source of preparation, including independent searching, informal conversations, and feedback from other librarians. Library science degrees accounted for less than 12% of training, and formal professional development accounted for 22%. The authors therefore call for library science programs, teacher education, professional development, and accreditation standards to address videogame literacies more explicitly.

Second-order barriers were also clear. Many librarians owned gaming devices and expressed positive attitudes toward games, but they often lacked confidence in their ability to play games or support game play. Some comments showed enthusiasm for teen bonding, cooperative play, Japanese language/culture programs, intergenerational game play, problem solving, and library spaces where teens saw the library as welcoming. Other comments revealed concerns about management, setup, noise, hygiene, behavior, adult patron complaints, staff acceptance, space use, and whether games had value. The authors note that some comments were gendered or stereotypical, which matters for GBLS because adult assumptions about teens and gaming can shape which literacies libraries recognize as legitimate.

For the GBLS project, this source gives a national-scale account of videogame programming as literacy infrastructure. It supports the argument that games in libraries can foster participatory, multimodal, social, and layered literacies, but that those benefits depend on programming design, staff preparation, institutional support, and youth involvement. It also provides a practical assessment lens: asking whether a GBLS program is limited by resources, training, space, and equipment, or by beliefs, confidence, professional identity, and assumptions about patrons. The authors' recommendation to start small, such as with a videogame discussion club, is useful for libraries that cannot immediately support hardware-heavy programming.

The study has limitations. It relies on survey responses and self-reported perceptions, and it does not directly observe youth learning or measure outcomes of videogame literacy. Differences across library types and local inequities are not fully analyzed in this article. Still, the source is highly valuable because it names the real conditions under which videogame programming is adopted, constrained, and interpreted by librarians. It also helps the GBLS review connect videogame play to adolescent and adult literacy research.

The article begins with two examples, *September 12th* and *Beyond Good & Evil*, to show that games can communicate complex political, ethical, and civic ideas. *September 12th* models the consequences of missile strikes in a fictional Middle Eastern city, making player action part of the argument about terrorism and collateral damage. *Beyond Good & Evil* places the player in the role of Jade, a photojournalist investigating propaganda, corruption, occupation, and resistance. Access means making media and technology available, especially to patrons who might otherwise be excluded. Motivation means encouraging discovery, enjoyment, learning, and library use through appealing materials. Guidance means helping patrons connect interests, needs, questions, and formats without controlling or censoring their choices. This framework is useful for GBLS because it translates game services into familiar library values: collect, invite, advise, contextualize, and support.

Challenge gives players "hard fun," feedback, flow, trial and error, and opportunities to develop portable skills. Connection includes self-discovery, shared play, discussion, community, and broader civic conversation. These categories anticipate later library-advisory work on appeals while remaining practical for librarians who need basic vocabulary for explaining why games draw patrons in.

Libraries should decide whether games belong in general collections, review or curriculum collections, gaming labs, staff programming collections, or circulating equipment collections. They should consider age ratings, platforms, single-player and multiplayer formats, genre variety, critically praised games, patron expertise, and local needs. The authors recommend using ESRB ratings, game-review sources, and experienced gamers as advisors. They also discuss educators and game scholars as special patron populations, especially in education libraries, and suggest serious games, games for curriculum exploration, and titles that provoke scholarly or civic discussion.

For access, the article emphasizes catalog visibility, display, checkout ease, outreach, social media, and the possibility of circulating consoles or setting up kiosks. For programming, it suggests integrating games into existing groups and events for children, teens, adults, seniors, English-language learners, guilds, and academic communities. It also encourages libraries to connect games to other materials: books, graphic novels, films, fan fiction, game design, music, podcasts, lectures, author/designer talks, and other cultural media. This cross-media orientation makes the source especially relevant to GBLS collection and advisory work.

For the GBLS project, Buchanan and Vanden Elzen help establish an early service rationale for games in libraries. Their framework of access, motivation, and guidance is still useful for explaining why game services are not a fad or a distraction from core library work. The article also helps connect collections, programming, media literacy, critical literacy, advisory, and cultural programming. Its limits are that it predates much of the later empirical literature, offers broad recommendations rather than assessment data, and uses examples from a 2012 platform and media environment. Even so, it remains a strong practitioner foundation for why libraries should engage games as media, technology, and culture.

# **Copeland, T., Henderson, B., Mayer, B., & Nicholson, S. (2013). Three different paths for tabletop gaming in school libraries. *Library Trends, 61*(4), 825-835. https://doi.org/10.1353/lib.2013.0018**

The article begins from the observation that school-library gaming has two primary goals: education and recreation. Tabletop games, if chosen well, can serve both goals because they are inexpensive, portable, durable, adaptable, social, and easier to integrate into classrooms than many digital games. In tabletop role-play, teachers and librarians can adjust events on the fly, reward student research, respond to creative suggestions, and let outcomes vary across iterations. The account is valuable because it insists that educational games must first be good games. Students respond when they realize they are playing something fun and challenging rather than a thinly disguised worksheet.

For life skills, librarians and special education teachers used games to support gross and fine motor skills, social skills, cooperative play, team play, and problem solving. Language-independent games helped students participate without reading ability becoming a barrier. Because tabletop prototypes can be made with paper, cards, poster board, and index cards, students can focus on representing systems, rules, and concepts rather than learning complex digital creation tools. The design process also lets students with different strengths collaborate.

For the GBLS project, this article is a strong service-model source. It shows that tabletop gaming in libraries can be curricular, recreational, social, inclusive, standards-aligned, and design-oriented. It connects several review themes: clubs and recurring play groups, TTRPG and role-play programs, instruction and information literacy, game design workshops, facilitation, reflection, and access. It also helps explain why tabletop games remain important even in a digital-games literature review: they are affordable, adaptable, socially rich, and transparent enough for players to understand and modify game systems.

The article is practitioner-based rather than a formal assessment study, so its evidence is anecdotal and local. Still, the three cases give concrete implementation models and vocabulary for school-library GBLS work. They also show that the library role can range from collection provision to club facilitation, curriculum collaboration, game mastering, professional development for teachers, and design coaching.

This historical framing is useful for GBLS because it counters the idea that games are merely a recent fad or a departure from library work. Instead, games are presented as part of libraries' longstanding role as social, educational, recreational, and community spaces.

Hill organizes the rationale for library gaming around several themes. Games can support active, contextualized learning; after-school game programs can foster social skills through cross-age play and peer teaching; games can shape behavior; and digital games can help patrons develop twenty-first-century literacies such as critical thinking, problem solving, collaboration, information literacy, and media literacy. The access argument is central: if expensive devices, systems, or games are unevenly distributed, libraries can act as social equalizers by offering access and media mentorship much as they have offered internet access.

The article also names implementation challenges that recur across GBLS sources. Librarians may worry about spending resources on games, and administrators may require evidence from other successful programs. Game programs can be noisy and may need careful scheduling or space planning. Collections can face theft, replacement costs, parental challenges over content, and budget limits. Hill also warns that libraries should avoid educational games that overemphasize curriculum while underemphasizing game quality, a point echoed in later sources that insist games must first work as games.

One of Hill's most useful contributions is its overview of game categories and service settings. Digital games, video games, role-playing games, and tabletop games are treated as distinct but overlapping library possibilities. Digital games are linked to active, goal-oriented, contextualized, adaptive, feedback-oriented learning. Video games are discussed as circulating materials, access stations, events, and potential academic resources. Role-playing games are described through collaborative storytelling and community building, while tabletop games are presented as portable, affordable, durable, adaptable, and transparent enough for players to understand mechanics.

Hill then surveys public, school, and academic library uses. Public libraries can use gaming programs, summer reading game elements, senior programming, teen clubs, and tournaments to build community and invite patrons into other services. School libraries can integrate games into language learning, history role-play, after-school clubs, and classroom collaboration. Academic libraries can offer game nights, circulating games, gaming stations, curriculum-support collections, new-student orientation games, and information-literacy games. The article also highlights community involvement and collaboration, including student organizations, campus departments, local game stores, retailers, and board game clubs.

The final sections address gamification and game creation. Hill distinguishes reward or points systems applied to existing practices from the creation and playing of games to teach library concepts. Gamification is tied to orientation, information literacy, resource use, reading programs, and motivation. Game creation is framed as a way for patrons to learn design, art, writing, planning, testing, local history, and collaboration by making games rather than only playing them.

For the GBLS project, Hill is best used as a synthetic professional overview. It does not provide original assessment data or a detailed case study, and much of its evidence comes through secondary discussion of other sources. Still, it helps map the range of game forms and library settings that GBLS must account for: public, school, and academic libraries; digital, video, role-playing, tabletop, gamified, and user-created games; collections, programs, access, literacy, community, partnerships, and advocacy. It is useful for introductory framing and for showing how mid-2010s library writing consolidated earlier games-in-libraries arguments.

Post, Henry, Nunnally, and Lanham examine how libraries can represent digitally distributed indie narrative games and their creators in library catalogs. The article begins from a shift that matters deeply for GBLS collections: video games are increasingly digital, platform-mediated, frequently updated, and produced by small studios or individual creators rather than only by large publishers releasing boxed games on physical media. The authors argue that library game collections should include indie games and alternative forms of play, not just mainstream commercial titles, but that doing so requires catalogers to adapt descriptive cataloging, subject cataloging, and name authority practices.

The study uses action research grounded in an applied cataloging project. The authors created 17 original MARC records and substantially enhanced one existing record for 18 digitally acquired games from Choice of Games, a publisher of independently made, text-based narrative games. They also created 33 original Library of Congress Name Authority File records for individuals involved in producing those games and edited four existing records to include game-design work. The reflective analysis compares this work with cataloging mainstream video games, other electronic resources, and narrative media such as books.

A central finding is that digitally distributed games resemble other electronic resources in some ways, but games still require game-specific description. OLAC guidelines supported much of the descriptive cataloging, including online-resource description, technical requirements, platform as edition, and notes about interactivity. For Choice of Games titles, the authors described the works as computer programs and games, but also used "text" as a content type because these are interactive text-based works rather than moving-image games. The authors note that many physical games now also depend on patches, downloadable content, or online play, so the line between physical and digital game description is blurring. Indie games distributed through Steam, itch.io, Kickstarter, studio websites, and episodic release models may require more precise version and relationship description when libraries collect them for game history or design research.

Subject cataloging raises a different problem. Narrative games occupy a hybrid position between literature and games: they have story, characters, setting, and themes, but they are also interactive computer games with mechanics and player choice. The authors found LCSH useful for narrative subject matter and OLAC video game genre terms useful for gameplay, but existing terms such as "Plot-your-own stories" and "Interactive book video games" did not match the language of narrative game communities. They therefore supplemented controlled vocabularies with terms such as interactive fiction, text-based games, and interactive narrative games, arguing that player and maker terminology can improve discovery.

Name authority work is the article's strongest contribution to GBLS infrastructure. Mainstream game records often rely on corporate creators because individual credits are hard to identify or too numerous. Indie narrative games, by contrast, often foreground authors, editors, artists, programmers, publishers, and small creative teams. By creating name authority records for people involved in Choice of Games titles, the authors attempted to represent how these games are actually made and to integrate indie game creators into the bibliographic universe. They argue that authority records help future libraries share work, support discovery, and recognize game creators as cultural producers alongside authors, filmmakers, and musicians.

For the GBLS project, this source strengthens the cataloging, metadata, discovery, and cultural-collection portions of the review. It pairs well with Lee, Schmalz, Keating, and Ha (2020) and McDonald et al. (2021): those sources focus on development artifacts, preservation, and taxonomy, while Post et al. show how everyday bibliographic cataloging must change when libraries collect digital indie games. It also supports equity and representation arguments, because indie games often document experiences, communities, creators, and play forms marginalized by mainstream definitions of "real games." The article is not about programming or patron outcomes, but it is directly about whether libraries can make diverse game materials findable, citable, and connected.

# **Robson, D., Phillips, J., & Guerrero, S. (2018). Don't just roll the dice: Simple solutions for circulating tabletop game collections effectively in your library. *Library Resources & Technical Services, 62*(2), 80-90. https://doi.org/10.5860/lrts.62n2.80**

Unique identifier: Zotero item key `PNVXZLYB`; Better BibTeX citation key `robsonDontJustRoll2018`; attachment key `T3MQW6LE`.

Robson, Phillips, and Guerrero provide one of the most concrete operational studies of tabletop game circulation in libraries. The article reports on a University of North Texas Media Library project designed to determine how much preservation and processing work is actually necessary for circulating tabletop games. The problem is practical and important: as board game collections grow, libraries need procedures for preserving boxes and components, preventing loss, checking games in and out, and keeping staff labor reasonable. The UNT collection had grown from a closed-stacks, campus-use collection to a larger circulating collection, eventually reaching hundreds of games, and the authors wanted evidence rather than anecdotes from gaming forums or local habit.

The project brought together a media librarian, preservation librarian, cataloger, circulation desk manager, and student assistants. The team purchased four new copies each of *Ticket to Ride* and *Betrayal at House on the Hill* because the two games included varied boards, cards, tokens, movers, room tiles, and other components. Each copy received a different level of preservation and processing, ranging from almost none to very extensive intervention. The authors define preservation as protective treatment for physical pieces, such as sleeves, contact paper, tape, cloth corners, spray, wax, or binding, while processing refers to barcoding, labeling, bagging, and inventory routines designed to support circulation and reduce loss.

The study tested both circulation use and simulated damage. The eight game copies circulated over an eight-month period, with staff rotating copies so each would circulate at least 15 times. After check-in, staff assessed damage and loss. The project also used pop-up messages in the circulation system and staff guidance to manage the research process. After the circulation phase, the authors ran controlled stress tests on components by exposing cards, tiles, boards, instructions, and boxes to hazards such as water, soda, salsa, cheese dip, stacking, and table-edge stress. Damage was rated on a scale from no damage to unusable.

The findings are useful because they resist overengineering. The fully preserved and fully processed copies had no damage or loss, but the differences between minimal and extensive processing were not large enough to justify the staff time and cost in most cases. The authors conclude that tabletop games can circulate like other library items without expanding preservation and processing procedures far beyond normal practice. Games do require more management time than books, but not so much that libraries should avoid collecting them. The acceptable level of damage will vary locally, but the UNT study suggests that libraries can make evidence-based tradeoffs between durability, cost, and staff burden.

The best-practice recommendations are concrete. UNT decided to sleeve cards, especially for high-use or hard-to-replace games, because cards quickly absorb liquid, oils, and contaminants and marked cards can affect gameplay. The library also reinforced game boxes with cloth corners, using in-house binding capacity and leftover cloth tape to reduce cost. Game pieces were bagged because bagging was inexpensive and helped minimize loss. The authors chose minimal processing for circulation: each game is cataloged, a single barcode is added to the box lid, and a single item record is used for circulation. Call numbers appear on lids and sides to support stacks management.

For the GBLS project, this source is important because it turns "board game collections" into an operational service model. It shows that access to tabletop games depends on mundane infrastructure: cataloging, barcodes, labels, inventories, bags, component sleeves, box reinforcement, circulation workflows, staff training, check-in expectations, replacement tolerance, and local preservation capacity. It also pairs well with Forsythe (2021), which cites Robson et al. for practical preservation techniques; together, the two sources show both how a small academic board game program can be built and how a larger circulating collection can be maintained.

The article is not an assessment of learning, belonging, or patron outcomes. Its evidence comes from two selected games and one university media library, so local conditions such as in-house binding capacity, staffing, circulation volume, and replacement budgets matter. Still, its narrowness is a strength for GBLS implementation: it gives libraries a tested starting point for circulating tabletop games without assuming that every piece needs intensive preservation or every check-in needs exhaustive staff counting.

Facilitators included librarians and faculty, hints carried a small time penalty, and successful teams unlocked a physical lock box.

The pharmacy and medical iterations show how the same game structure can be adapted across settings. Pharmacy students completed an on-site virtual individual escape room in a lecture hall, using laptops, Google Forms, Google Docs, PubMed, the MeSH Database, and PubMed Search Builder. Medicine students completed a fully remote individual escape room during online orientation, with a brief lecture and demonstration followed by independent online puzzle work, direct-message hints, a virtual lock box, and a short debriefing. A later optometry version also moved fully online. The formats therefore ranged from in-person/team to hybrid/individual to fully online/individual.

For GBLS, the most useful contribution is the implementation detail. Helbing et al. identify several design decisions libraries must make: whether the activity should be team-based or individual, how much time and money can be spent, whether the setting should be physical, hybrid, or online, and whether grades or points should be attached. Team-based formats support communication and collaboration, while individual formats better demonstrate individual proficiency. Initial planning took roughly 12 hours, but later adaptations required about three to five hours. Online tools such as forms, documents, and QR codes can keep costs low, while physical rooms may feel more active and help students learn the library space.

The article is also helpful on grading and accessibility. Points can signal that the activity matters and encourage full participation, but they can shift attention toward fairness, cheating, hint penalties, or rank rather than toward skill development. Completion-based grading created a lower-pressure environment in the pharmacy version. The authors also caution that escape-room clues should avoid unnecessary barriers for learners for whom English is not a first language; spelling, grammar, idioms, puns, and expressions can create friction unrelated to the learning objectives. Team-based formats may help distribute those burdens.

The evidence is modest. The authors report that embedded formative assessments showed students completed the tasks within the allotted time, and informal observation and anecdotal feedback suggested enjoyment and engagement. They do not provide systematic survey data, comparison groups, long-term retention measures, or formal assessment of information literacy gains. Still, the case report is valuable for GBLS because it demonstrates how escape-room design can be integrated into real health sciences library instruction, adapted across modalities, and aligned with concrete research and resource-use objectives rather than simply added as a novelty.

# **Welhouse, Z., Lee, J. H., & Bancroft, J. (2015). "What am I fighting for?": Creating a controlled vocabulary for video game plot metadata. *Cataloging & Classification Quarterly, 53*(2), 157-189. https://doi.org/10.1080/01639374.2014.963776**

Unique identifier: Zotero item key `DTE3PY5T`; Better BibTeX citation key `welhouseWhatAmFighting2015`; attachment key `XBQB9RQU`.

Welhouse, Lee, and Bancroft develop a controlled vocabulary for video game plot metadata as part of the University of Washington and Seattle Interactive Media Museum video game metadata schema work. The article begins from a cataloging problem that is directly relevant to libraries: video games are cultural and research objects, but existing organizational systems often describe them through platform, title, publisher, price, and broad genre while giving little reliable access to what a game is "really about." Commercial sites, fan summaries, Steam tags, WorldCat summaries, and Library of Congress Subject Headings may supply useful fragments, but the authors argue that none provide consistent, standardized, plot-level access for discovery, browsing, collocation, advisory, or scholarship.

The article is grounded in user warrant. Earlier GAMER/SIMM work identified personas including game players, parents, collectors, scholars, developers, librarians/curators, teachers, and industry professionals, and this article uses that broader research to justify plot/narrative as a metadata field. In interviews with 56 users, 21 mentioned story, plot, or narrative as important, often as one of their top factors, while also noting that there was no effective way to search games by it. A later survey of 1,257 participants ranked "Plot/Narrative" as the seventh most useful metadata element, with positive responses from 71 percent of participants. For libraries, this user evidence matters because plot access could support readers' advisory-like game advisory, parent mediation, curricular matching, thematic displays, exhibits, and research into game stories over time.

The method combines domain analysis and card sorting. The authors collected candidate terms from RPGs, instruction books, in-game text, package descriptions, fan-created descriptions, game websites, wikis, encyclopedias, and other game-related sources. They then reduced and organized a large term list using criteria of accuracy, understandability, and comprehensiveness. The article also reviews literary plot classification systems, including appeal elements, folktale motifs, dramatic situations, and "basic plots," but finds that these systems are often too broad, too fine-grained, too literary, or too difficult for catalogers to apply consistently to games.

Their main design contribution is a pair structure inspired by a faceted approach. Each plot statement combines a controlled VERB and OBJECT, with an implied SUBJECT of the player-character or characters. Examples include seeking treasure, escaping a dystopia, restoring order, developing friendship, confronting self, preventing disaster, discovering intrigue, and saving the world. Limiting records to three plot pairs discourages cataloging every side quest while pushing catalogers beyond a single generic statement such as "characters save world." In the sample records, the structure allows games within the same genre, especially RPGs, to be collocated by plot elements that would otherwise be hidden by broad genre labels.

For GBLS, the article strengthens the review's cataloging and discovery thread. It shows that game collections need metadata that supports how patrons actually seek, compare, and interpret games, not only how vendors package them. Plot metadata can help a librarian connect a game to novels, films, nonfiction, class topics, or patron interests; it can help a teacher pair a game with literary themes; and it can help researchers ask cultural questions about recurring stories in games. In this sense, game-based library service includes not only events and play programs but also the infrastructure that makes games findable, recommendable, and intellectually legible.

The limitations are also important for the GBLS review. Plot metadata introduces hard cataloging questions about granularity, optional side quests, nonlinear narratives, moral choices, games with thin or no plot, and the amount of game knowledge required from catalogers. The article also names practical concerns around spoilers, the need for free-text summary fields, and possible crowdsourced description to supplement professional cataloging. These problems are useful because they show that game metadata is not a simple matter of adding more tags; it requires local policy, interface choices, user testing, domain expertise, and perhaps collaboration with game communities.

Overall, Welhouse et al. are best used as foundational evidence for video game cataloging, metadata, and advisory rather than as a program outcome study. The article does not evaluate a library service intervention, but it gives the GBLS project a concrete vocabulary for why discovery systems matter: richer game metadata can turn video games from loosely described entertainment objects into searchable cultural materials that support access, interpretation, curriculum, preservation, and library-mediated recommendation.
