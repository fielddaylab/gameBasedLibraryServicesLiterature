# **Summary of Kym Buchanan & Angela M. Vanden Elzen (2012), “Beyond a Fad: Why Video Games Should Be Part of 21st Century Libraries”**  ***Education Libraries, Vol. 35, Nos. 1–2, pp. 15–33.***

---

### **Overview**

Buchanan and Vanden Elzen argue that **video games belong in modern library collections and programming** because they cultivate critical literacies, foster community, and align with libraries’ enduring missions of **access, motivation, and guidance**.  
 They combine theory, case examples, and practical recommendations to show how games can serve as **tools for learning, creativity, and social connection**, not just entertainment. The article situates games within the long tradition of libraries adapting to new media—from books to the internet—and calls for librarians to embrace this next evolution.

---

### **1\. Video Games as Meaningful Media**

The authors open by referencing the **U.S. Supreme Court’s 2011 decision (Brown v. EMA)**, which granted **First Amendment protection** to video games, affirming them as expressive works like books or films. They note that, like earlier moral panics over comic books or movies, skepticism about games reflects cultural unfamiliarity rather than evidence of harm.

They use two case examples—*September 12th* and *Beyond Good & Evil*—to illustrate how video games can be **serious art forms** that communicate ideas through visual design, interactivity, and simulation:

* **September 12th** (Newsgaming, 2003): A simple browser game critiquing the cycle of violence in the “war on terror.” The player’s missile strikes cause collateral damage, turning mourners into new terrorists—an allegory for the consequences of militarism.

* **Beyond Good & Evil** (Ubisoft, 2003): A complex adventure game exploring **media ethics, propaganda, and resistance**, through the player’s role as Jade, a photojournalist exposing government corruption.

These examples demonstrate how games can **provoke reflection, empathy, and discussion**, similar to literature and film.

---

### **2\. The Role of Libraries: Access, Motivation, and Guidance**

Buchanan and Vanden Elzen frame their argument around three enduring library missions:

1. **Access** – Libraries democratize media access, bridging gaps in technology and affordability. Offering video games continues this role, ensuring that everyone—not just affluent gamers—can explore a major cultural form.

2. **Motivation** – Librarians inspire discovery and curiosity. Games naturally motivate participation and persistence (“hard fun”), helping patrons learn through play and exploration.

3. **Guidance** – Librarians curate and contextualize media, helping patrons interpret and engage critically. For games, this means teaching media literacy, contextualizing content, and connecting games to other learning materials.

---

### **3\. Literacies in the Age of Games**

The authors argue that video games introduce **new literacies** while reinforcing traditional ones.  
 They identify three defining traits of games as a medium:

* **Visual communication** – Reading symbolic cues and visual storytelling.

* **Interactivity** – Making decisions and interpreting consequences.

* **Simulation** – Engaging with models of real or imaginary systems.

These qualities require **critical literacy**: understanding authorship, design bias, and metaphor. For example, recognizing that *September 12th* simplifies complex social dynamics is part of reading it critically. Games thus foster **systems thinking, problem-solving, and interpretive skills** relevant across disciplines.

---

### **4\. Why People Play: Immersion, Challenge, and Connection**

The authors synthesize psychological and educational theories (Gee, Csikszentmihalyi, Papert, McGonigal) to explain three universal appeals of gaming:

1. **Immersion** – The emotional and cognitive absorption of “being inside” a game world, creating empathy and “embodied learning.”

2. **Challenge** – The pleasure of “hard fun” and *flow*: optimal engagement when difficulty matches ability. This supports learning transfer, persistence, and metacognition.

3. **Connection** – Games foster personal identity, interpersonal relationships, and civic discourse. Multiplayer and narrative games encourage teamwork, ethical reasoning, and social storytelling.

They acknowledge potential risks—excessive play, violent content—but stress that **balanced, guided play** and **critical discussion** mitigate these concerns, just as libraries have long mediated access to controversial books or films.

---

### **5\. Practical Guidelines for Librarians**

#### **a. Selecting and Curating Games**

* Include a mix of genres (action, puzzle, RPG, “serious games”).

* Consider **ESRB age ratings** (E, T, M) and display information for parents.

* Support multiple platforms (Wii, Xbox, PlayStation) but wait to adopt new consoles until stable markets emerge.

* Include games with educational or artistic value (*SimCity, Portal, Civilization*, etc.).

* Create “review collections” for educators and researchers studying games as learning tools.

#### **b. Integrating Games into Collections and Programming**

* Circulate both games and consoles, with secure storage and short loan periods.

* Create **in-library play stations or kiosks** to invite hands-on engagement.

* Use games in **community events and tournaments** to attract diverse audiences—from children and teens to seniors.

* Support **game-based learning** by connecting teachers, students, and researchers with educational titles and resources like *Serious Games* networks.

#### **c. Connecting Games to Other Media**

* Link displays of popular games to **related books, films, and nonfiction** (“If you like Halo, try these sci-fi novels”).

* Encourage **fan fiction, reviews, and creative writing** inspired by games.

* Promote critical analysis and storytelling through **discussion groups or contests**.

* Showcase **game-inspired art and film** events, such as screenings of documentaries (*The King of Kong*, *Second Skin*).

#### **d. Training and Motivation for Staff**

* Provide librarians with **training sessions or web resources** on gaming culture and media literacy.

* Encourage staff to play games together to build familiarity and enthusiasm.

* Develop library guides (LibGuides) featuring recommended games, ratings, and related materials.

---

### **6\. Libraries as Connective Cultural Spaces**

Buchanan and Vanden Elzen conclude that libraries are **ideal mediators between new media and the public**.  
 By incorporating games, libraries can:

* Reach new users (especially youth who may not otherwise visit).

* Model **critical engagement** with interactive media.

* Serve as **forums for discussion** about creativity, ethics, and cultural values.

* Preserve games as part of the historical and artistic record, much like books and films.

They argue that games, like literature, can convey complex messages and emotional truths—and that libraries should help patrons **read, interpret, and create** within this expanding media landscape.

---

**In essence:**  
 Buchanan and Vanden Elzen (2012) contend that **video games are legitimate cultural and educational media** that align perfectly with libraries’ missions to **provide access, inspire learning, and guide critical reflection**. By curating, circulating, and programming with games, libraries not only stay relevant in the digital age but also foster **literacy, community, and creativity** among 21st-century learners.

# **Summary of Ryan F. Buller (2017), “Lending Video Game Consoles in an Academic Library”**  ***portal: Libraries and the Academy, Vol. 17, No. 2, pp. 337–346.***

---

### **Overview**

Ryan Buller, Access Services Librarian at the **University of Denver**, documents how the university’s library created and implemented a **video game console lending program**—one of the first of its kind in an academic library. The project was designed to support the **Emergent Digital Practices (EDP)** program, an interdisciplinary major combining art, design, programming, and media studies.

The case study shows how libraries can **adapt traditional collection models** to meet **curricular and creative needs** beyond books or databases—demonstrating flexibility, innovation, and cross-campus collaboration.

---

### **1\. Background and Purpose**

In **May 2015**, the University Libraries launched a lending service that provided **video game consoles** for student and faculty use.

* The idea emerged from a **faculty grant** aimed at building a video game collection for academic study and design.

* Librarians realized that **access to hardware** was just as important as access to software: many students could not afford consoles or televisions, limiting their ability to analyze or test games.

* Lending consoles aligned with the library’s mission to support teaching and research across disciplines.

Buller frames this decision as a **redefinition of what constitutes a library collection**, extending the principle of access to new forms of creative and technological media.

---

### **2\. Literature Context**

Earlier library gaming initiatives (Beales 2006; Czarnecki 2007; Levine 2006\) focused on **public libraries** using games to attract new patrons through tournaments or youth programming.  
 In contrast, **academic libraries** (Laskowski & Ward, 2009; Robson & Durkee, 2012\) began using video games to support curriculum in **game design, media studies, and cultural research**.

Buller builds on this trend but expands it: his focus is not just on circulating games, but on **circulating consoles** themselves, treating hardware as a legitimate academic resource.

---

### **3\. Rethinking the Library Collection**

Buller argues that what a library chooses to collect and lend must reflect its **core mission: supporting faculty and student work.**

* For game design students, books and articles alone were insufficient—**hands-on experience with multiple consoles** was necessary to understand technical and artistic differences.

* Libraries have historically provided **access hardware** for other media (microfilm readers, VHS players, record players), so lending consoles was consistent with past practice.

* The decision reinforced that libraries must evolve to meet **nontraditional academic needs** in emerging disciplines.

---

### **4\. Deciding to Lend, Not Just Host**

The library debated whether to:

1. **Create a dedicated gaming space**, or

2. **Circulate consoles for off-site use.**

**Reasons for choosing circulation:**

* Students needed extended access for design and testing, not short in-library sessions.

* Dorms or homes provided better setups for testing games on personal schedules.

* Circulation enabled **greater equity and flexibility**, while a fixed space would have limited access to one user at a time.  
   Thus, consoles were lent out like books or laptops, rather than locked in a lab.

---

### **5\. Hardware and Setup**

To support the EDP program’s interdisciplinary needs, the library purchased **multiple generations** of each major console:

* Sony PlayStation 3 and 4

* Microsoft Xbox 360 and One

* Nintendo Wii and Wii U

Each system was packaged in a **GAEMS Personal Gaming Environment**—a rugged carrying case with a built-in screen and speakers—so students without televisions could still use the equipment.  
 All cables, controllers, and peripherals were included, with checklists and images to ensure accurate return.

**Lending policy:**

* One-week loan period, renewable twice.

* Patrons could place holds, ensuring fair access.

---

### **6\. Implementation Challenges**

**a. Accountability and Damage Prevention**

* Every console was cataloged with **serial numbers for internal components**, checked after each return to prevent tampering or parts substitution.

* Detailed check-in forms allowed any staff member to verify completeness.

**b. Software Security and Privacy**

* Each console had a **library-managed account** to prevent permanent changes.

* Patrons could log into their own profiles but not save credentials or restrict access for others.

* Staff wiped all personal data and confirmed no credit card information remained before recirculating.

**c. Internal Resistance**  
 Some staff questioned whether this was an appropriate or cost-effective library service. Buller addressed concerns by:

* Emphasizing the program’s **direct curricular support** for an academic discipline.

* Comparing circulation data and costs to underused traditional resources.

* Framing the project as part of the library’s evolving mission to support **research and creativity in digital media**.

---

### **7\. Opportunities and Impact**

**Institutional Collaboration:**  
 The console program fostered closer collaboration between the library and the EDP department, positioning the library as an **active partner in curriculum development**.

**Model of Adaptation:**  
 It showcased how academic libraries can **adapt to new scholarly forms**—extending their relevance in fields like digital art, gaming, and media production.

**Staff Development:**  
 The initiative prompted discussions about **what academic libraries should collect**, encouraging staff to think creatively about supporting 21st-century disciplines.

**Success Metrics:**

* Circulation levels met or exceeded expectations.

* Word-of-mouth among students and faculty drove demand without formal marketing.

* The library later expanded holdings to include **extra controllers** and sought further funding to grow the collection.

---

### **8\. Conclusion**

Buller concludes that the video game console lending service **successfully advanced the library’s mission** by:

* Supporting interdisciplinary teaching and research.

* Meeting genuine curricular needs.

* Demonstrating flexibility and innovation in collection development.

The project also sparked institutional reflection on the **changing nature of academic libraries**: from repositories of static media to **active partners in creation, experimentation, and digital practice**.

---

**In essence:**  
 Buller (2017) shows how the **University of Denver’s academic library** became a key collaborator in digital media education by lending **video game consoles**. The program—designed to support curriculum, not recreation—illustrates how libraries can evolve beyond books to provide **hands-on access to creative technologies**, aligning 21st-century library practice with emerging academic disciplines.

Jennifer Burek Pierce traces the **early history of games and toys in American public libraries**, revealing that **play and tactile learning were once central to children’s library services**.  These playful methods were later suppressed as the library profession became more standardized, emphasizing **uniformity, book-centered learning, and adult-defined notions of cultural improvement**.

---

### **1\. The Rise of Play in the Library (1876–1900)**

Informed by thinkers like **Friedrich Froebel** and **G. Stanley Hall**, early children’s librarians saw play as essential to learning. They created **interactive “children’s rooms”** filled with colorful images, dissected maps, puzzles, stereoscopes, and craft materials.

* Librarians like **May Lowe (1892)** proclaimed that “the reign of the children began,” celebrating the integration of games and pictures into library service.

* Caroline Hewins’s *Books for the Young* (1882) and Arthur Bostwick’s *The American Public Library* (1910) both advocated for **visual and tactile materials** to attract young readers.

* These spaces encouraged not only reading but **making, doing, and socializing**, aligning with child-centered educational reforms.

---

### **2\. Why Librarians Used Toys and Games**

* **Educational influence:** The **kindergarten movement** emphasized hands-on, sensory learning. Librarians adopted Froebelian ideas—believing that **pictures, colors, and manipulatives** stimulated comprehension.

* **Social reform:** Children’s librarians saw play as a way to **elevate working-class and immigrant youth**, offering “beautiful objects” to inspire imagination and moral growth.

* **Multimedia learning:** Librarians used **dissected maps**, **Sliced Animals** spelling games, **scrapbook-making**, and **lantern slides** as forms of “rational entertainment” that developed attention, reasoning, and visual literacy.

* **Cultural aspiration:** Toys and games often promoted **patriotism, geography, and moral order**, linking play with civic identity and discipline.

---

### **3\. The Children’s Room as a Progressive Space**

Children’s rooms became **experimental laboratories** for new kinds of literacy and engagement:

* They were **interactive and democratic spaces**—children could touch, play, and even create materials like homemade scrapbooks.

* These rooms **reversed early library restrictions** that had barred children from entering.

* Librarians sought to **meet children on their own terms**, acknowledging developmental stages and varied needs.

* Some branches, such as in St. Louis and New York, integrated **storytelling, flower arranging, and even printing presses** into youth programming.

Through these practices, librarians cultivated **peer-based learning** and **agency among young patrons**, anticipating today’s maker spaces.

---

### **4\. Backlash and Decline (1900–1925)**

By the early 20th century, as librarianship professionalized, many leaders grew uneasy with what they saw as the **“experimental temptation”** of playful youth services.

* Critics like **Montrose J. Moses** and **Clara Whitehill Hunt** insisted that children’s rooms should mirror the seriousness of adult departments.

* The push for **efficiency, uniformity, and respectability** (echoing Melvil Dewey’s reforms) led to a retreat from games and visual displays.

* The 1906 ALA survey of children’s services narrowed its focus to **discipline, circulation, and collection management**, sidelining creative practices.

* Picture bulletins and decorative displays were condemned as “inartistic” distractions from books.

By the 1920s, children’s services had become **book-centered, moralistic, and standardized**, losing the vibrant multimodal experimentation of earlier decades.

---

### **5\. Historical Significance and Legacy**

Pierce argues that this forgotten era of playful, sensory engagement reveals that:

* **Games and toys were integral to early library history**, not a 21st-century innovation.

* Librarians once embraced **multimodal and participatory learning**, anticipating modern ideas about gamification, media literacy, and informal education.

* The eventual marginalization of these practices reflected a broader tension between **education as discipline** and **education as creativity**.

Today’s discussions about digital games, apps, and maker spaces **echo the same debates** over whether play belongs in libraries—and whether entertainment supports or undermines learning.

---

### **6\. Key Takeaway**

Burek Pierce concludes that the early 20th-century retreat from games and toys silenced an important tradition of **child-centered, experimental librarianship**.  
 Understanding this history challenges the notion that today’s game-based or multimedia library programs are “new.” Instead, they continue a **long-standing struggle to balance imagination and instruction, play and professionalism** in library service.

---

**In essence:**  
 Between 1876 and 1925, American children’s librarians built playful, multimedia spaces filled with puzzles, maps, toys, and pictures to engage young learners. Their efforts—deeply tied to Progressive Era educational reform—were later suppressed by calls for order and uniformity. Burek Pierce (2016) restores this history, showing that **games and creative play have always been part of the library’s mission to cultivate curiosity, literacy, and joy.**

Autonomous play that fits asynchronous learning models.

Experiential engagement that promotes problem-solving persistence.

Instructors can integrate investigative games into reference courses as supplemental, active-learning activities, particularly where live reference simulations are impractical.  
The games encourage:

Experimentation under uncertainty (parallel to patron interactions).

Reflection on reasoning choices and strategies.

Development of persistence and tolerance for ambiguity—key qualities in reference work.

Some investigative games contain violent or distressing content, so inclusion should be optional.

Students may need explicit connections drawn between gameplay reasoning and RUSA professional competencies.

The approach supplements, but does not replace, live instruction or supervised practice.

Further empirical research is needed to confirm whether such games effectively improve reference intuition.

7\. Key Takeaway

Burgess and Wallace (2021) argue that investigative video games—by simulating hypothesis testing, evidence evaluation, and reflective reasoning—can serve as valuable pedagogical tools for teaching clinical intuition and metacognitive awareness in LIS reference education.  
They bridge the gap between theory and practice, especially in online programs, offering an interactive method for students to internalize the decision-making processes essential to effective reference work.

In essence:

# **Summary of Abigail Chapman (2021), “Trials of Metadata: Emerging Schemas for Videogame Cataloguing”**  ***Journal of Library Metadata, Vol. 21, Nos. 3–4, pp. 63–103.***

---

### **Overview**

Abigail Chapman’s article offers the most comprehensive comparative analysis to date of how **video games are being catalogued** across libraries, museums, and fan communities. As games have gained recognition as cultural artifacts, Chapman argues that **traditional cataloguing standards (like RDA and LCSH)** fail to capture their hybrid nature—simultaneously **digital, physical, visual, and interactive**.  
 The paper compares five major **metadata schemas**—two academic (GAMECIP and VGMS), one community-driven (MobyGames), and two commercial (Steam and Itch.io)—to reveal how each handles descriptive, technical, and genre information.

The study shows that while all schemas share a commitment to greater granularity and multimedia awareness, none fully resolves the challenges of **describing, discovering, and preserving** video games as cultural objects.

---

### **1\. Background and Rationale**

* **Videogames now occupy museums and libraries**, yet cataloguing standards have not kept pace.

* Earlier cataloguing systems such as **AACR2** and **RDA** were designed for books and audiovisual media, making them inadequate for describing games that are:

  * Both **digital and physical**,

  * Highly **interactive**,

  * Continuously updated, and

  * Dependent on specific **hardware platforms**.

* Early practice borrowed from board game cataloguing (“realia”) or machine-readable data files (MRDFs), leading to confusion and inconsistency.

* Fan databases like **MobyGames** and community archives like the **Internet Archive Software Library** filled the gap long before formal guidance appeared.

---

### **2\. Research Purpose and Method**

Chapman compares **five metadata schemas** to assess how they manage **RDA core elements** (title, publisher, edition, carrier, etc.) and genre classification.  
 Schemas analyzed:

1. **GAMECIP Core Metadata Schema (CMS)** – University of California, Santa Cruz (2017)

2. **Video Game Metadata Schema (VGMS)** – University of Washington GAMER Group (2012–2017)

3. **MobyGames** – Crowd-sourced database (1999–present)

4. **Steam** – Commercial digital game distribution platform

5. **Itch.io** – Indie game marketplace and tagging system

The analysis uses **Beall’s three evaluative criteria**:

* Connection to content standards (RDA alignment)

* Granularity of description

* Community/domain specificity

---

### **3\. Findings: RDA Core Elements**

#### **a. Title and Series**

* Games often have **multiple or conflicting titles** across regions and platforms (*The Legend of Zelda: Ocarina of Time/Master Quest*).

* Academic schemas (CMS, VGMS) allow variant, localized, or colloquial titles, while MobyGames privileges English or U.S. release titles.

* **VGMS** introduces a **franchise element**, linking related games beyond publisher series—an important innovation missing from RDA.

#### **b. Statement of Responsibility**

* Traditional authorship is meaningless for games involving dozens of roles (developer, designer, composer, etc.).

* CMS, VGMS, and MobyGames use flexible **“creator” or “agent”** elements rather than RDA’s linear authorship model.

#### **c. Publication Information**

* Publisher and release data are inconsistently recorded across schemas.

* MobyGames offers the **most detailed controlled vocabulary**, tracking publisher names, release countries, and dates.

* Commercial schemas (Steam, Itch.io) emphasize **release dates and updates** over traditional publication details.

#### **d. Edition and Platform**

* Games frequently appear in **multiple editions, versions, and patches**; differentiating them is essential for preservation.

* All schemas include a **platform element** (e.g., PlayStation, Windows), absent from RDA but critical for describing compatibility.

* **CMS and VGMS** distinguish between commercial editions and software versions.

#### **e. Identifiers and Carriers**

* Unlike ISBNs, there is no universal identifier for games (UPC, EAN, etc., vary).

* CMS is the only schema with an explicit identifier element.

* For carriers, GAMECIP and VGMS developed **controlled vocabularies** naming specific formats (e.g., “Nintendo 64 Game Pak”), surpassing RDA’s vague categories.

#### **f. Additional Shared Elements**

* Common to all schemas: **platform, system requirements, developer, summary, and genre**—none treated as “core” in RDA but vital to discovery.

---

### **4\. Genre: The Central Challenge**

Chapman identifies **genre** as the most complex and evolving metadata problem.  
 While RDA and Library of Congress vocabularies include only a few terms (“computer war games,” “educational games”), fan and industry systems use hundreds.

The paper distinguishes **four major approaches to genre**:

| Type | Description | Examples |
| ----- | ----- | ----- |
| **User-generated genres** | Crowdsourced tags on Steam and Itch.io; reflect user perception, mood, or mechanics | “story-rich,” “cute,” “roguelike” |
| **Narrative genres** | Focus on setting, theme, and story type | “fantasy,” “sci-fi,” “comedy,” “war” |
| **Visual genres** | Concerned with aesthetic style and presentation | “2D side-scroller,” “pixel art,” “photorealism” |
| **Gameplay genres** | Defined by player experience and mechanics | “platformer,” “RPG,” “simulation,” “shooter” |

*   
  **VGMS** and **MobyGames** both adopt **multifaceted vocabularies**, allowing multiple genre dimensions.

* VGMS divides genre into gameplay, visual, and mood-related categories, while MobyGames includes theme, setting, and gameplay.

* User-tag systems (Steam, Itch.io) are flexible and culturally rich but inconsistent and unsuitable for formal catalogues.

* Chapman suggests that future **Library of Congress Genre/Form Terms (LCGFT)** could draw from these hybrid taxonomies.

---

### **5\. Discussion and Implications**

#### **Strengths of Emerging Schemas**

* Provide **medium-specific detail** missing in RDA.

* Incorporate **controlled vocabularies** for platform, media type, and genre.

* Embrace **granularity and flexibility**, useful for both discovery and research.

* Facilitate crosswalks between **library and community data ecosystems** (e.g., linking MobyGames with Internet Archive records).

#### **Remaining Gaps**

* Lack of elements for **physical packaging, peripherals, or manuals**.

* Inconsistent treatment of **identifiers** and **accessibility details** (e.g., multiplayer, rating systems).

* Need for standardized vocabularies for **narrative and visual styles**.

* Difficulty of maintaining metadata as games **evolve through patches and updates**.

---

### **6\. Conclusions and Future Directions**

Chapman concludes that video games **redefine cataloguing itself**, demanding schemas that reflect their **hybrid technological, artistic, and participatory nature**.  
 The most promising direction is **faceted metadata**—a model that integrates bibliographic, aesthetic, and gameplay attributes while maintaining interoperability with RDA.  
 She predicts that:

* Folksonomies like Steam tags will continue shaping terminology.

* Libraries and museums must collaborate with fan and commercial archives.

* Metadata must adapt to **new forms like virtual and augmented reality**, ensuring long-term cultural preservation.

---

**In essence:**  
 Chapman (2021) demonstrates that video games challenge every assumption of traditional library metadata—from authorship to genre. Emerging schemas such as **GAMECIP, VGMS, and MobyGames** represent crucial steps toward a **new, multifaceted cataloguing paradigm** that combines academic rigor with community-driven vocabulary to make games discoverable, citable, and preservable as 21st-century cultural artifacts.
