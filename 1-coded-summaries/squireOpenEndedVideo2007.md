## Summary

Squire develops a model of game-based learning around open-ended simulation, or "sandbox," games. Rather than treating all video games as one instructional medium, he distinguishes targeted games, professional role-playing games, augmented-reality experiences, persistent worlds, and open-ended simulations by factors such as time scale, replayability, creative expression, and the number of viable solution paths. His central claim is that different game forms support different learning processes and therefore require different theories of design and assessment.

The chapter contrasts targeted games such as *Supercharged!* with sandbox games such as *Civilization III* and *Grand Theft Auto: San Andreas*. A targeted game can help players construct a particular conceptual model, but it may offer few pathways beyond its intended domain. Open-ended games instead invite players to understand a system, try multiple ways of acting within it, and use it for creative expression. Their educational value lies less in delivering a common sequence or correct answer than in recruiting diverse interests and supporting different trajectories through a possibility space. The same game may become a historical simulation, competitive arena, design environment, or context for social interpretation, depending on the player and community.

Squire synthesizes observations and interviews from game cultures, schools, camps, and after-school programs to describe learning as a designed experience. Players enter with different knowledge, interests, and identities; develop increasingly specialized expertise; interpret events with other players; and sometimes move outward toward history, politics, game design, teaching, or other practices. He calls attention to two processes. "Induction" brings newcomers into a community by making valued roles and expert identities visible. "Propulsion" helps participants carry emerging interests into new communities of practice. Both require mentoring. Advanced participants, teachers, parents, and facilitators help learners recognize opportunities, use design tools, connect play to books or school topics, teach others, and form new pathways beyond the game.

The chapter's cases also show why engagement is not automatic. Some students do not recognize expert gaming identities as valuable, and school goals or schedules may conflict with long, divergent play. Open-ended games produce varied outcomes because players begin from different positions, choose different strategies, and interpret events through different communities. Even single-player games are socially mediated through friends, websites, discussion, and shared genre knowledge. Squire therefore proposes learning environments that combine consequential systems, multiple readings, creative production, interpretive communities, multi-age participation, and sustained facilitation.

For the larger GBLS project, this model is especially useful for recurring clubs and open-ended game programs. It suggests that libraries should select game forms according to service purpose rather than treating "a video game program" as a uniform intervention. Sandbox games may be appropriate when the goal is systems thinking, participant agency, creative production, or long-term interest development, but they demand time, technical access, mentoring, and tolerance for divergent outcomes. Libraries can also provide propulsion: a game session can lead into collections, history, design software, media criticism, peer leadership, or community-created scenarios. Assessment should document trajectories, artifacts, changing participation, and transfer opportunities rather than expecting all participants to acquire the same facts.

## Productive Incongruences and Challenges

**Category mismatch:** `Game_Format` has `video_game` and `serious_game` but no term for the source's key design category: open-ended simulation or sandbox game. Genre labels based on subject matter do not capture the relevant affordances of replayability, multiple solution paths, system experimentation, and creative expression. **Proposal:** add `sandbox_game` or `open_ended_simulation` to `Game_Format`, with a definition based on interaction structure rather than setting.

**Category mismatch:** The schema cannot directly code `trajectory_of_participation`, `induction`, `propulsion`, `mentoring`, or `interpretive_community`. These are not minor themes; they constitute the chapter's learning model. **Proposal:** consider `mentoring` and `identity` as `Theme` values and describe participation trajectories in narrative coding even if they are not added to the controlled lexicon.

**Evidentiary challenge:** The values framework correctly asks assessment to follow purpose, but open-ended play complicates any expectation of common outcomes. Squire argues that divergence is a defining affordance rather than measurement noise. **Proposal:** qualify outcome evaluation for sandbox programs so that participant-defined trajectories, artifacts, and movement into new practices can count alongside predefined learning measures.

**Outline tension:** The argument joins `Clubs and Recurring Play Groups`, `Game Design Workshops, Jams, and Participatory Creation`, and `Design for Reflection, Learning Transfer, and Participant Agency`. **Proposal:** cross-reference these sections to show that sustained play can become production and peer leadership when programs deliberately build pathways.

## Evidence Cautions and Limitations

The chapter combines theory, prior studies, and illustrative cases rather than presenting a single fully specified empirical design. Sample sizes, recruitment, and analytic procedures for the focal participants are not reported in enough detail here to support broad causal claims. The author marks several conclusions as hypotheses or early-stage design propositions, and the examples are tied to particular games and early-2000s educational contexts. The attached file is a 2007 manuscript under consideration; the citation above reflects the published 2008 chapter, while the stable Zotero citation key retains 2007.
