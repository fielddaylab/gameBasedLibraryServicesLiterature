[(Burek Pierce, 2016\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?JZ8xxf) explores a particular era, between 1876 and 1925, where influence from progressive thinking in education led to the development of child centric spaces and materials being offered by public libraries, focusing on puzzles, toys and games. # Defining GBLS

For this study, Games‑based Library Services (GBLS) refers to library experiences or offerings directly centered around modern gaming media — including video games, board games, tabletop role‑playing games (TTRPGs), virtual and augmented reality games (VR and ARGs), and collectible or competitive card games.

“Library services” includes any offerings intended for library users, such as in‑person or virtual programs, circulating or in‑library‑use collections, displays, take‑home kits, giveaways, and similar activities.

### Digital vs Analog Game Debate

Some authors explicitly push back against a default emphasis on digital games, arguing that libraries are structurally better equipped to support analog, co-present, collaborative play that leverages the library’s strengths as a non-commercial, socially inclusive public space. [(Seay, 2018a)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?8smmh5), for example, contends that immersive analog formats (e.g., escape rooms, educational LARPs, megagames) better align with how people learn and remember (through embodied, synchronous group problem-solving) and position libraries less as sites for screen-based activities and more as hosts of shared, participatory experiences.

Copeland et al. (2013) provide a concrete school-library account of why tabletop games remain especially useful in library settings. Because tabletop games are relatively inexpensive, portable, durable, and adaptable, they can be used across a wider range of classroom and informal contexts than many digital games. The authors also note that tabletop games require players to operate the mechanics manually rather than relying on a computer to administer the system. This can make the structure of the game more visible to learners, allowing students to adapt rules, collaborate in teams, adjust pacing, and better understand how the game works. In this sense, tabletop games are not simply lower-tech substitutes for digital games; they offer distinct affordances for social, curricular, and reflective learning.

### Games vs. Gamification Debate

Scholars in game studies and library science draw a clear conceptual distinction between games and gamification, a distinction that has important implications for learning-oriented institutions such as libraries. [(Seay, 2018b)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?9zAdzt) argues that gamification involves the application of discrete game elements such as points, badges, leaderboards, and progress tracking to non-game activities in order to motivate participation, whereas games are complete, rule-governed systems that create meaning through sustained play and internal coherence. This position aligns with Ralph Koster’s influential definition of games as systems that make learning enjoyable by enabling players to recognize and master patterns within a bounded rule set [(Koster, 2003\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?SGLXcr), and with James Paul Gee’s account of games as sites of situated, embodied learning in which players adopt identities, test hypotheses, and learn through failure and feedback within meaningful contexts [(Gee, 2007\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?o1VOud). Gee explicitly cautions that extrinsic reward structures alone do not produce deep learning, arguing instead that games teach because they embed knowledge in action and social practice. Similarly, scholars of gamification such as Sebastian Deterding et al. [(Deterding et al., 2011\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?9TLSr5) note that gamification abstracts game mechanics from games themselves, often prioritizing behavioral compliance over experiential meaning. Taken together, these authors suggest that while gamification can be effective for short-term engagement or task completion, it risks instrumentalizing play if divorced from the epistemic and social qualities that define games proper. For libraries, institutions historically oriented toward inquiry, intrinsic motivation, and the public good, this literature implies that game-based experiences grounded in play, identity, and collaboration may be more congruent with library values than gamified systems primarily designed to communicate metrics for participation.

### Existing Library Information Systems

Existing data structures such as Machine Readable Cataloging (MARC) are flexible enough to store useful data about games, even if it doesn’t describe a particular grammar. For example, [(McCulloch, 2017\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?iUqAFM) suggests treating board games as realia (Leader/06 \= r) and using Library of Congress Genre and Form Terms (LCGFT) genre terms (e.g. Board Games” within the 655 field, detailed physical inventories of parts within the 300 field, and key attributes such as the number of players and duration of play within the uncontrolled 500 notes. [(Hobart, 2024\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?3sK664) focuses on three-dimensional, physical game artifacts held in a special collections context, such as paper toys, card games, and boxed games from the 19th and early 20th centuries. They demonstrate a pragmatic hybrid descriptive approach by using Resource Description and Access (RDA) and MARC as the structural framework for item-level records while selectively incorporating Online Audiovisual Catalogers (OLAC)’s object cataloging guidance and the principles of Descriptive Cataloging of Rare Materials (DCRM) to meet special collections needs. In practice, this means treating games as three-dimensional artifacts (e.g., Leader/06 is coded as “realia” as with McCulloch), providing detailed physical component inventories, and relying heavily on notes to document copy-specific features, supplied information, and evidence-based conjecture. Because no single standard adequately supports games, Hobart supplements underdeveloped vocabularies (such as LCGFT) with additional genre terms and adjusts the discovery layer to collocate games and toys meaningfully.

### Challenges to Cataloging Games

There is significant literature describing the challenges in library cataloging system that are primarily grounded in describing printed texts and audiovisual materials and struggle to adequately describe game systems such as tabletop roleplaying games [(Smith, 2024\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?0rpklE) that contain multiple structures (rulebooks, dice, miniatures, etc.) and digital games [(Chapman, 2021\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?AXf8xL) that are simultaneously digital, physical, visual, and interactive.

For example, they identify genre as the most complex and evolving metadata problem, with widely adopted library information vocabularies such as AACR2, RDA, and LCGFT including only a few terms (“computer war games,” “educational games”), while fan and industry systems use hundreds. When games are approached as artifacts, the vocabulary provided by Getty Art & Architecture Thesaurus (AAT) is able to describe the individual physical elements (e.g. miniatures, dice) but falls short in describing their collection together in a game as a coherent procedural and textual work.

Lee and colleagues at the University of Washington Information School have produced a sustained, programmatic body of research demonstrating that existing library cataloging systems are fundamentally inadequate for describing and providing access to video games as cultural and scholarly objects (e.g. Lee et al., 2012; Lee et al., 2013). Through domain analysis, user-centered schema design, and empirical cataloging exercises conducted in partnership with the Seattle Interactive Media Museum, this work shows that standards developed for books and other static media fail to capture the interactive, versioned, and platform-dependent nature of games. Rather than treating these limitations as edge cases, Lee et al. argue that they are structural, requiring new conceptual models, core metadata elements, and controlled vocabularies specifically designed for games.

Clarke et al. (2014) extend this UW metadata research agenda by focusing specifically on relationships among video games. They argue that existing conceptual models such as FRBR are poorly suited to the complex ways games relate to one another across editions, platforms, series, distribution methods, expansions, downloadable content, adaptations, and influence. This is not a marginal problem. Games are often released across multiple platforms, reissued in special editions, expanded through downloadable content, connected to larger franchises, and surrounded by related media such as soundtracks, strategy guides, art books, anime, films, and spin-offs. As a result, the descriptive unit of a “game” is often unstable. A PlayStation release, an arcade release, a digital release, and a later expanded edition may share a title while differing in source code, interface, content, visual presentation, or gameplay experience. Clarke et al. therefore show that game cataloging requires conceptual models capable of representing relationships, not only isolated items.

Reasons existing cataloging systems are insufficient (as identified by Lee et al. and related UW research):

* Games lack stable “chief sources of information” analogous to title pages, undermining traditional descriptive rules.  
* Bibliographic models (e.g., FRBR-based approaches) struggle to represent the multiplicity of editions, platforms, patches, and regional variants inherent to games.  
* Genre vocabularies are inconsistent, marketing-driven, and too coarse to support meaningful discovery or comparison.  
* Existing standards do not adequately represent gameplay characteristics, interactivity, or player experience.  
* Release date, publisher, and developer information is often unreliable or conflicting across sources. Traditional authorship categories do not capture the dozens of roles (writer, level designer, game engine programmer, composer, etc.) seen in game projects.  
* Unlike ISBNs, there is no universal identifier for games (UPC, EAN, etc.).   
* Commercial databases prioritize popularity and promotion over authority, verification, and long-term access.  
* Traditional cataloging assumes static objects, whereas games are dynamic systems that change over time.

This leads to games being incorrectly grouped with fiction and criticism texts about games, and different types of games being grouped together (tabletop roleplaying with RPG video games for example). 

### Discoverability

Discovery of materials is a core mission of the library, yet several researchers find the existing library information system insufficient [(Lee et al., 2012, 2013, 2016; Robson et al., 2019\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?vhxH6D).

When schemas and discovery systems are poorly aligned with how people actually find and assess video games, which users instead discover through peer networks, community platforms, storefront ecosystems, and observational media such as gameplay videos and streams. This suggests that library cataloging should incorporate more user-centered, faceted descriptors and interoperable links to community knowledge structures that represent gameplay, platform/versioning, and social evaluation signals, rather than relying primarily on traditional bibliographic description. Unfortunately, existing commercial game databases provide abundant but inconsistent, uncontrolled, and often unreliable metadata [(Lee et al., 2013\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?mlGNnt).

In 2015, Lee et al. [(Lee et al., 2015\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?4EHK2O) conducted an empirical evaluation of video game metadata by having LIS-affiliated catalogers create records for a sample of games (with each title independently cataloged by two catalogers) and then analyzing discrepancies between coders to identify where consistency and “entity-level” decisions break down, particularly for multi-platform and digitally distributed games. They complemented this cataloging analysis with user research, interviews and a large-scale survey, to keep the evaluation anchored in discoverability and real user information needs rather than bibliographic convention alone. With a discoverability focus in mind, they found that users prioritize highly practical access points (especially platform and price) alongside experiential descriptors (genre/style, theme/plot/mood), relationships among games (series/franchise), and visual surrogates (notably gameplay video), while some fine-grained or package-centric elements were less useful relative to the effort required.

Clarke et al. (2014) extend this UW metadata research agenda by focusing specifically on relationships among video games. They argue that existing conceptual models such as FRBR are poorly suited to the complex ways games relate to one another across editions, platforms, series, distribution methods, expansions, downloadable content, adaptations, and influence. This is not a marginal problem. Games are often released across multiple platforms, reissued in special editions, expanded through downloadable content, connected to larger franchises, and surrounded by related media such as soundtracks, strategy guides, art books, anime, films, and spin-offs. As a result, the descriptive unit of a “game” is often unstable. A PlayStation release, an arcade release, a digital release, and a later expanded edition may share a title while differing in source code, interface, content, visual presentation, or gameplay experience. Clarke et al. therefore show that game cataloging requires conceptual models capable of representing relationships, not only isolated items.

Robson et al. (2019) [(Robson et al., 2019\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?hEbRXW) extend the video-game metadata and discoverability agenda associated with Lee and colleagues by applying it to tabletop games in an academic library context, showing how board games require domain-specific access points that traditional catalogs do not readily support. Working at the University of North Texas Libraries, they demonstrate that libraries can pragmatically develop local genre vocabularies and structured fields for key decision criteria (e.g., number of players, play time, recommended age) while national standards and controlled vocabularies remain incomplete. They argue that cataloging improvements alone do not solve discoverability. In contrast, effective access depends on a socio-technical approach that couples rich metadata with end-user facing tools (in their case, search and filtering systems based on Apache Solr and Blacklight facets interfaces) and cross-unit collaboration so that the interface actually exposes game-relevant filters to users.

## Purposes for Games Collections and Services

### Using Games to Teach Library Facilities, Services, and Information Literacy

A substantial body of library and information science literature documents the persistent challenges students face in understanding library spaces, services, and information literacy concepts. These challenges are often described in terms of library anxiety, limited time for instruction, and uneven student engagement with traditional lecture-based orientations [(Ariya et al., 2025; Capdarest-Arest et al., 2019; Sureephong et al., 2024\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?tn3x0P). In response, librarians have increasingly explored game-based approaches as pedagogical tools for introducing students to library environments and practices. Across this literature, games are framed not only as novelty interventions but as structured learning activities that leverage interaction, exploration, and social play to reduce anxiety, increase familiarity, and support information literacy learning.

#### Games for Library Orientation and Spatial Literacy

Several recent studies focus specifically on the use of games to teach students how to navigate library facilities and become aware of available services. Chen et al. [(2024)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?HjJSiJ) examine an augmented reality (AR) navigation game implemented in a large academic library. Their study combines AR technology with gamification elements such as points, rewards, and goal-based challenges. Findings indicate that participants reported significantly higher enjoyment, increased self-efficacy in navigation tasks, and reduced navigation-related anxiety compared to non-gamified approaches. The authors argue that the integration of spatial exploration with game mechanics transforms what is often a stressful or overwhelming experience into one that supports confidence-building and curiosity-driven learning.

Jedlicka and Eaton [(Jedlicka & Eaton, 2024\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?tPMw9B) similarly address library anxiety and spatial awareness through a mobile, browser-based AR scavenger hunt developed at Kingsborough Community College Library (CUNY). Their open-source design emphasized accessibility and ease of deployment, allowing students to explore library spaces, services, and points of access through guided challenges. Assessment results showed that students found the activity engaging and that it increased awareness of underutilized locations and services. However, the authors report a critical implementation finding: participation and completion rates were low when the game was offered as a voluntary, standalone activity. When embedded within course-linked library instruction sessions, completion rates rose to near universal levels. This result underscores a recurring theme in the literature—that games are most effective when integrated into curricular or instructional contexts rather than positioned as optional add-ons.

Analog game designs have also been shown to effectively support orientation and service awareness, particularly in contexts with limited time or staffing. Based on lessons learned facilitating an Alternate Reality Game [(Giles, 2015\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?VN1X0l) with similar goals but a different format, Giles et al. [(2019)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?UrEt9L) describe the development and assessment of a low-cost, Apples to Apples–style card game used during transfer student orientation at James Madison University Libraries. Designed to replace a lecture-based session, the game emphasized small-group interaction, humor, and peer discussion. Survey data demonstrated overwhelmingly positive responses: more than 90 percent of participants reported that the game was both enjoyable and helpful, and a majority indicated that it increased their comfort level with library spaces. The authors argue that analog, social games are particularly well suited to orientation contexts because they simultaneously convey practical information and foster a sense of belonging, especially for students who may not otherwise attend traditional orientations.

Other studies explore more experimental game formats while also highlighting practical constraints. Watson et al. (2024) present a tabletop roleplaying game proof of concept designed to teach navigation and services at the J.D. Williams Library at the University of Mississippi. While the project generated interest among librarians and demonstrated conceptual promise, the authors report that the development effort required significant time and labor and ultimately had to be dramatically scaled back. The game was never released to a public audience. This case illustrates an all-to-common counterpoint within the literature: while immersive and narrative-rich game designs can align well with learning objectives, their sustainability depends heavily on institutional capacity, development resources, staff time and staff artistry.

#### Information Literacy Instruction

Beyond orientation and spatial literacy, games have also been widely used to teach information literacy concepts such as plagiarism, citation practices, and academic integrity. Horban and Skachenko [(Horban & Skachenko, 2019\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?9WIl5B) provide a comparative review of twelve digital games developed by academic libraries for information literacy instruction. Focusing on plagiarism prevention, they identify common design features across successful implementations, including clear goals, immediate feedback, and progressively increasing challenges. Across the reviewed cases, the authors report broadly positive instructional outcomes, suggesting that game-based approaches can effectively support conceptual understanding when aligned with well-defined learning objectives.

This finding is consistent with earlier and parallel work examining the relationship between library use and information literacy development. Studies by Brown and Kasper [(2013)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?OW1GVr), Capdarest-Arest et al. [(2019)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?Jobfzq), Burgess and Wallace [(Burgess & Wallace, 2021\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?kABdv2), Abrams and Van Eck [(Abrams & Van Eck, 2021\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?deMRIJ), and Adams [(Adams, 2009\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?8Ry3nm) collectively demonstrate that students’ familiarity with library services and spaces is strongly associated with improved confidence and competence in information-seeking behaviors. Game-based interventions are a mechanism through which libraries can accelerate library familiarity by creating playful, low-risk opportunities for exploration and practice.

Cho et al. [(2023)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?WwWpMa) extend the literature on game-based information literacy by showing how library games can cultivate epistemic resilience, not merely source-evaluation skills. Their public-library escape room placed participants inside a fictional misinformation scenario involving misleading headlines, manipulated charts, deepfakes, bots, and social media sharing. The most important design feature was not simply the puzzle structure, but the combination of immersive play, collaborative problem-solving, and a librarian-led debrief that helped participants reflect on vulnerability, confirmation bias, and the consequences of sharing misinformation. In this sense, the program reframes information literacy as a civic and social practice: patrons do not simply learn how to identify misinformation, but rehearse how to think, pause, revise, and recover in an unstable information environment.

### Games Supporting Libraries as a Public Commons

Games and play have long been understood as central to social life, learning, and community formation rather than as peripheral or purely recreational activities. Classic cultural theory positions play as a foundational mode through which societies generate meaning, norms, and shared practices. Huizinga’s Homo Ludens [(1950)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?ueUH7c) argues that play precedes culture and underwrites the development of law, art, and civic ritual, while Putnam [(2000)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?FMWLF5) later demonstrates that shared social activities are critical to the formation of social capital. Within libraries, game-based programming extends this lineage by offering structured yet voluntary forms of participation that encourage interaction, cooperation, and informal learning across age groups in a shared public context. Danilovic and de Voogt (2021) argue that many “classic” board games are unusually portable across cultures because their formal rules can be shared independently of specific cultural meanings. This may help explain the enduring popularity of games such as chess, checkers, and mancala in library settings, where patrons from different backgrounds can participate through a common rule system.

One way to describe this affordance of libraries is through taking up the notion of libraries as “third places,” social environments distinct from home and work that support belonging, civic life, and informal association [(Oldenburg, 1989\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?JTx1CB). Empirical studies of libraries reinforce this framing, showing that they function as inclusive community anchors where intergenerational interaction is not only possible but routine [(Fisher et al., 2016\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?Q9aPvu). Game-based programming intensifies this role by creating reasons for people to gather, remain, and interact over time. Through play, libraries are a space for cultivating shared experiences that strengthen a sense of place, reinforcing their role as a public commons rather than transactional service points. This argument is reinforced by Wyatt and Leorke [(2024)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?t7rSn6), who extend third-place theory to describe contemporary public libraries as active public commons. Drawing on observational and programmatic evidence, they show how game-based activities support co-presence, informal interaction, and civic belonging across generations. Rather than supplementing information services, play becomes a core mechanism for sustaining democratic social life in a community. Within this public context, Nicholson [(2010)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?j2rArt) argues that games should be understood not primarily as media objects but as designed social experiences that can be intentionally aligned with library goals such as inclusion and community building. 

Libraries as a public space, a context for encountering others outside of one’s own curate private.

Games also intersect with the library’s long-standing mission to address inequities in access, particularly in relation to technology and digital participation. Libraries increasingly function as mediators of the digital divide by providing not only access to devices and the internet but also librarian led support in using those resources [(Jaeger et al., 2012\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?ivHqQR). Game-based programs can serve as low-stakes entry points into digital literacy, computational thinking, and media fluency, especially when participation is scaffolded by librarians rather than left to market-driven platforms that require payment for access. At the same time, the literature is clear that game-based programming is not inherently equitable. Games are cultural artifacts embedded within broader social structures, and participation patterns often reproduce existing inequalities. Farmer’s [(2011)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?2qUK1b) review of research across education, gender studies, and the game industry demonstrates persistent gender-based participation gaps, with girls playing less frequently, for shorter durations, and disengaging more readily after negative early experiences. Farmer argues that libraries have an ethical responsibility to intervene, not by simply offering access, but by curating diverse game genres, designing inclusive programs, and actively scaffolding participation. Equity, in this account, is produced through intentional design and facilitation.

Contemporary library scholarship increasingly frames libraries not merely as repositories of information but as active learning environments that support knowledge creation. The Association of College and Research Libraries articulates this shift clearly in *The Value of Academic Libraries* and related framework documents which define information literacy as engagement in the “discovery of information in all formats” and emphasize environments that foster “collaborative learning and the creation of new knowledge” (ACRL 2011). This reconceptualization expands the scope of legitimate library practice beyond textual and database-centered resources to include experiential multimodal and participatory forms of learning. Within this framework programming is not ancillary but central to how libraries fulfill their educational and civic missions.

Coonan (2019) similarly emphasizes a movement beyond the library as a bounded instructional site toward the everyday spaces in which people encounter, test, share, and co-create knowledge. Her discussion of information literacy across public, school, professional, and gaming contexts reinforces the idea that libraries support civic and intellectual life not only by providing access to information, but by cultivating spaces where inquiry and interpretation can be practiced socially. This aligns with GBLS insofar as play creates participatory situations in which patrons learn through interaction, experimentation, and shared meaning-making.

This orientation aligns closely with constructivist and sociocultural learning theories that emphasize interest-driven peer-supported learning across formal and informal contexts. Ito et al (2013) describe learning as emerging through participation in shared practices rather than through isolated skill acquisition arguing that environments which support experimentation, collaboration, and production are especially powerful for youth and lifelong learners. Libraries figure prominently in this work as uniquely positioned institutions that can host such environments without the assessment pressures of schools or the commercial imperatives of digital platforms. From this perspective game-based programming represents a particularly robust instantiation of connected learning principles by integrating interest play social interaction and iterative meaning-making within a public setting.

Lankes (2016) provides a unifying theoretical lens by asserting that “the mission of librarians is to improve society through facilitating knowledge creation in their communities.” This formulation explicitly centers libraries as platforms for communal knowledge production rather than service points for information delivery. Game-based programming fits squarely within this vision insofar as it creates conditions for collective inquiry experimentation and reflection in non-commercial public space. Taken together these works establish a coherent foundation for understanding games in libraries not as entertainment add-ons but as legitimate and strategically aligned practices through which libraries operationalize discovery collaboration and the creation of new knowledge within the public commons.

Related scholarship emphasizes that the form of games matters as much as their content. Seay (2018) contends that libraries should prioritize analog and tabletop games as a deliberate counterweight to the persuasive and extractive logics embedded in many digital platforms. Analog games emphasize face-to-face interaction, shared physical space, and tactile engagement, aligning with patrons’ expectations of libraries as human-centered environments. These characteristics reinforce the library’s identity as a non-commercial commons, distinguishing it from entertainment venues governed by attention economies and surveillance-based monetization (Zuboff, 2019). Supporting this interpretation, Cross et al. (2023) found that social interaction was the strongest motivation for board game play among 1,603 hobby gamers, exceeding competition and reinforcing the social character of tabletop gaming communities.

Recent qualitative work further connects game-based programming to democratic practice. Nelson and Matthews (2025), in an in-depth study of role-playing game programs at two public libraries, argue that library-facilitated Dungeons & Dragons functions as a form of “democratic rehearsal.” Participants experiment with cooperation, authority, and difference within a low-risk, collectively governed environment. The library context reduces gatekeeping, lowers financial and social barriers, and sustains engagement over time, enabling forms of participation that are difficult to achieve in commercial or school-based settings. Play, in this sense, becomes a practical enactment of democratic values rather than a symbolic gesture.

Summarizing, this body of literature positions game-based programming as a critical strategy through which libraries operationalize their role as public commons. Games support social learning (Putnam, 2000; Huizinga, 1938), reinforce the library as a third place (Oldenburg, 1999; Fisher et al., 2007), address digital inequities (Boyle, 2018), and align with professional mandates for discovery and collaborative knowledge creation (ACRL, 2011). When designed with attention to equity, modality, and facilitation, game-based programs do not merely entertain—they enable libraries to model inclusive, democratic, and participatory forms of public life.

The Trinity High School board game club described by Copeland et al. (2013) illustrates how tabletop gaming can support the library as a social commons. The club attracted students who were less likely to participate in traditional extracurricular activities such as sports and created a safe, sociable after-school space for play. Students reported that the club gave them access to games they did not have at home, people to play with, and someone who could explain the rules. Henderson observes that the club helped students develop cross-year friendships, practice turn-taking, negotiate rules, manage winning and losing, and build social confidence. In this account, tabletop gaming is not only recreation; it is a low-cost structure for belonging, peer learning, and social development within the school library.

### Expanding the Library’s Cultural Mandate

Libraries have historically expanded their cultural mandate by incorporating new media forms as they emerge, moving beyond print to include film, recorded music, digital media, and, more recently, makerspaces. Scholarship on games and play situates digital and analog games squarely within this trajectory, arguing that games are not merely entertainment objects but culturally expressive media that shape how people understand systems, values, and power. Within media and game studies, games are widely understood as *cultural artifacts*, designed systems that encode social assumptions and invite critical interpretation (Consalvo, 2007; Zimmerman, 2015). From this perspective, the inclusion of games in library collections and programming represents a continuation of libraries’ long-standing role as stewards of contemporary culture and facilitators of media literacy broadly.

Consalvo’s (2007) work on gaming cultures demonstrates that games function as sites of meaning-making where players negotiate identity, ideology, and social norms. Rather than treating games as isolated products, she emphasizes the broader cultural ecosystems surrounding them, including communities of play, paratexts, and interpretive practices. This framing aligns closely with library approaches to media literacy, which emphasize not only access but also critical engagement with how media shape understanding. Zimmerman (2015) similarly positions games as central to twenty-first-century culture, arguing that play and game design are increasingly dominant ways through which societies model systems, experiment with rules, and imagine alternatives. His argument that we are entering a “ludic century” underscores the cultural relevance of games as media that warrant preservation, interpretation, and public access.

Within library and information science, Buchanan and Vanden Elzen (2012) explicitly connect these theoretical claims to library missions. Writing in *Library Technology Reports*, they argue that video games are legitimate cultural and educational media that align with libraries’ core commitments to access, learning, and intellectual freedom. Drawing parallels to earlier debates over the inclusion of popular fiction, film, and recorded music, they contend that resistance to games often reflects generational bias rather than principled distinctions about educational value. Importantly, they emphasize that libraries are uniquely positioned to contextualize games—through programming, facilitation, and collection development—in ways that encourage reflection rather than passive consumption.

This argument mirrors earlier moments in library history. The incorporation of film collections in the mid-twentieth century and music collections in the late twentieth century similarly prompted concerns about legitimacy, entertainment versus education, and appropriate use of public resources. In each case, libraries ultimately embraced these media as culturally significant forms that supported literacy, creativity, and public engagement. More recently, makerspaces have followed a comparable trajectory, reframing libraries as sites of participatory cultural production rather than solely consumption. Games fit naturally within this lineage, functioning as both expressive artifacts and participatory systems that invite exploration, critique, and collaborative meaning-making.

Specific game examples illustrate how games can operate as objects of cultural inquiry within library contexts. *September 12th* (Newsgaming, 2003\) is a minimalist browser-based game designed as political critique rather than entertainment. Through a simple mechanic—launching missiles at terrorists in a Middle Eastern village—the game exposes the logic of militarism by ensuring that civilian casualties generate new enemies. Mourning figures transform into terrorists, making visible the cycle of violence embedded in the “war on terror.” Frequently cited in discussions of newsgames and procedural rhetoric, *September 12th* demonstrates how games can function as editorial commentary, aligning with libraries’ roles in supporting civic literacy and critical engagement with current events.

More complex commercial titles likewise offer rich opportunities for cultural analysis. *Beyond Good & Evil* situates players within a science-fiction narrative centered on media ethics, propaganda, and resistance. Players assume the role of Jade, a photojournalist who documents state violence and exposes government corruption, using photography as both a gameplay mechanic and a narrative device. Scholars and critics have highlighted the game’s interrogation of surveillance, truth, and the politics of representation, positioning it as an example of how mainstream games can engage with themes traditionally associated with documentary film or investigative journalism. For libraries, such games offer opportunities to support critical media literacy by treating games as texts that can be read, discussed, and contextualized alongside other cultural works.

Synthesized, this literature supports the conclusion that incorporating games into library collections and programming represents an expansion, not a departure, of libraries’ cultural mandate. Games function as contemporary cultural artifacts that reflect and shape social values, provide platforms for critique and experimentation, and invite participatory forms of literacy. As libraries continue to redefine their roles in relation to evolving media landscapes, games offer a powerful medium through which libraries can sustain their commitments to access, learning, and democratic culture.

### Programs inside of Games

Since the mid-2000s, library and information science scholarship has intermittently explored whether libraries can operate within game worlds and immersive virtual environments, rather than merely using games as instructional tools or programming content. This line of inquiry emerges from a broader reconceptualization of libraries as social and civic institutions and optimism about virtual worlds as persistent, shared social spaces.

The most sustained early experimentation occurred within a virtual environment called Second Life, where librarians created avatar-mediated library spaces offering reference services, programs, exhibits, and professional gatherings. Bell and Trueman’s Virtual Worlds, Real Libraries [(Bell & Trueman, 2008\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?BKCZC6) remains the foundational account of this period, documenting how academic and public librarians treated Second Life environments as experimental library branches rather than as games. Empirical studies of Second Life libraries further examined user information needs [(Chow et al., 2012\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?MFHxhj), librarian identity and professional practice [(Mon, 2012\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?goMlav), and the pedagogical affordances of presence-based interaction in immersive environments [(Bronack et al., n.d.; Webber & Nahl, 2011\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?yC9bFO)

Across this literature, findings suggest that virtual libraries in Second Life were effective as niche, high-engagement spaces, particularly for professional development, distance education, and experimental instruction. However, subsequent reflective and evaluative work highlights persistent structural challenges, including high labor demands, technical barriers for users, platform instability, and difficulty sustaining broad public participation once novelty diminished [(Bell & Trueman, 2008\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?pnchLD). By the early 2010s, many Second Life library initiatives had been discontinued, and the literature increasingly framed them as historically significant experiments rather than scalable service models.

More recently, interest in immersive environments has re-emerged alongside renewed discourse on the “metaverse” and social virtual reality platforms. Hollister’s exploratory study of Meta Horizon Worlds provides the most current, systematic examination of libraries operating within a contemporary VR platform [(Hollister, 2024\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?jtzaZY). Using unobtrusive VR-based observation and qualitative content analysis, Hollister identified 44 library-themed worlds, categorizing them as art and asset libraries, community libraries, personal libraries, or library-aesthetic social and game spaces. Crucially, the study finds that few of these virtual libraries are formally affiliated with real-world libraries, and that most prioritize socializing, cultural expression, or creative reuse of assets over traditional library services such as reference or access to digital collections.

Taken together, the literature indicates a clear shift over time. Early work imagined game worlds and virtual environments as potential sites of librarianship itself, while more recent research frames them as cultural and social infrastructures that libraries may utilize. Rather than replicating full library services within games, contemporary examples emphasize participation, community expression, and the affective power of library space and aesthetics. As [(Hollister, 2024\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?mrqi2z) concludes, virtual libraries in the metaverse may represent an opportunity for future growth, but their viability depends on platform stability, institutional commitment, and a realistic alignment between library values and the alignment with the affordances and creators of the technologies.

# Lessons Learned about Designing Programming

## Barriers for Videogame programming adoption and facilitation

Abrams and Van Eck (2021) show that videogame programming depends on both institutional resources and professional beliefs. First-order barriers include funding, equipment, training, space, staffing, and administrative support. Second-order barriers include librarian confidence, assumptions about the value of videogames, and perceptions of teen behavior. This distinction is important for GBLS because it shows that successful programming requires more than purchasing games or consoles. Libraries must also build staff capacity, address internal skepticism, and design programming models that support the kinds of literacies games make possible.

## **Understand audience needs** 

The strongest examples of game-based library services begin with a clearly defined purpose and a grounded understanding of the intended audience. Libraries use games for many different reasons: to orient students to library spaces, teach information literacy, attract new audiences, develop local community, support wellness, circulate culturally significant media, or create opportunities for informal learning. Because these goals differ, the same game format will not serve every purpose equally well.

Farrell, Neeser, and Bishoff (2017) argue that academic libraries should begin with local needs assessment rather than assumptions about what game services users want. At the University of Minnesota, librarians initially assumed that the library could serve as a casual recreational gaming space. Low turnout revealed a mismatch between the proposed service and the campus context, where gaming clubs already existed and many students were commuters. In response, the library shifted toward programming more aligned with scholarly and student interests, including talks, panels, seminars, and game-development resources developed in partnership with campus organizations (Bishoff et al., 2015; Farrell et al., 2017).

Cho et al. (2023) provide a related example from public libraries. Their misinformation escape room was not designed simply to entertain patrons or increase attendance. Its purpose was to help participants develop epistemic resilience in relation to misinformation. Rather than focusing only on source-evaluation skills, the game placed participants in a situation where they encountered misleading headlines, manipulated charts, deepfakes, bots, and social media sharing as part of a larger narrative experience. This clarity of purpose shaped the entire design. The escape room format was selected because it could immerse participants in uncertainty, allow them to make mistakes safely, and provide a shared experience for later discussion.

Together, these studies suggest a broad design principle: libraries should first ask what kind of learning, participation, or community outcome the service is meant to support. Only then should they choose the game format, audience, facilitation model, collection strategy, and assessment approach.

## Partner with other organizations

Several studies suggest that game-based library programming is more effective when developed with partners rather than delivered in isolation. Partnerships can help libraries understand audience needs, promote events, recruit participants, select games, provide technical expertise, and connect programs to existing communities of practice.

In academic settings, partnerships with student organizations are especially important. Bishoff et al. (2015) describe how collaboration with student groups helped the University of Minnesota develop more relevant programming after initial recreational gaming events saw limited turnout. Vanden Elzen and Roush (2013) and Werner (2013) similarly emphasize the importance of working with students and campus groups to understand local gaming culture, choose appropriate games, and schedule events around the academic calendar. In public libraries, partnerships may include local game stores, schools, youth organizations, fandom communities, museums, community groups, or other libraries.

Partnerships can also extend the library’s capacity. Bremer (2017), for example, describes a cross-library event involving both an academic and a public library. Cho et al. (2023) used collaboration among researchers, librarians, students, and an escape room developer to design and test the misinformation escape room. Such examples suggest that GBLS often works best as networked programming. Libraries provide trust, access, space, and facilitation, while partners may contribute subject expertise, design knowledge, community connections, or promotional reach.

## Selecting Games

Games, like any other media, are as diverse as their audiences. A well rounded collection will include games of different types (analog and digital, console and computer) as well as genres (action, puzzle, RPG, “serious games”). Games should be selected for inclusivity by selecting a diversity of genres and topics [(Farmer, 2011\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?7fvNdQ) Curators should also consider ESRB age ratings (E, T, M) and display information for parents. For consoles, support multiple platforms (Wii, Xbox, PlayStation, Meta). Newer consoles attract audiences who want to try them [(Bishoff et al., 2015\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?LhDsDI), but older consoles have larger catalogs of content. Various authors mentioned partnering with local game stores and gaming clubs to help select titles, but there are also industry awards that can help identify particularly important titles, such as the Games For Change, Peabody and GEE\! awards. [(Werner, 2013\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?HmtJRw) mentions that the collection development policies should be made clear to address potential parental concerns.  

## Match the Game Form to the Goal and Audience

The reviewed literature also suggests that modality matters. Board games, video games, escape rooms, tabletop roleplaying games, esports competitions, virtual reality experiences, circulating collections, and game-development resources each organize participation differently. They differ in cost, accessibility, technical requirements, social dynamics, staff expertise, and learning affordances. Game selection should therefore be treated as a design decision rather than a matter of preference or popularity.

Escape rooms are particularly well suited to collaborative problem-solving, puzzle-based inquiry, time-limited engagement, and structured reflection. In Cho et al. (2023), the escape room format worked because misinformation often involves partial evidence, emotional pressure, misleading patterns, and the need to interpret uncertain information with others. Breakout boxes and similar escape-room formats have also been used in school library contexts to support inquiry, teamwork, communication, and perseverance (Lewallen, 2019).

Tabletop roleplaying games, by contrast, may be especially useful for sustained group belonging, role-taking, identity play, and collaborative storytelling. Nelson and Matthews (2025) found that the library’s role in Dungeons & Dragons programming was not simply to provide materials, but to provide stability, regular meeting times, a welcoming environment, and access to dungeonmasters or other facilitators. Esports and tournaments may be useful for attracting new audiences and increasing visibility, but several authors suggest balancing competitive events with casual, low-barrier opportunities for social play so that programming does not only serve already confident or competitive players (Vanden Elzen & Roush, 2013; Werner, 2013).

This principle is especially important for equity. Games are diverse media, and audiences encounter them with different levels of comfort, familiarity, and prior experience. A well-designed service should consider whether the chosen format invites participation from the intended audience or unintentionally privileges patrons who already possess gaming knowledge, technical access, or social confidence.

## Select and Curate Games Intentionally 

Game selection is a core part of program design. Games, like books, films, music, and other media, vary widely by genre, theme, difficulty, audience, cultural context, accessibility, and representational politics. A well-rounded game collection or program should include multiple forms of play, including analog and digital games, console and computer games, cooperative and competitive games, short and long-form games, and games across genres such as action, puzzle, strategy, roleplaying, party games, serious games, and simulation.

Several authors recommend selecting games with inclusivity in mind. Farmer (2011), for example, argues that libraries should attend to gendered participation patterns and curate diverse game genres and topics rather than assuming that a single type of game will appeal broadly. Curators should also consider age ratings, parent information, accessibility, cost, platform availability, and the social norms associated with different play communities. Console selection involves similar tradeoffs. Newer systems may attract patrons who want to experiment with current technology, while older systems often have larger and more affordable catalogs.

Curation also requires attention to relationships among games, since patrons may approach a collection through franchises, platform versions, expansions, spin-offs, adaptations, or related media rather than through isolated titles.

Curation can also connect games to other library materials and programs. Displays that link popular games to related books, films, nonfiction, comics, music, or historical materials can position games within broader media ecosystems. For example, a science fiction game might be paired with novels, films, or nonfiction about space exploration, artificial intelligence, or political conflict. For example, display signage such as, “If you like Halo, try these sci-fi novels and films.” Documentary screenings, game-related talks, local game showcases, design workshops, and critical discussion groups can further support games as cultural objects rather than isolated entertainment products.

Said cataloged items’ “aboutness” then is organized/classified in reference to the “aboutness” of other items in the library; that’s the miracle of how library collections are made and organized for ease of use and findability. Technically, then, another library could “copy catalog” that newly created entry in their own database – if they share the same ILS.  
  5.) Displaying, mending, and weeding: this is the “maintenance” portion of collection development. Choosing how, when, and where to display materials obviously has a profound impact on browse-ability and circulation. Librarians have to consider “analysis paralysis”; too many options and folks don’t even know what to look for. The recommendation is usually to fill up a shelf no more than 60% or so in order to curate the browse-ability of a collection.

  Beyond that, of course – books and DVDs and games and such get damaged; if they can be repaired, specialized library training, tools, and resources can be used to “mend” and fix items so they can continue to circulate. Beyond THAT, there is weeding (one of the most important considerations for a librarian to keep in mind). It’s SO important to regularly remove items from ALL collections in a library to remove obsolete information, remove gross or totally dated materials, thin out a collection so it doesn’t become bloated, run reports to check for popularity / circulation strength and remove whatever doesn’t leave the shelves, etc.

  I’ve simplified things quite a bit, but those 5 components are generally recognized as the key steps of “Collection Development” (as mentioned, sometimes separated to be tasks for different staff members in a large library).

  For scale and scope, it’s important to imagine how these steps would be applied library-wide; books vs. DVDs vs. board games vs. video games vs. TTRPG books vs. graphic novels vs. audiobooks vs… well, you get the picture. Then you can expand that even further to account for different departments; Childrens, Adults, Teens, etc. Collection development is a process that is somewhat unique for different materials and different demographics.

Things get even more chaotic with folks that do collection development in libraries but have had no real formal training whatsoever.

  As for Reader’s Advisory – this is also often called “Ready Reference”. If Collection Development is how the sausage is made behind closed doors at the library, then Reader’s Advisory is bringing that metaphorical sausage to interested folks that visit the library space. This metaphor is stretched thin, lol, but whatever.

Essentially, a series of questions to the patron to tease out information about what they are interested in or looking for. Copeland et al. (2013) offer a useful set of criteria for selecting tabletop games for school-library collections and classroom use. First, the game should provide an engaging play experience; it must work as a game before it can work as an educational resource. Second, the game should connect to the curriculum through its mechanics, theme, or strategies for success. Third, time must be considered, since classroom periods and after-school clubs impose practical limits on what can be played. Finally, librarians should consider return on investment by weighing the game’s potential learning impact against the time, effort, and energy needed to implement it. These criteria are valuable because they avoid reducing game selection to either popularity or curricular relevance alone. Effective educational game selection requires both play value and instructional fit.

## Fiction is a Gateway into Emotionally Charged Topics

One of the distinctive affordances of games is their ability to create fictional or semi-fictional situations in which difficult topics can be explored indirectly. This is especially valuable for libraries because many issues they address such as misinformation, health, identity, privacy, politics, surveillance, and social conflict. All of these can quickly become polarizing when approached through direct instruction.

Cho et al. (2023) demonstrate this principle clearly. Their escape room avoided politically charged terms such as “fake news” and instead used a fictional scenario about a supplement company and a public water supply. This fictional framing did not avoid the problem of misinformation. Rather, it created enough distance for participants to experience its dynamics without immediately reducing the activity to partisan debate. The narrative structure allowed players to feel suspicion, make assumptions, encounter manipulated evidence, and eventually recognize that they had shared a deepfake. Fiction functioned as a protective frame for reflection.

This is a valuable lesson for game-based library services more broadly. Play can make difficult subjects discussable by moving participants into a shared imagined situation. The game world does not replace real-world issues, but it can create a safer and more open route back to them. In library programming, this makes games especially useful for topics where direct instruction may feel moralizing, overly didactic, or politically risky.

## Design for Collaboration and Shared Experience

Many successful game-based library programs are social experiences. They give patrons a reason to gather, talk, solve problems, negotiate rules, teach one another, and remain in shared public space. Collaboration is not merely an enjoyable feature of these programs; it is often central to their educational and civic value.

In Cho et al. (2023), the collaborative structure of the escape room mattered because participants worked together to solve puzzles and then reflected together during the debrief. This transformed misinformation education from an individual checklist activity into a shared practice of interpretation. Participants did not simply receive information from an expert. They compared observations, made decisions, experienced error together, and discussed the consequences of those decisions. The shared gameplay experience then provided common ground for the debrief conversation.

This principle also appears in studies of gaming programs designed to build community. Bell and Trueman (2014), Vanden Elzen and Roush (2013), Werner (2013), Bremer (2017), and Nelson and Matthews (2025) all point in different ways to the value of games as social infrastructure. In a commercial gaming venue, social play may be primarily entertainment. In a library, social play can also become a form of public learning. The library provides the setting, norms, facilitation, and institutional trust that allow playful interaction to become community-building, peer learning, and civic practice.

## Treat Facilitation as Part of the Game System

A recurring lesson across game-based library services is that facilitation should not be treated as an afterthought. Librarians do not simply provide rooms, equipment, or access to games. They often scaffold participation, establish norms, welcome newcomers, troubleshoot technical problems, mediate conflict, connect programs to collections, and help patrons interpret the experience.

Cho et al. (2023) make this point especially strongly. The researchers identify the librarian-led debrief as a central part of the learning experience. Librarians hosted the game, guided post-game discussion, asked follow-up questions, drew quieter participants into the conversation, and helped connect the fictional scenario to real-world misinformation. Their role mattered because libraries are trusted institutions and librarians bring both information expertise and facilitation skill to public learning settings.

Nelson and Matthews (2025) make a similar point in relation to Dungeons & Dragons programs. While roleplaying games themselves may require relatively few material resources, the library’s major contribution was providing stability, a regular time and place, access to facilitation, and a welcoming environment that patrons might not find in game stores or private groups. This suggests that in many GBLS contexts, the library’s most important resource is not the game but the facilitated social infrastructure around it.

Copeland et al. (2013) further show that tabletop gaming depends heavily on facilitation. In curriculum-integrated role-playing games, the librarian or teacher may function as a game master, keeping the game on track while also adapting events in response to student creativity. In board game clubs, facilitation includes teaching rules, managing conflict, helping players handle winning and losing, maintaining rule consistency, and supporting peer teaching as experienced students explain games to newcomers. These examples reinforce the broader point that library gaming is not simply a matter of providing games. The facilitator helps transform play into a stable, welcoming, and educationally meaningful experience.

## Build in Reflection and Transfer

Games produce experience, but experience does not automatically become learning. For many educational and civic library programs, reflection is the mechanism that helps participants connect what happened in the game to broader concepts, personal experiences, collections, services, and future behavior.

Cho et al. (2023) show that the post-game debrief was essential to this process. During gameplay, participants were focused on solving puzzles and completing the escape room. The debrief created time for them to discuss how they had been misled, what assumptions they had made, how deepfakes and manipulated data worked, and how misinformation circulates through social media. This reflective structure helped move the activity from entertainment to epistemic resilience. Participants were not only learning about misinformation techniques; they were reflecting on how people become vulnerable to misinformation, how sharing occurs, and how difficult it can be to undo misinformation once it has circulated.

This principle applies broadly. Library game programs should consider when and how participants will reflect on what they experienced. Reflection may take the form of a facilitated debrief, a discussion group, a display connecting the game to related materials, a short writing activity, classroom integration, or a follow-up program. The key point is that transfer should be designed intentionally. If the program has an educational, social, or civic purpose, participants need some structure for connecting play to that larger purpose.

## Plan for Cataloging , Discovery, and Circulation

When game-based library services involve collections, discoverability and circulation become central design concerns. The literature shows that libraries have developed pragmatic local approaches, but cataloging games remains difficult. Existing library systems were primarily designed around books, audiovisual media, and relatively stable information objects. Games often challenge these systems because they may include multiple components, platforms, versions, expansions, rulebooks, peripherals, digital accounts, or embodied forms of play.

For tabletop games, some libraries use detailed physical inventories, player counts, playtime, recommended age, and local genre terms to improve access. Robson et al. (2019) show that cataloging improvements alone are not enough; discovery depends on whether game-relevant metadata is exposed through user-facing search and filtering systems. Slobuski, Robson, and Bentley (2017) found significant variation in tabletop game collection practices, including whether libraries circulate games, create catalog records, accept donations, or rely on local processing systems.

Circulation also requires practical decisions. Libraries may need to choose between counting every game piece and using faster but less precise approaches such as weighing boxes. Evidence from Slobuski et al. (2017) suggests that loss and damage may be less severe than many libraries fear, but local procedures are still needed. For console lending, Buller (2017) describes a model in which complete gaming systems were circulated in durable cases with screens, cables, controllers, checklists, and serial-number tracking. Such examples show that circulation is not simply a logistical matter. It shapes whether patrons can access games equitably, whether collections remain usable, and whether services can be sustained over time.

## Prepare Staff and Build Institutional Capacity

Game-based library services often require staff preparation. Librarians may need familiarity with gaming culture, game genres, age ratings, platforms, facilitation strategies, accessibility considerations, and common technical problems. Staff do not need to be expert players in every genre, but they do need enough confidence to support patrons, explain the purpose of programs, troubleshoot participation, and connect games to library goals.

Staff preparation can take many forms: internal play sessions, LibGuides, game selection guides, shared program scripts, debrief guides, collection development policies, troubleshooting documents, or partnerships with experienced players and community groups. Cho et al. (2023) trained librarians to host the escape room and facilitate the post-game discussion, demonstrating that staff preparation was central to the program’s effectiveness. Similarly, Dungeons & Dragons programs may depend on the library’s ability to identify or support dungeonmasters, while esports or console events may require staff to manage hardware, registration, rules, and audience expectations.

Institutional capacity also matters. Ambitious game designs may align well with library goals but still fail if they require more labor, technology, or ongoing support than the library can provide. Watson et al. (2024), for example, illustrate how an immersive tabletop roleplaying game concept for library orientation generated interest but required significant development labor and was ultimately scaled back. The lesson is not that ambitious game design should be avoided, but that scope, staffing, and sustainability must be considered from the beginning.

Abrams and Van Eck (2021) further clarify why staff preparation and institutional capacity are central to videogame programming. In their national survey of U.S. librarians, they distinguish between first-order barriers, such as funding, equipment, space, training, and administrative support, and second-order barriers, such as librarian confidence, beliefs about videogames, and assumptions about teen behavior. This distinction is useful because different barriers require different interventions. A library may need money for consoles and screens, but it may also need professional development that helps staff understand videogame play as a legitimate literacy practice. Likewise, concerns about noise, supervision, or adolescent behavior may reflect real management challenges, but they may also reveal deeper uncertainty about whether teen gaming belongs in the library at all. For this reason, sustainable GBLS requires both material support and professional culture change.

## Design for Accessibility, Adaptation, and Sustainability

Game-based library services should be designed for adaptation, not only for ideal implementation. Libraries differ widely in staffing, budgets, spaces, technology, patron demographics, language needs, and community priorities. A program that works in a large urban academic library may not transfer directly to a small rural public library, a school library, or a branch with limited staffing.

Cho et al. (2023) report several practical implementation issues. Even though the online escape room used familiar tools such as Zoom, Teams, and web browsers, librarians still encountered technical problems with microphones, cameras, screen sharing, and participant no-shows. Librarians also expressed interest in versions of the game adapted for different age groups, communities, languages, and in-person settings. In response, the project developed additional resources, including a debrief kit and an in-person version that libraries could print and assemble with ordinary office supplies.

This is a useful model for sustainable GBLS design. Programs are more likely to travel across contexts when they include clear instructions, low-cost materials, adaptable facilitation guides, accessibility considerations, and options for different group sizes or timeframes. Sustainability should also include maintenance: keeping equipment functional, updating digital games or platforms, replacing missing components, preserving staff knowledge, and avoiding programs that depend entirely on one enthusiastic individual.

## Evaluate Outcomes Beyond Attendance and Enjoyment

Many game-based library programs are evaluated primarily through attendance, satisfaction, and anecdotal engagement. These measures are useful, but they are not sufficient when games are being used to support learning, inclusion, civic reasoning, or community development. Evaluation should match the purpose of the program.

Cho et al. (2023) provide a stronger model by examining participant reflection, awareness of misinformation techniques, possible shifts in attitudes, social media behaviors, librarian observations, and design implications. The study remains exploratory, but it demonstrates how evaluation can move beyond whether patrons liked the activity. If the goal is epistemic resilience, then evaluation should ask whether participants practiced recognizing uncertainty, reflected on their own vulnerability, discussed strategies with others, or reconsidered their future information behavior.

For GBLS research more broadly, this is an important area for further development. Libraries need practical, low-burden ways to assess not only participation, but also learning, belonging, access, reflection, and longer-term impact. Future studies could compare facilitated and self-guided game experiences, online and in-person formats, one-time and recurring programs, or different approaches to debriefing. Such work would help move the field from program description toward transferable knowledge about what kinds of game-based library services work, for whom, under what conditions, and why.

### Training and Motivation for Staff

Provide librarians with training sessions or web resources on gaming culture and media literacy. Encourage staff to play games together to build familiarity and enthusiasm. Develop library guides (LibGuides) featuring recommended games, ratings, and related materials.

### Developing Events

In the higher educational setting, partnerships and co-planning with student audiences is essential [(Vanden Elzen & Roush, 2013; Werner, 2013\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?W5ZxD5). Doing so will help understand the campus culture and work around the academic, such as avoiding high stress dates for students. They also suggest that competitive events should be balanced with low-barrier, casual play events to ensure inclusivity. 

While roleplaying games themselves require very few resources, [(Nelson & Matthews, 2025\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?TCkZH3) in a study of programs at two midwestern libraries found that the a primary role of the library was to provide stability (regular time/place), provide or facilitate the dungeonmaster, and facilitate a welcoming environment that many players do not experience in game stores or private groups. 

## Summary

Taken together, these lessons suggest that game-based library services are strongest when games are designed, selected, cataloged, circulated, and facilitated as purposeful public learning experiences. The best examples do not treat games as entertainment add-ons or simple engagement tools. Instead, they use games to create structured opportunities for access, exploration, collaboration, reflection, and community participation. In this sense, the central design question is not whether games belong in libraries, but how libraries can use games in ways that advance their educational, cultural, and civic missions.

# Limitations and Future Work

Very few of the reviewed programs explicitly mention adult audiences outside of college students. Some exceptions are [(Nelson & Matthews, 2025\)](https://www.zotero.org/google-docs/?ICCFyV) for DnD.

Very few metastudies exist to synthesize across individual contexts and projects into generalizable knowledge. This paper and communities such as GGRT may be a first step.

So many of the papers and projects are written by practitioners, not researchers or theorists. We need to keep developing the theoretical and empirical intersections of the role of libraries and the affordances of interactive media. For example, there may be a role for the discussion of the changing role of “public” resources and media in American society. 

Empirically What kinds of programming best support videogame literacies: scheduled play, open play, tournaments, clubs, circulation, or hybrid models?

How do librarian beliefs about teens, noise, play, and literacy shape whether videogame programming is supported or resisted?

What professional development models help librarians move from informal experimentation to sustainable practice?

How do videogame programs affect youth who lack access to consoles, gaming PCs, broadband, or game-literate peer communities at home?

Future GBLS research should examine how library game programs connect with the informal literacies patrons already practice outside institutional settings. Coonan (2019), drawing on work in videogaming and transmedia literacy, emphasizes that game players develop practices of problem-solving, identity performance, navigation, networking, and negotiation through everyday play.

How do tabletop game clubs support belonging among students who do not participate in traditional extracurricular activities?

What facilitation practices help students learn to negotiate rules, manage conflict, and teach games to peers?

How can school libraries evaluate the curricular impact of tabletop games without reducing them to worksheets or content-delivery tools?

What selection criteria best balance authentic play value with curriculum alignment?

How do tabletop game-design projects function as assessment, especially for students who struggle with traditional forms of classroom expression?
