# Foundations: Games, Libraries, and Public Purpose

## Define Games-Based Library Services as a Service Ecology

Use **Games-Based Library Services (GBLS)** consistently as the umbrella term. GBLS is broader than game programming or “gaming”: it includes collections, circulation, in-library access, advisory, displays, instruction, orientation, outreach, community events, game creation, research support, preservation, staff preparation, and assessment. Present these activities as an interconnected service ecology shaped by relationships among collections, programs, spaces, staff expertise, technology, policies, partners, and patrons.

## Treat Games as Core Cultural Media

Write from the premise that games are a major form of contemporary culture and belong within the cultural mandate of libraries. Do not frame games as an extracurricular reward, novelty, concession to youth interests, or optional service added after “serious” library work. Libraries collect, interpret, preserve, and provide access to consequential cultural forms; games merit the same professional attention afforded to books, film, music, and other media.

## Position Libraries as Public Knowledge and Participation Infrastructure

Libraries are among the remaining free, locally embedded institutions connecting communities with organized knowledge, cultural participation, technology, and human guidance. Explore how GBLS can strengthen this role amid misinformation, fragmented media environments, declining civic trust, and unequal access to learning. Games are not a cure for an “emergency of epistemology,” but well-designed GBLS can support inquiry, interpretation, collaboration, experimentation, and reflection.

## Use History as Precedent Rather Than Outcome Evidence

Use historical sources to show that games, toys, puzzles, contests, role-play, and playful environments have long appeared in library service. This history counters the idea that GBLS is a recent technological fad and reveals recurring tensions between experimentation and professional respectability. Do not use historical accounts as proof of contemporary learning, social, or service outcomes.

## Preserve Distinctions Among Games, Gamification, and Play

Do not collapse full games, gamified services, and broader playful activity into a single category. Games are coherent systems of rules, choices, goals, feedback, and consequences. Gamification applies selected game elements to nongame activity. Play may involve exploration, imagination, improvisation, or social interaction without a complete game system. These forms serve different purposes and require different evaluation strategies.

## Organize Around Library Purposes and Format Affordances

Prioritize service goals such as access, discovery, learning, belonging, cultural stewardship, research, creative production, and public participation. Formats should illustrate how libraries pursue those goals rather than dictate the review’s intellectual structure. Avoid hierarchies in which digital games are presumed innovative or analog games inherently social and accessible. Compare the distinct affordances, barriers, audiences, resource demands, and local fit of analog, digital, and hybrid forms.

# Learning, Reflexive Complexity, and Adaptive Participation

## Frame the Problem as a Gap Between Knowledge and Coordinated Action

The contemporary crisis is not simply a shortage of information or predictive capacity. Institutions can produce sophisticated evidence and explanations without reliably producing trust, coordination, or adaptive collective action. Many systems are reflexive: people react to predictions, metrics, media, policies, and representations in ways that alter the systems being described. Use this framing to explain why people need opportunities to practice judgment and coordination, while avoiding the implication that every library game addresses reflexive complexity.

## Treat Games as Informal Learning and Rehearsal Environments

Libraries and games both create voluntary, exploratory spaces for learning outside formal classrooms. Well-designed games can place participants inside worlds of action and consequence where they test ideas, inhabit roles, encounter resistance, interpret feedback, revise assumptions, and experience unintended effects. Frame this as rehearsal rather than mastery. Libraries add facilitation, public access, trustworthy context, collections, and pathways into further learning.

## Center Adaptive Judgment Rather Than Information Recall

For complex systems, knowing facts is necessary but insufficient. Participants must learn when evidence applies, how local actions interact with larger structures, whose expertise matters, what tradeoffs are acceptable, and when strategies should change. GBLS can contribute when it supports cycles of action, feedback, interpretation, and adaptation. Do not reduce educational value to recall, completion, correct answers, or engagement alone.

## Treat Affect as Part of Reasoning

Emotion shapes attention, memory, judgment, motivation, avoidance, and willingness to remain engaged with difficulty. Games can give fear, uncertainty, hope, conflict, and failure a structure that makes difficult topics playable enough to think with. Narrative, visual design, sound, role, pacing, humor, and atmosphere are not merely decorative; they influence what participants notice, value, and remain willing to confront.

## Use Shared Play as a Foundation for Deliberation

Shared game experiences can provide a common but nonidentical basis for discussion. Participants may occupy different roles, make different decisions, or witness different outcomes within the same designed system. Libraries can use facilitated reflection to compare interpretations, surface assumptions, connect experience to evidence, and move from individual play toward collective inquiry. Games should open deliberation rather than dictate a single approved conclusion.

## Distinguish Adjacent Benefits from Designed Outcomes

Some benefits emerge from ordinary participation in commercially successful games even when designers did not intend them, including informal learning, identity work, community formation, and systems reasoning. Other games are explicitly designed to produce educational, civic, health, or social outcomes. Preserve this distinction: research on adjacent benefits explains why existing games matter, while public-purpose design asks how games can be deliberately created and supported to advance stated goals.

# Access, Discovery, Cultural Stewardship, and Public Media

## Make Equitable and Universal Access a Design Constraint

Frame GBLS as part of libraries’ work to reduce unequal access to media, technology, expertise, safe public space, and cultural participation. Access includes more than owning or lending an object; patrons may need hardware, broadband, accounts, accessible interfaces, staff guidance, welcoming groups, culturally relevant materials, time, and space. Distribution should be designed around where people are and the barriers they face, not treated as a neutral step after content creation.

## Treat Discovery and Advisory as Public Responses to Media Abundance

The contemporary challenge is less scarcity than overwhelming abundance, uneven visibility, and commercially shaped recommendation. Cataloging, metadata, curation, and games advisory are public-facing services that help people find trustworthy, relevant, culturally significant, and locally appropriate experiences. Description should represent mechanics, narrative, mood, duration, player count, difficulty, social mode, accessibility, platform, version, expansions, and technical dependencies.

## Recognize Games as Cultural Heritage and Research Objects

Games are not stable, self-contained artifacts. Their meaning and playability may depend on hardware, software versions, patches, servers, interfaces, documentation, development materials, communities, events, and related media. Libraries and archives should be framed as stewards of these relationships rather than repositories for released products alone. Preservation, intellectual property, licensing, platform dependence, and disappearing physical distribution are urgent GBLS concerns.

## Imagine a Public Media Infrastructure for Games

Allow the review to gesture toward libraries as nodes in a distributed public-media infrastructure supporting access, discovery, facilitation, public-interest creation, preservation, research, and community participation. Define Public Media by public purpose, accountability, broad access, and meaningful participation rather than legacy broadcast affiliation. Treat this as an aspirational authorial proposition and future direction, not an established conclusion of the GBLS evidence base.

## Distinguish Commercial Openness from Public Access

Commercial platforms may permit anyone to publish while making sustainable visibility dependent on branding, monetization, algorithmic compatibility, engagement metrics, and conformity to platform norms. Technical permission to participate is not equitable capacity to persist, reach audiences, or serve public purposes. Position libraries as potential counter-institutions supporting work and communities underserved by market incentives.

## Build Interactive Infrastructure Rather Than Borrowing Broadcast Models

Games require production and service systems unlike those for linear media. Prototyping, playtesting, quality assurance, accessibility testing, software maintenance, licensing, server operations, community management, updates, preservation, and facilitation may continue long after release. Any public infrastructure for games must assume these ongoing interactive responsibilities.

## Join Artistic Ambition, Research, and Public Purpose

Reject the idea that educational or public-interest games should be aesthetically thin or narrowly didactic. Public-purpose interactive media should draw on artists, writers, musicians, performers, designers, programmers, researchers, educators, librarians, and communities. Build formative research, playtesting, accessibility review, and summative evaluation into production so that evidence informs design and libraries serve as test sites, facilitators, partners, and evidence-generating institutions.

# Community-Centered Design, Facilitation, and Sustainable Practice

## Treat Belonging and Human Flourishing as Primary Outcomes

Do not describe social connection as a pleasant secondary effect. In many GBLS programs, community is the service. Games provide structured reasons to enter libraries, remain in shared space, meet peers, interact with supportive adults, and develop recurring relationships. Connect wellness to respite, joy, identity exploration, creative expression, and membership rather than relying solely on a clinical model. Libraries are not treatment providers, and therapeutic claims require appropriate evidence.

## Design for Inclusion and Cultural Specificity

An open door does not guarantee an inclusive service. Ask who can find the program, enter the space, understand the rules, use the equipment, see themselves represented, and participate without gatekeeping or harassment. Attend to disability, language, age, race, gender, sexuality, class, transportation, rurality, technology access, and prior experience. Do not flatten culturally situated games into generic mechanics; support community-authored games through partnership, contextualization, appropriate description, and playable access.

## Treat Facilitation and Professional Preparation as Infrastructure

Facilitators teach rules, welcome beginners, manage conflict, adapt difficulty, establish norms, connect play to learning, protect participants from exclusion, and lead reflection. GBLS cannot mature if workers must rely on personal hobby knowledge or isolated champions. LIS education, continuing education, reusable resources, communities of practice, shared standards, and institutional support should address selection, advisory, accessibility, facilitation, technology, policy, assessment, and cultural interpretation.

## Begin with Community Need and Local Context

Preserve Baker’s central tension: libraries risk irrelevance if they ignore important media practices, but they also risk wasting scarce resources if they build services without understanding demand. Planning should begin with patrons, demographics, strategic priorities, service gaps, and capacity. Do not assume models transfer unchanged among urban, suburban, rural, tribal, school, academic, public, and special libraries. Treat local variation as substantive evidence rather than noise.

## Treat Partnerships as Capacity and Leadership Development

Partnerships with patrons, students, educators, cultural organizations, game creators, hobby groups, researchers, social-service organizations, and other libraries can contribute expertise, equipment, audiences, legitimacy, and leadership pathways. Present community members as co-designers and facilitators rather than only recipients. Partnerships should extend library capacity without leaving essential services dependent on a single volunteer or enthusiast.

## Treat Platforms and Operations as Service Design

Budgets, staffing, schedules, noise, privacy, conduct, licensing, accounts, maintenance, sanitation, storage, transportation, marketing, and leadership support determine access and continuity. Apply the same scrutiny to programs inside commercial game worlds: ask whether the platform fits the mission, who controls it, whether intended patrons are present, what labor it requires, and whether a partnership or advocacy organization is better positioned to operate at scale.

# Evidence, Infrastructure, and the Research Agenda

## Align Claims and Assessment with Intended Outcomes

Attendance, enjoyment, motivation, knowledge, retention, literacy, belonging, confidence, behavior, transfer, and well-being are different outcomes. Do not use one as evidence for another. Assessment should follow service purpose: collections may require discovery and usage measures; instruction should assess targeted knowledge or practice; community programs should examine belonging and continued participation; equity-oriented work should ask who participates, who does not, and why.

## Weight Evidence Without Discarding Practitioner Knowledge

Give experiments, surveys, interviews, observations, mixed-methods studies, systematic reviews, and meta-analyses the greatest weight for outcome claims. Use practitioner reflections, handbooks, columns, reports, webinars, and collaborator expertise to identify design principles, operational knowledge, emerging concerns, and research questions. Synthesize recurring professional knowledge without converting repetition into causal proof.

## Use Vignettes to Demonstrate Field-Level Claims

Specific games or programs may appear as brief vignettes when they make an argument about cultural interpretation, design, access, or service concrete. Do not allow examples to become recommended-title lists, article-by-article summaries, or substitutes for synthesis. Each vignette should establish a larger point and return quickly to the field-level analysis.

## Treat Infrastructure as the Link Between Exemplars and Public Scale

Promising games and pilots often disappear when grants end, teams dissolve, platforms change, or no institution assumes responsibility. The challenge is not only to make better games but to build systems for discovering, adopting, supporting, updating, facilitating, evaluating, and preserving them. Libraries are well positioned to contribute, but doing so requires resources, standards, partnerships, and durable commitments.

## Keep the Needs-Assessment Agenda Visible While Writing with Disciplined Ambition

The field still needs paired staff and patron evidence about which GBLS formats libraries offer, how they fund and promote them, which barriers staff encounter, how prepared staff feel, what patrons know and use, what communities want, and how patterns vary by demographics and context. Baker’s proposal is a research-agenda artifact rather than outcome evidence. Write confidently that games deserve serious attention while remaining candid about descriptive evidence, unmeasured claims, and the limits of games as a remedy.
